<h1 class="heading__title">Product Page SEO: 17 eCommerce Best Practices</h1>
<div class="heading__params">
<span aria-hidden="true" class="heading__label">
										Last updated:
									</span>
<span aria-label="The date of last update" class="heading__caption">

<div class="post-detail__text typo">
<section aria-label="Product Page SEO in detail" class="highlighted-box highlighted-box--with-background typo-block"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">
<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/product-page-seo--reversed.svg)"></i></span>Product Page SEO in detail
	</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>If your product pages don't play a central role in your SEO strategy, you're missing out.</p>
<p>In this article you’ll learn how to leverage your product pages to boost your SEO performance— and your revenue.</p>
</div>
</section>

<p>eCommerce spending is higher than ever—and everyone’s looking to profit from that. The competition is ruthless. If you slip up, your competitors will happily take your coveted spot in Google’s search results. And if you don’t grab SEO opportunities, your competition will.</p>
<p>There’s also a big misconception in eCommerce: lots of folks think visitors come in through the homepage or a category page and then make their way down to a product page. This assumption is wrong, and it’s a missed opportunity.</p>
<p>Product pages often get a lot of organic traffic from visitors who already have their credit card handy. If your product pages aren’t playing a central role in your SEO strategy, you’re missing out.</p>
<p>Keep reading, and you’ll learn how to leverage your product pages to boost your SEO performance—and your revenue.</p>
<h2 id="why-optimizing-product-pages-is-tricky">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#why-optimizing-product-pages-is-tricky" tabindex="-1"></a>Why optimizing product pages is tricky</h2>
<p>In SEO you can’t just look at product pages in isolation, because search engines don’t view your store that way either. Pages affect one another (positively and negatively), so you need to look beyond product pages. Search engines evaluate your entire store; the whole is greater than the sum of its parts.</p>
<p>Optimizing product pages requires you to focus on these pages without losing sight of the bigger picture—how your product pages work together—or the rest of your site.</p>
<p>When it comes to the bigger picture, think for example about dealing with:</p>
<ul>
<li>Category pages and product pages competing for the same queries</li>
<li>Very similar product variants</li>
<li>Products available in multiple categories</li>
<li>Discontinued or expired products</li>
</ul>
<p>Now this may feel overwhelming, but don’t worry—we’ll explain in detail how to tackle these issues further down in the article.</p>

<h2 id="applying-the-pareto-principle-and-a-growth-mindset">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#applying-the-pareto-principle-and-a-growth-mindset" tabindex="-1"></a>Applying the Pareto Principle and a growth mindset</h2>
<p>In eCommerce SEO you’re likely working on larger sites. And that’s different from working on smaller sites. For instance on a large site you won’t be able to tackle every issue. But the beauty is, you don’t have to. As long as you focus on the product categories and products that drive most revenue and have the best margins, you’re good.</p>
<p>You’re likely to find that 20% of your categories are driving 80% of the revenue, so focus on those. Then once you find you’ve got a handle on those, move on to the next categories that are worth your time.</p>
<p>When it comes to your roadmap with improvements and changes you want to get done, apply a growth mindset that forces you to focus on the things that have the most impact. For example improving the way product names are generated, the way product detail templates are structured, and how you can leverage Product Schema.</p>
<h2 id="how-to-optimize-product-pages">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-to-optimize-product-pages" tabindex="-1"></a>How to optimize product pages</h2>
<p>Alright, let’s get our hands dirty and start going through all of the best practices to optimize your product pages:</p>
<h3 id="naming">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#naming" tabindex="-1"></a>1. Align product names with your keyword strategy</h3>
<p>Aligning product names with the search queries you’re targeting goes a long way. Include the brand name, product name, model number, and useful product attributes such as color, size, and even the SKU if that makes sense.</p>
<p>Let’s say you run an eCommerce store called “Into The Wild” that sells backpacks, and you want to rank for <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">osprey kyte 46 pack women</code>. For starters you’ll need to include those keywords in your product name, meta information, and URL. We’ll cover the meta information and URL in detail below, but let’s first focus on the product name.</p>
<p>REI does a good job at picking their product names:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-rei-example@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-rei-example@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-rei-example@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-rei-example@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-rei-example@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-rei-example@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-rei-example@1x.png"/><img alt="Screenshot from REI showing a good product name" class="image" height="310" src="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-rei-example@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-rei-example@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-rei-example@2x.png 2x" title="Screenshot from REI showing a good product name" width="600"/></picture></div>

<h3 id="url">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#url" tabindex="-1"></a>2. Descriptive and consistent product URLs</h3>
<p>A good URL structure helps both visitors and search engines to understand your site structure. And you’re likely to get more clicks on your snippet in Google when the visitor sees a URL that reflects their search query.</p>
<p>A good URL structure is descriptive, easy to read, consistent, brief, and lowercase.</p>
<p>When it comes to product pages, the last part of the URL—the so-called <a href="/academy/urls/faq/slug/">URL slug</a>—is relevant.</p>
<p>In case of the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Osprey Kyte 46 pack Women’s</code> page, a great URL slug would be <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">osprey-kyte-46-pack-womens</code>, making the full URL for example <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/backpacks/hiking/osprey-kyte-46-pack-womens/</code>.</p>

<h3 id="meta-information">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#meta-information" tabindex="-1"></a>3. Meta information: title tag and meta description</h3>
<p>When we talk about “meta information” within SEO, we’re referring to both the title tag and the meta description. Both play an essential role in how well your product page ranks and how it’s presented to searchers.</p>
<p>The illustration below highlights the role of the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Title</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Description</code> in what your snippet will look like:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-snippet-illustration@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-snippet-illustration@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-snippet-illustration@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-snippet-illustration@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-snippet-illustration@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-snippet-illustration@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-snippet-illustration@1x.png"/><img alt="Screenshot of a search engine result snippet from Google, with the title, URL and description highlighted." class="image" height="215" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-snippet-illustration@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-snippet-illustration@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-snippet-illustration@2x.png 2x" title="Screenshot of a search engine result snippet from Google, with the title, URL and description highlighted." width="600"/></picture></div>
<p>A good title is…</p>
<ul>
<li>easy to read</li>
<li>has important keywords at the start</li>
<li>is unique</li>
<li>has a length between <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">285</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">575</code> pixels <br/>
(<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">30</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">60</code> characters respectively).</li>
</ul>
<p>The same applies for a good meta description—except that the preferred length is from <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">430</code> to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">920</code> pixels (<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">70</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">155</code> characters respectively) and that we recommend including a call-to-action.</p>
<p>Especially for larger stores, it’s impossible to manually define titles and meta descriptions for every single product. Therefore we recommend working with templates to generate them automatically. You should be able to manually overwrite the automatically generated titles and meta descriptions.</p>
<p>That way you can generate decent titles and meta descriptions in the blink of an eye, and yet you can always go in and tweak them to perfection.</p>
<p>Here is an example template:</p>
<ul>
<li>Title tag: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$productName - $storeName</code>, which translates to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Osprey Kyte 46 pack Women’s - Into The Wild</code>
</li>
<li>Meta description: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Go on an adventure with the $productName. Order now for $productPrice—free shipping and next day-delivery!</code>, which translates to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Go on an adventure with the Osprey Kyte 46 pack Women’s. Order now for $180—free shipping and next day delivery!</code>
</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="headings">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#headings" tabindex="-1"></a>4. Descriptive headings</h3>
<p>Headings help visitors and search engines to quickly see what a page is about and how it’s structured.</p>
<p>We recommend using the H1 heading for the product name. Then, use H2 headings for the most important sections such as the product’s features, technical specification, reviews, and Q&amp;As. You can try to weave the product name into one of the H2s naturally, but if you’re finding that it hurts the user experience, don’t do it.</p>
<p>For our example, this would result in:</p>
<ul>
<li>H1: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Osprey Kyte 46 pack Women’s</code>
</li>
<li>H2s:
    <ul>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Features</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Technical Specifications</code></li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Reviews</code> or <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Reviews for Osprey Kyte 46 pack Women’s</code>
</li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Questions and Answers</code></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="product-attributes">

<p>Including product attributes helps you to rank for queries around the product. Include the brand name, series name, model name, and SKU.</p>
<p>Linking these product attributes is also a great way to strengthen your internal link structure.</p>

<h3 id="product-descriptions">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#product-descriptions" tabindex="-1"></a>6. Write engaging product descriptions</h3>
<p>Solid product descriptions convince visitors to buy from you. Make them personal and engaging, explain the product’s benefits and features, but don’t write an essay—nobody’s interested in that.</p>
<p>This is also where you can incorporate closely related, low-competition queries that you want to rank for. But here again, don’t overdo it.</p>
<p>Cover the key features first. For example, with the Osprey Kyte 46 pack Women’s, REI highlights:</p>
<ul>
<li>What the backpack is best used for</li>
<li>Whether a raincover is included</li>
<li>What the backpack’s capacity is</li>
<li>Whether the backpack is reservoir (“hydration bladder”) compatible</li>
</ul>
<p>Besides describing the product, we also highly recommend listing its specifications such as size, color, and weight—everything a visitor may be searching for in Google and needing so they can make a purchase.</p>

<h3 id="breadcrumbs">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#breadcrumbs" tabindex="-1"></a>8. Include breadcrumb navigation</h3>
<p>Good breadcrumb navigation works well for both visitors and search engines. Users can quickly grasp the store’s hierarchy and jump up to a category page using the breadcrumbs, and they add relevant internal links for search engines too. But there are different views on how to structure them.
Here’s an example of poorly structured breadcrumbs that don’t help anyone:</p>
<section aria-label="Bad example of code" class="code-box code-box--negative"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">Home &gt; Hiking backpacks for women</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>And here’s an example of breadcrumbs that are structured well:</p>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">Home &gt; Backpacks &gt; Hiking backpacks &gt; Hiking backpacks for women</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p><strong>Important</strong>: be sure to <a href="https://developers.google.com/search/docs/advanced/structured-data/breadcrumb#json-ld" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">mark up your breadcrumbs</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> with Schema to make it even clearer what the breadcrumbs are and how they should be interpreted by machines.</p>

<h3 id="related-products">

<p>We like REI’s approach (yes, we’re big fans), where they feature two types of related products:</p>
<ol>
<li>Alternative backpacks that are often considered by visitors</li>

<h3 id="faq-qa">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#faq-qa" tabindex="-1"></a>11. Include a FAQ or Q&amp;A section (and mark it up with Schema)</h3>

<h3 id="page-load">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#page-load" tabindex="-1"></a>12. Keep page load times down</h3>
<p>Fast loading stores make more money. In 2006, Amazon was already studying the effect of page load times on conversions, and they found that every <a href="/academy/page-speed-resources/faq/amazon-page-speed-study/">100ms in added page load time cost them 1% in sales</a>.</p>
<p>In 2017, <a href="https://www.thinkwithgoogle.com/marketing-strategies/app-and-mobile/mobile-page-speed-new-industry-benchmarks/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">a Google study</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> showed that as page load time goes from one second to 10 seconds, the probability of a mobile site visitor bouncing increases by 123%.</p>
<p>While page speed has long been a ranking factor for Google, now with the roll out of the <a href="https://developers.google.com/search/blog/2021/04/more-details-page-experience" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Page Experience update</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> and the introduction of <a href="/academy/core-web-vitals/">Core Web Vitals</a> page load time has become even more important.</p>
<p>Optimizing for speed is a technical endeavor, and there are hundreds of tiny things you can tweak to squeeze every millisecond out of your pages. But in practice, focusing on these three best practices will usually get you 90% of the way there:</p>
<p><strong>1. Use a Content Delivery Network (CDN)</strong>: instead of hosting your store in a single place, your content is distributed around the world. When somebody visits your store, they connect to the endpoint closest to their location, decreasing the latency to reach your store. Plus, CDNs can cache your pages so they can be served blazingly fast.</p>
<p><strong>2. Optimize your assets</strong>: examples of assets are images, JavaScript code, and CSS stylesheets. These are essential to ensure your store looks and behaves as it should, but if you mismanage them, they can also worsen your page load times. Besides the image SEO best practices we described in section “High-quality images”, be sure to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Minify and compress your JavaScript and CSS to optimize their size.</li>
<li>Load your assets at the right moment. A good example of this are photos of related products placed below the fold: you don't need to load them immediately, slowing down the rendering of your pages. Instead, load and display them once the rest of the page has loaded, a technique called "lazy loading". You can do the same with parts of your CSS and JS files: only immediately load what you absolutely need to display the page in the browser, then load the rest later.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>3. Use browser caching</strong>: most pages on your store rely on a shared set of assets, such as CSS stylesheets, Javascript code, and common images such as your store’s logo. The problem is that browsers happily re-request any asset they encounter, meaning that when a visitor navigates from one page to another, their browser loads all the assets again. That’s such a waste!</p>
<p>To prevent this, your web server can signal to the browser to keep a cached copy of the assets and re-use them in the future. This is done by sending a cache header together with the asset, instructing the browser to re-use the asset until a certain expiry time.</p>

<h3 id="xml-sitemap">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#xml-sitemap" tabindex="-1"></a>13. Include product detail pages in the XML sitemap</h3>
<p>Whether you publish new products or update existing ones, you want Google to crawl and (re)index them as soon as possible. That’s where XML sitemaps come in.</p>
<p>Create separate XML sitemaps for different content types. For example, create one each for category pages, product pages, manufacturers, supporting pages, and blog articles. Be sure to only include indexable pages that return the HTTP status <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">200 OK</code> and limit the number of URLs per XML sitemap to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">50,000</code>.</p>

<h3 id="variants">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#variants" tabindex="-1"></a>14. Don’t let near-identical product variants cannibalize each other</h3>
<p>Going with a somewhat extreme example will help explain why product variants can be problematic. Take these sneakers that are available in all the different styles and sizes:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-academy-product-page-seo-variants@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-academy-product-page-seo-variants@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-academy-product-page-seo-variants@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-academy-product-page-seo-variants@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-academy-product-page-seo-variants@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-academy-product-page-seo-variants@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-academy-product-page-seo-variants@1x.png"/><img alt="Screenshot showing sneakers with product variants" class="image" height="312" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-academy-product-page-seo-variants@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-academy-product-page-seo-variants@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-academy-product-page-seo-variants@2x.png 2x" title="Screenshot showing sneakers with product variants" width="600"/></picture></div>
<p>There's 17 sizes and 10 styles, leading to 170 product variants. Creating separate product pages for each variant leads to a lot of potential duplicate content. Tricky business!</p>
<p>Alright, let's get back to our backpacks. Say you’re selling the Osprey Kyte 46 Pack for women in black, blue, yellow, red and green. Aside from the color, all other product properties are the same. Should you now create indexable product pages for each color?</p>
<p>There's two factors that influence your decision:</p>
<ol>
<li>Whether there's search demand</li>
<li>Whether you have the resources to write unique product descriptions</li>
</ol>
<p>Even if the anwer to both questions is "yes" you still need to run the numbers on this investment. If search demand is so low that you’re not going to get a positive ROI out of writing unique pages with unique descriptions for each color, then you need to choose a primary variant and canonicalize the other variants to it, to prevent these pages from fighting each other in the rankings—something we call keyword cannibalization.</p>
<h4 id="canonicalize-if-unique-product-pages-dont-make-sense">Canonicalize if unique product pages don't make sense</h4>
<p>So let’s say the red backpack is the primary variant in this example: then make sure it has <a href="/academy/canonical/#canonical-url-referencing-itself">a self-referencing canonical URL</a>—telling search engines it is the canonical version and should be indexed. The blue and green variants then have a canonical URL pointing to the red variant.</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-near-identical-product@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-near-identical-product@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-near-identical-product@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-near-identical-product@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-near-identical-product@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-near-identical-product@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-near-identical-product@1x.png"/><img alt="Illustration of canonicalized variant products" class="image" height="215" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-near-identical-product@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-near-identical-product@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-near-identical-product@2x.png 2x" title="Illustration of canonicalized variant products" width="600"/></picture></div>
<p>This setup communicates your indexing preferences clearly, and it works well for visitors too — they can for example share and bookmark the variant they’re looking at.</p>
<p>You may be left wondering what happens if you don’t canonicalize but just create three near-identical, indexable product pages instead. In that case, Google may decide to canonicalize the product variants themselves, and they’d flag this in Google Search Console’s Index Coverage report under the <a href="/academy/index-coverage/faq/duplicate-different-canonical/">Duplicate, Google chose different canonical than user</a> status.</p>
<p>However, if you do think you’ll get a positive ROI out of creating separate, indexable product pages for each color because there’s enough search demand, then by all means, go for it. But be sure to include unique SKU/GTIN numbers, write unique product descriptions, and show images that are specific to the product variant!</p>
<h3 id="multiple-categories">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#multiple-categories" tabindex="-1"></a>15. Canonicalize products that are in multiple categories</h3>
<p>You can have perfectly good reasons for putting products in multiple categories. Maybe a backpack is already sitting in the “Osprey hiking backpacks for women'' category, but it turns out mountain bikers love it as well. And to top it all off, it’s also on sale. When visitors navigate through those categories—or when you send out a newsletter highlighting that there’s a sale—you want them to find the backpack, so it only makes sense to include it in the other categories as well.</p>
<p>So now the backpack is in three categories, and every product page has its own unique URL:</p>
<ol>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/backpacks/hiking/osprey-kyte-46-pack-womens/</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/backpacks/sale/osprey-kyte-46-pack-womens/</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/backpacks/mountain-biking/osprey-kyte-46-pack-womens/</code></li>
</ol>
<p>Because you don’t want these pages to compete amongst themselves, we recommend picking a primary product and canonicalizing the other ones to that—similar to how we handled the near-identical products.</p>

<h3 id="discontinued-products">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#discontinued-products" tabindex="-1"></a>16. Leverage the discontinued products</h3>
<p>Lots of stores don’t leverage the potential of discontinued products. These are products that are either temporarily or permanently unavailable. They may be out of stock, their product line may have been discontinued, or maybe you stopped selling them. Whatever the reason, handle them with care, because these discontinued product pages often still carry a lot of value!</p>
<p>When we say “value”, we mean they either…</p>
<ul>
<li>are still getting (organic) traffic, or</li>
<li>have links from other websites</li>
</ul>
<p>If you expect a product to be in stock again later, keep it online and:</p>
<ul>
<li>Keep it in the XML sitemap and on-site search</li>
<li>Put “out of stock” or a similar label on the product’s picture</li>
<li>Hide the product’s price and keep visitors from adding it to the cart</li>
<li>Show alternative products that are likely to fit the visitor’s needs</li>
<li>Remove the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Offer</code> property from the Product Schema markup to prevent Schema penalties</li>
<li>Let visitors know the product will be available again, or offer them a way to be notified once it’s back in stock</li>
<li>Pause all active PPC campaigns directing customers to the product’s page</li>
</ul>
<p>Here’s an “out of stock” page for <a href="https://www.rei.com/product/895638/osprey-kyte-46-pack-womens-2018" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">the 2018 version</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> of the example backpack:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-alternatives@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-alternatives@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-alternatives@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-alternatives@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-alternatives@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-alternatives@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-alternatives@1x.png"/><img alt="Screenshot showing an out of stock product with suggested alternatives" class="image" height="388" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-alternatives@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-alternatives@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-product-page-seo-alternatives@2x.png 2x" title="Screenshot showing an out of stock product with suggested alternatives" width="600"/></picture></div>
<p>In the red rectangle, they feature alternative products. And the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">See available version</code> link takes you to <a href="https://www.rei.com/product/141492/osprey-kyte-46-pack-womens" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">the backpack’s successor</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>, which is currently being sold. Note the “Adventurers who viewed this also viewed” copy—very much on brand!</p>
<p>If you don’t expect products to come back into stock <strong>and</strong> they have no value, then it’s safe to just return a <a href="/academy/http-status-codes/#410">HTTP Status 410</a>.</p>
<p>But if they do have value, take one of these approaches:</p>
<ol>
<li>Keep each product online as long as search demand is high and show alternative products. We recommend that you remove internal links to it, take the product out of the XML sitemap and on-site search, and apply the robots noindex directive.</li>
<li>Reuse those URLs for other, highly similar products.</li>
<li>Implement 301 redirects to the most relevant alternative products.</li>
</ol>

<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/ian-howells@1x.avif, /media/people/ian-howells@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/ian-howells@1x.webp, /media/people/ian-howells@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Ian Howells" class="image" height="200" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/ian-howells@1x.jpg" srcset="/media/people/ian-howells@1x.jpg, /media/people/ian-howells@2x.jpg 2x" title="Ian Howells" width="200"/></picture></span></span></div>
<div class="quote-box__content typo">
<blockquote class="quote-box__blockquote">
<p>When dealing with permanently discontinued products, I'll do one of two things:</p>
<ol>
<li>If there's still demand for the specific model, and the page was ranking on primarily model specific terms, I'll make a new blog post and 301 the old product URL to it. It will be titled something along the links of "What Happened to %ProductName%?". This is a great opportunity to not only grab CTR, but provide a good resource that explains when it got discontinued, and offer some alternatives.</li>
<li>If the page ranked for more generic terms like Brand + Product Type, I'll generally 301 redirect to a newer model (assuming one's available.)</li>
</ol>
</blockquote>

<h3 id="orphans">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#orphans" tabindex="-1"></a>17. Don’t let product pages become orphans</h3>
<p>With a large product inventory that’s constantly changing, it’s easy to lose sight of products. Some product pages may end up becoming so-called <a href="/academy/orphan-pages/">orphan pages</a> when their parent category gets removed.</p>
<p>Orphan pages aren’t a part of the site structure and thus have no internal links. This generally leads to lower rankings—and sometimes even Google deciding to completely de-index the products.</p>
<p>It’s hard enough to get your product pages to rank with internal links, so be on the lookout for this!</p>

<h2 id="what-are-cookies">What are cookies?</h2>
<p>Cookies are small text files which are stored on the browser or hard drive of your Device when you visit a webpage or use an application. Cookies may be needed to show the webpage or application on your Device and are also used to enhance the user experience. Cookies cannot damage your Devices or the files saved on it. There are different types of cookies. Some cookies come directly from our website (first party cookies) and others come from third parties which place cookies on our site (third party cookies).</p>
<p>Cookies can be stored for varying lengths of time on your browser or device. Session cookies are deleted from your computer or device when you close your web-browser. Persistent cookies will remain stored on your computer or Device until deleted or until they reach their expiry date.</p>
<h2 id="how-do-we-use-cookies">How do we use cookies?</h2>
<h3 id="essential-technical-cookies">Essential (Technical) cookies</h3>
<p>We try to provide an advanced and user-friendly website that adapts automatically to needs and wishes of our visitors and users. To achieve this, we use technical cookies to i.e. show you our Website, to make it function correctly, to create your ContentKing account, to sign you in and to manage your requests. These technical cookies are necessary for our website to function properly.</p>
<h3 id="functional-cookies">Functional cookies</h3>
<p>We also use functional cookies to remember your preferences and settings (as username, password, language etc.) and to help you to use the Website efficiently and effectively. These functional cookies are not strictly necessary for the functioning of our Website, but they add functionality for you and enhance your experience.</p>
<h3 id="analytical-cookies">Analytical cookies</h3>
<p>We use analytical cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. These analytic cookies generate statistical and other information about website use by means of cookies, which are stored on users’ Devices. The information generated relating to our Website is used to create reports about the use of the Website. Analytical cookies may be placed without your consent, unless such cookies have an impact on your privacy. In such cases, prior consent will be asked for.</p>
<h3 id="google-analytics">Google Analytics</h3>
<p>We use Google Analytics cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. With these analytical cookies a permanent cookie is saved on your device to register your use of the Website. Google Analytics analyses this data and provides us with the results. This way, we obtain insight into the traffic of the Website and the way in which the Website is used. Based on this information we are able to make specific adjustments to the Website.</p>
<p>The information that we obtain is transferred to Google and stored by Google on servers outside the European Economic Area. We have entered into a data processing agreement with Google in which agreements have been made regarding the processing of personal data.</p>
<p>If you would like more information about analytical cookies and their expiry date, please visit the Google Analytics <a href="https://developers.google.com/analytics/devguides/collection/gajs/cookie-usage">information page</a>. Google also offers an <a href="https://tools.google.com/dlpage/gaoptout?hl=en">opt-out option</a> for data collection within the scope of Google Analytics.</p>
<h3 id="marketing-cookies">Marketing cookies</h3>
<p>We use third party marketing cookies in order to follow your internet browsing behavior and collect data and information on your browsing behavior from various websites you visited. This information is used to make the content of the displayed advertisements as relevant as possible, and to limit repetition of the same advertisements.</p>

<p>By using the Website you can get access to social media websites, such as Facebook, Twitter and LinkedIn. These buttons come with a code which has been made by these networks themselves. Using these so called social plug-ins you can login to your social media profile and subsequently share information from the Website with others. With these social plug-ins third party marketing cookies are saved on your Devices. These cookies serve the purpose of optimizing your user experience.</p>
<p>Companies such as LinkedIn, Twitter and Facebook may share your personal data outside the European Economic Area. Please read our Privacy Policy <a href="/legal/privacy-policy/">here</a> and the respective social network’s privacy statement to see how these companies treat your (personal) data.</p>
<h3 id="pixels">Pixels</h3>
<p>We also place pixels of third parties. A pixel keeps track of your surfing behavior and how you use the Website. These data are aggregated and give us information about the target group that visits our website. Based on this information, we can show an advertisement to a specific or similar target group on the website of the third party that produced the pixel.</p>
