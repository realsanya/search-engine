<h1 class="heading__title">H1-H6 Headings and SEO: everything you need to know</h1>
<div class="heading__params">
<span aria-hidden="true" class="heading__label">
										Last updated:

<span aria-label="The date of last update" class="heading__caption">

<div class="post-detail__text typo">
<section aria-label="Headings in short" class="highlighted-box highlighted-box--with-background typo-block"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">
<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/headings--reversed.svg)"></i></span>Headings in short

<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Headings give both your visitors and search engines important clues about the content’s hierarchy and relevancy.</p>
<p>There are six "importance levels", from the most important H1 heading to the least important H6 heading.</p>

<h2 id="what-are-headings">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-are-headings" tabindex="-1"></a>What are headings?</h2>
<p>Headings are phrases at the beginning of a section that explain what it's about. Correct use of headings on a page provides hierarchy and overview, and helps quickly communicate what a web page is about. They help visitors quickly scan a page, and they help search engines quickly understand a page's structure and what it's about.</p>
<p>Headings come in six levels: H1 through H6, of which headings H1, H2 and H3 are most frequently used. Headings are defined in a page's HTML.</p>
<p>The H1 heading is often referred to as the "main heading", while <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H2-H6 headings</code> are referred to as "subheadings" because they indicate sub-levels.</p>
<h3 id="what-about-heading-tags">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-about-heading-tags" tabindex="-1"></a>What about heading tags?</h3>
<p>People often refer to "heading tags" when talking about headings.</p>
<p>To avoid any confusion, let's take an example and look at the H1 heading element as it appears in the HTML source: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;h1&gt;This is the H1 heading&lt;/h1&gt;</code>:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;h1&gt;</code>: opening tag for the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code>
</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">This is the H1 heading</code>: the text that'll appear on a visitor's screen as the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code>.</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;/h1&gt;</code>: closing tag for the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code>
</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="and-what-about-header-tags">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#and-what-about-header-tags" tabindex="-1"></a>And what about header tags?</h3>
<p>First of all, don't confuse header tags with the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;head&gt;</code>-section. Secondly, header tags are often incorrectly used to refer to headings (I'm looking at you HubSpot).</p>
<p>In reality they can be used to define the header of a document or section. This means they can be wrapped around navigational aids, or they can be wrapped <em>around</em> headings. Using them is optional, here's an example:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;html&gt;
    &lt;head&gt;
        &lt;title&gt;Headings H1-H6 and SEO&lt;/title&gt;
    &lt;/head&gt;
    &lt;body&gt;
        &lt;article&gt;
            &lt;header&gt;
                &lt;h1&gt;Header tags explained&lt;/h1&gt;
                &lt;h2&gt;A brief introduction to header tags&lt;/h2&gt;
            &lt;/header&gt;
        &lt;/article&gt;
    &lt;/body&gt;
&lt;/html&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<section aria-label="Useful resources" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-background highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--resources-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Useful resources</div>

</ul>

<h2 id="why-use">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#why-use" tabindex="-1"></a>Why use headings?</h2>
<p>Headings are useful for both visitors and search engines:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<strong>Visitors</strong> use headings to quickly scan a page to determine whether it looks relevant for them. What's more, visually impaired visitors use screen readers to browse the web. And screenreaders rely on headings to "understand" web pages, and help their users to navigate.</li>
<li>
<strong>Search engines</strong> use headings to understand a page's structure and what topics it's about. While only a small factor in search engines' ranking algorithms, including keywords in headings <em>does</em> contribute to improved SEO performance. Even if you're not on board with the idea that headings make for a small ranking factor, keep in mind that everything you do to improve the user experience generally translates to better SEO performance.</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="headings-and-featured-snippets">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#headings-and-featured-snippets" tabindex="-1"></a>Headings and featured snippets</h3>
<p>Search engines may display featured snippets, special snippets containing an extracts from a page, in their SERP. They need to understand content well enough to confidently pick these extracts, and they rely on headings to do this. In summary, smart and correct use of headings helps you appear in featured snippets, which in turn increases your visibility in the SERP and drives more organic traffic to your site.</p>

<h2 id="hierarchy">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#hierarchy" tabindex="-1"></a>Heading hierarchy</h2>
<p>Headings should be logically structured on a page. A page starts with the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code>, followed by an <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H2 heading</code> and — should you need another sub-section — it can be followed by an <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H3 heading</code>. If you need more sub-sections, you can even add <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H4-H6 subheadings</code>.</p>
<p>In the illustration below we're using three heading levels:</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-3" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-headings-en@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-headings-en@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.png"/><img alt="Visual that explains the hiearchy among h1 h2 h3 headings" class="image" height="355" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-headings-en@2x.png 2x" title="Visual that explains the hiearchy among h1 h2 h3 headings" width="600"/></picture></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-3">A page with H1, H2 and H3 headings.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Don't skip a level in the hierarchy. For example, don't go from an <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code> to an <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H3 heading</code>. There should be an <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H2 heading</code> in between.</p>
<p>Here's a good example of correct heading usage:</p>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;h1&gt;This should describe the page's topic&lt;/h1&gt;
&lt;h2&gt;This is an H2 subheading&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;h3&gt;This is an H3 subheading&lt;/h3&gt;
&lt;h2&gt;This is another H2 subheading&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;h3&gt;This is another H3 subheading&lt;/h3&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>If you're writing long-form content, you can go all out with headings. You could have a heading structure like:</p>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;h1&gt;This should describe the page's topic&lt;/h1&gt;
&lt;h2&gt;This is an H2 subheading&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;h2&gt;This is another H2 subheading&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;h3&gt;This is an H3 subheading&lt;/h3&gt;
&lt;h4&gt;This is an H4 subheading&lt;/h4&gt;
&lt;h5&gt;This is an H5 subheading&lt;/h5&gt;
&lt;h6&gt;This is an H6 subheading&lt;/h6&gt;

&lt;h2&gt;This is yet another H2 subheading&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;h3&gt;This is an H3 subheading&lt;/h3&gt;
&lt;h4&gt;This is an H4 subheading&lt;/h4&gt;
&lt;h5&gt;This is an H5 subheading&lt;/h5&gt;
&lt;h6&gt;This is an H6 subheading&lt;/h6&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>And here's an example heading hierarchy that you should avoid:</p>
<section aria-label="Bad example of code" class="code-box code-box--negative"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;h2&gt;Why is this H2 at the top?&lt;/h2&gt;
&lt;h4&gt;And why is this an H4 heading?&lt;/h4&gt;
&lt;h1&gt;This H1 heading should actually have been at the top&lt;/h1&gt;
&lt;h3&gt;A random H3 heading here&lt;/h3&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>

<h2 id="heading-sizes">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#heading-sizes" tabindex="-1"></a>Heading sizes</h2>
<p>The further down you go with headings, the smaller the headings should become. The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code> should be the largest heading on the page. The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H2 heading</code> is a little smaller than the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1</code>, and in turn the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H3 heading</code> is smaller than then <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H2 heading</code> and so on.</p>

<h2 id="heading-order-in-html-source">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#heading-order-in-html-source" tabindex="-1"></a>Heading order in HTML source</h2>
<p>It doesn't matter in what order headings appear in your HTML source. The order in which the headings are presented on a visitor's screen (or when a page is rendered by search engines) is leading.</p>
<h2 id="h1-heading">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#h1-heading" tabindex="-1"></a>H1 heading</h2>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code> describes a page's main topic. It should be highly related to the page's content, unique across your website and a page should only contain one <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code>.</p>
<p>Don't put the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code> around an image, since search engines don't understand images as good as they do text. You'd be missing out on an opportunity to signal to search engines what a page is about.</p>
<section aria-label="Bad example of code" class="code-box code-box--negative"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;h1&gt;&lt;img src="logo.png"&gt;&lt;/h1&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>Your <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code> <em>can</em> match your <a href="/academy/title-tag/">page title</a>, but it doesn't need to. Visitors are much more likely to notice your <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code> than your page title, so always prioritize making sure your <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code> makes sense for your visitors.</p>

<h3 id="h1-heading-length">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#h1-heading-length" tabindex="-1"></a>H1 heading length</h3>
<p>You should avoid making the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code> too long. If you do, there's a big chance the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code> won't be readable for your visitors, which hurts their <a href="/academy/ux-seo/#clear-headings">user experience</a>. We recommend sticking to a maximum length of <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">60 characters</code>, similar to the title attribute. As for the minimum length, it needs to be long enough to make sense. In our experience, sticking to at least <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">4 characters</code> helps you with this. Above all: make sure the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code> makes sense for your visitors.</p>
<h3 id="multiple-h1-headings">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#multiple-h1-headings" tabindex="-1"></a>Multiple H1 headings? Not even in HTML5.</h3>
<p>When the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">HTML5</code> specification was first introduced, support for multiple <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 headings</code> per page (each in their own section-element) was included. However, since browsers didn't adopt this recommendation, support for this was subsequently dropped when the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">HTML5.1</code> standard was published. For more information, see <a href="https://github.com/w3c/html/issues/169" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">this W3C thread</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>

<h3 id="other-uses-h1-headings">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#other-uses-h1-headings" tabindex="-1"></a>Other uses for the H1 heading</h3>
<p>If you haven't defined a page title, or if search engines didn't find your title to be relevant enough, they may show the page's <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H1 heading</code> as the title in the page's search engine result – its snippet.</p>
<h2 id="h2-h6-headings">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#h2-h6-headings" tabindex="-1"></a>H2-H6 subheadings</h2>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Subheadings H2-H6</code> are used to further structure the content.</p>
<p>Most pages benefit from having at least one <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H2 heading</code>, but if it doesn't make sense for visitors to include one then you can leave it out.</p>
<p>Use <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">H2-H6 subheadings</code> where it makes sense, and keep in mind not to skip heading levels.</p>
<h2 class="toc-hidden" id="faqs">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#faqs" tabindex="-1"></a>Frequently asked questions about headings</h2>
<h3 id="headings-ranking-improvement">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#headings-ranking-improvement" tabindex="-1"></a>Do headings really improve my rankings?</h3>
<p>Yes, they do! Even today, headings still play a role in communicating relevance for keywords to search engines.</p>
<p>While the role of headings in search engine algorithms has decreased over the years, it does send a clear signal to search engines regarding what a page is about.</p>
<h3 id="h1-heading-title-same">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#h1-heading-title-same" tabindex="-1"></a>Can my page title and H1 heading be the same?</h3>
<p>Yes, they can. But keep in mind to <strong>always</strong> choose an H1 heading that makes most sense for your visitors.</p>
<h3 id="multiple-h1-headings-faq">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#multiple-h1-headings-faq" tabindex="-1"></a>Can I have multiple H1 headings on one page?</h3>
<p>It is recommended to use only one H1 heading per page. Multiple H1 headings per page were introduced with HTML5, but browsers didn’t adopt the practice. Since HTML5.1, there is no support for multiple H1 headings.</p>
