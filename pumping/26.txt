<section aria-label="Recovering from Google Ranking Drop in short" class="highlighted-box highlighted-box--with-background typo-block"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">
<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/google-ranking--reversed.svg)"></i></span>Recovering from Google Ranking Drop in short
	</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>A dramatic Google ranking drop can be caused by many factors, such as for instance content changes, an algorithm update, technical issues, improvements competitors made, SERP lay-out changes and a Google penalty.</p>
<p>We've put together a handy checklist to quickly diagnose the issue and recover your rankings!</p>

<p>Imagine this: your rankings suddenly dropped dramatically. Maybe by a dozen positionsâ€”or maybe they've dropped outside the top 100.</p>
<p>Your face has gone pale and you've got sweaty palms. What now?</p>
<p>How do you go about diagnosing what the issue is, and in what order? What are the most likely reasons for your rankings to have dropped?</p>
<h2 id="no-panic">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#no-panic" tabindex="-1"></a>Step 1: Don't panic!</h2>
<p>Don't panicâ€”this happens to all of us. This is a part of SEO. In fact, it's the one thing we can be sure of in SEO.</p>
<p>The good news is that often there's nothing to worry about. Maybe your <a href="/academy/best-rank-tracker/">rank tracking application</a> is acting up or Google's tuning their ranking algorithm. Check again the next day, and often you'll see that things have gone back to normal.</p>
<p>But what if your rankings don't bounce back, or if you're just worried and want to make sure everything is in order? Go through the steps listed in this guide to quickly determine whether your website is suffering from SEO issues, or all is good and Google only needs a bit of time.</p>

It's crucial to have a list of things to check in place, so that you do the right things for the right reasons and don't start pushing buttons at random that could make matters worse. Always keep in mind that Google doesn't care about your website, they only care about their users (and their own bottom line). Follow the user and rankings will come.</p>

<h2 id="verify">Step 2: Did your Google Rankings <em>really</em> drop?</h2>
<p>Did you rankings really drop, or is your rank tracker indeed just acting up?</p>
<p>Check your rank tracker's website and Twitter account to see if there are any known issues. Google and the trackers play a bit of a cat and mouse game. When Google changes their search engine result pages, rank trackers often need to update their software. Until they can, the rankings they report to their users may be off.</p>
<section aria-label="Pro tip" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--protip-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Pro tip</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Set up rank tracking for your most important keywords using a second rank tracking application. Then, when you're questioning your main one's validity, you can check your backup for verification.</p>
</div></section>

<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#verify-using-analytics-google-search-console-and-other-tools" tabindex="-1"></a>Verify using analytics, Google Search Console and other tools</h3>

<h2 id="impact">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#impact" tabindex="-1"></a>Step 3: Scope the ranking drop's impact</h2>
<p>The search queries and pages that are impacted by a ranking drop say a lot about the underlying issue.</p>
<p>Use your rank tracking application, web analytics data, Google Search Console data, and Bing Webmaster Tools data to identify what the scope of the ranking drop's impact is so we can diagnose the issues.</p>
<p>List all search queries that are showing a ranking drop, and include for each one:</p>
<ul>
<li>What cluster the query belonged to</li>
<li>Their old ranking (to establish a baseline)</li>
<li>Their new ranking</li>
<li>The difference</li>
<li>The URL that was ranking</li>
<li>The content type that was ranking</li>
<li>Whether the <a href="/academy/control-crawl-indexing/#how-does-indexing-work">page is indexable</a>
</li>
<li>Any comments that may be useful (for instance, the date when you've made changes to this page)</li>
</ul>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-3" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-google-ranking-drop-screenshot-sheet-en@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-google-ranking-drop-screenshot-sheet-en@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-google-ranking-drop-screenshot-sheet-en@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-google-ranking-drop-screenshot-sheet-en@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-google-ranking-drop-screenshot-sheet-en@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-google-ranking-drop-screenshot-sheet-en@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-google-ranking-drop-screenshot-sheet-en@1x.png"/><img alt="Template diagnose Google Rankings drop" class="image" height="191" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-google-ranking-drop-screenshot-sheet-en@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-google-ranking-drop-screenshot-sheet-en@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-google-ranking-drop-screenshot-sheet-en@2x.png 2x" title="Template diagnose Google Rankings drop" width="600"/></picture></div>

<p>To help you with this process, we've made <a href="https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/18OCepb_yC9WGrvRbcESmjo25EBG8oanLH1iUFaRcxx4/edit#gid=0" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">a template</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>

<section class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--steps-style"><div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>If you're using Google Analytics, you can quickly identify sections that took a hit by heading over to:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Behavior</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Site Content</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Content Drilldown</code>
</li>
<li>Choose Segment <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Organic Traffic</code>
</li>
<li>Compare the period in which your rankings dropped to the period in which they were still good.</li>
</ol>

<h2 id="recent-changes">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#recent-changes" tabindex="-1"></a>Step 4: Recent website changes</h2>
<p>Ranking drops are often caused by large changes to a website. When you've <a href="/academy/website-migrations/">migrated</a> a bunch of pages, rolled out a (responsive) redesign or reworked a lot of content you'll see big fluctuations in your rankings. This is natural, and should only worry you in ase your rankings don't come back to their previous state.</p>
<h3 id="ask-around-within-your-team">

<h3 id="check-your-seo-monitoring-tool">

<p>If your rankings are essential to your business, then it's a no-brainer to monitor your website for on-page SEO changes. We distinguish between technical changes and content changes â€” technical changes often affect the entire website, whereas content changes often only affect the pages directly affected.</p>
<h3 id="technical-changes">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#technical-changes" tabindex="-1"></a>Technical changes</h3>
<p>Double check that search engines are still crawling and indexing your website in the same way as before.</p>
<p>Do this by crawling the website and checking specifically for changes in:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<a href="/academy/http-status-codes/">HTTP status codes:</a> are your pages still returning HTTP status 200, and are redirects still in place?</li>
<li>
<a href="/academy/canonical/">Canonical URL</a>: have your canonical URLs changed?</li>
<li>
<a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/">Meta robots tag</a>: have search engines suddenly been told not to index your key pages?</li>
<li>
<a href="/academy/robotstxt/">Robots.txt</a>: was your <a href="/blog/robots-txt-change-tracking/">robots.txt changed</a>? Do search engines still have access to all the sections of your website that should be indexed?</li>
<li>
<a href="/academy/hreflang/">Hreflang</a>: are your hreflang definitions still set up correctly?</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="content-changes">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#content-changes" tabindex="-1"></a>Content changes</h3>
<p>Search engines use content to determine relevancy for search queries, so content changes has a big impact on your rankings. And pages that are removed have an even bigger impact.</p>
<p>To double-check whether your content has changed, crawl your website and check specifically for changes in:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<a href="/academy/title-tag/">Title</a>: even the smallest change in the title tag can have a big impact on your rankings. Were there any changes?</li>
<li>
<a href="/academy/meta-description/">Meta description</a>: while not impacting your rankings directly, the meta description does impact the click-through rate (CTR) for your search result. And the CTR does impact your rankings. So check for changes in your meta descriptions.</li>
<li>
<a href="/academy/headings/">Headings</a>: headings communicate relevancy to search engines too. Changes to headings may impact your rankings, so check to see if they were changed.</li>
<li>Body content: Google heavily relies on your page's body content to determine what queries it's relevant for. If you've found changes in titles, meta descriptions, or headings, there's a big chance the body content has been changed too. See if you can find the changes that were made. For instance, WordPress tracks these changes automatically. Alternatively, you can look up Google's cached version of your page with a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">cache:</code> query or by checking <a href="http://web.archive.org/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">the Way Back machine</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</li>

<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#blocking-googlebot" tabindex="-1"></a>Blocking Googlebot</h3>
<p>More often than you'd think the reason why rankings drop is because a trigger happy system administrator decided to block all traffic from bots, or all traffic originating from the United States in the firewall.</p>
<p>Keep in mind that almost all crawl activity performed by Google is done from the United States, so you'd be actively preventing Google from crawling your site, which will quickly lead to your pages being deindexed.</p>
<p>Use a VPN with a location in the United States, and visit your site. If that works fine, do the same thing but additionally, define your user-agent as Googlebot (see the exact <a href="https://support.google.com/webmasters/answer/1061943?hl=en" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">user-agent strings here</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>). Does that work?</p>
<p>If that works fine, there's another thing to try: see if Google's IPs may be blocked. There's af ew ways you can do this:</p>
<ol>
<li>Use the <a href="https://search.google.com/search-console?action=inspect" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Test live URL</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> feature in Google Search Console. Fill in a URL there, and hit the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">TEST LIVE URL</code> button in the top right. If that process fails, Google's IPs may be blocked. Want to dig deeper? <a href="/academy/url-inspection-tool/#test-live-url">Here</a> we explain in detail how to test live URLs.</li>
<li>Use the <a href="https://search.google.com/test/rich-results" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Rich Snippet</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> tester to request a URL on your site and see if that works. If that fails too, you're on to something â€” Google may really be blocked on a IP-level!</li>
</ol>
<h3 id="load-time">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#load-time" tabindex="-1"></a>Load time</h3>
<p>Have the load times significantly increased for pages affect by the ranking drop?</p>
<ul>
<li>Check out your <a href="https://www.google.com/webmasters/tools/crawl-stats" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">crawl stats</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> in the old Google Search Console.</li>
<li>Check the page loadtime in Google Analytics (<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Behavior</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Site Speed</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Overview</code>)</li>
<li>Analyze it through <a href="https://crux.run/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">CrUX.run</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>
</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="algorithm-update">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#algorithm-update" tabindex="-1"></a>Step 6: Google algorithm update</h2>
<p>Google updates are often the reason behind ranking fluctuations.</p>
<p>According to <a href="https://searchengineland.com/google-made-3200-changes-to-search-in-the-past-year-319506" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Search Engine Land</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>, Google made 3.200 changes to their algorithms in 2018. We think that number only grew since then.</p>
<p>Some changes are small and barely noticable; other changes are huge and make or break businesses.</p>
<p>Check if your ranking drop coincides with a Google update. While Google is rarely open about smaller updates, SEO specialists across the globe keep a watchful eye and have built tools that find large changes in rankings and try to tie them to updates.</p>
<p>To check whether there's been reports of a Google update, check Barry Schwartz's <a href="https://www.wasthereagoogleupdate.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Was There A Google Update</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>
<p>To monitor ranking volatility over time, check out the tools below:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://www.advancedwebranking.com/google-algorithm-changes/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Advanced Web Ranking</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
<li><a href="https://www.rankranger.com/rank-risk-index" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">RankRanger</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
<li><a href="https://algoroo.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Algoroo</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
<li><a href="https://www.accuranker.com/grump/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">AccuRanker</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
</ul>
<p>Keep in mind that ranking drops due to Google algorithm updates don't always happen instantaneously. Often times, you'll see your rankings gradually slipping away. This makes sense, as Google has to recalculate and reprocess immense amounts of data sets.</p>
<p>Google filters such as Penguin or Panda can work the same way, leading to rankings drop over the course of several weeks.</p>

<h2 id="manual-action">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#manual-action" tabindex="-1"></a>Step 9: Google Manual Action</h2>
<p>When rankings drop, people are often afraid that they've received a Google penalty. In most cases they haven't â€” that's why this section is placed so far down in this article.</p>
<p>If you've gone through all of the other checks and you haven't found the reason for your ranking drop yet, do check whether you may have received a manual action.</p>
<h3 id="are-you-still-indexed">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#are-you-still-indexed" tabindex="-1"></a>Are your pages still indexed?</h3>
<p>Manual penalties come in different forms, the most severe being a complete de-indexing of your website â€” which usually only happens to websites that are clearly trying to game Google's system.</p>
<p>Do a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">site:</code> query to check if your website is still indexed. Example of a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">site:</code> query:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 1620px;"><img alt="Google site query" class="image" height="830" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/google-site-query.png" title="Google site query" width="1620"/></div>
<p>If your pages are indeed still indexed, then that's good news. If your pages aren't indexed anymore, this could mean you have a manual penalty. It could also mean you have a serious SEO issue in your website that's technical in nature, as outlined in <a href="/academy/ranking-drop/#technical-issues">step 5</a>.</p>
<h3 id="are-you-still-ranking">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#are-you-still-ranking" tabindex="-1"></a>Are you still ranking for non-branded queries?</h3>
<p>If Google applies a penalty, it's often less severe than a complete de-indexing of your website. A common example of a less severe penalty is only letting your website rank for its branded search queries.</p>
<p>Check to see if you can identify this kind of pattern in the search queries for which your website dropped. Are you still ranking for non-branded queries? If you are, then that's good news. If you aren't, a manual penalty could be the issue.</p>

<h2 id="hacked">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#hacked" tabindex="-1"></a>Step 10: Website hacked</h2>
<p>Unfortunately, site hacking is a really active line of business. Once they're "inside," hackers often infest websites with malicious code and spammy content and links. This is very harmful for your rankings, so it's important to rule out that you were hacked.</p>
<p>It's actually not likely your website was hacked, but if it has been, you need to know about it ASAP so that you can take measures to regain control of your website and restore it to its original condition.</p>
<p>Google scans websites for malicious code and activity. If they find your website's been hacked, they'll inform you about this through Google Search Console. While this check isn't fool proof, it literally just takes 10 seconds to check Google Search Console to see whether they know about a hack so we recommend always checking this.</p>

<h2 id="search-intent-behavior">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#search-intent-behavior" tabindex="-1"></a>Step 11: Change in search intent and behavior</h2>

<p>Take for example the query <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Birds of Prey</code> â€” this could refer to the movie that had trouble ranking (and was later named "Birds of Prey (and the Fantabulous Emancipation of One Harley Quinn"), the bird species. We may not realize this, but for a lot of queries the query intent changes over time.</p>
<p>Especially during strange times, like the COVID-19 pandemic. Search behavior rapidly changed, which must â€” directly or indirectly â€” have impacted rankings. Some products and services were no longer needed, and for others there's an extreme demand.</p>

<h2 id="competitors">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#competitors" tabindex="-1"></a>Step 12: Competitors making moves</h2>
<p>Your competition is constantly making moves, trying to over take you and thereby pushing your listings down in the search results.</p>
<p>While it's rare that the competition takes you over for many search queries in a short amount of time, competition can definitely play a role in your ranking drop.</p>
<p>Imagine this scenario: during a recent release, a set of 301 redirects was accidentally removed. URLs that carried a lot of authority now served <a href="/academy/http-status-codes/#404">404 â€“ Page not Found</a> pages instead, rendering that authority useless. At that same time, your competition stepped up their game and won some of your featured snippets. Together, this has a big impact and your website's rankings drop across the board.</p>
<p>Investigate and discover who you lost you rankings to. In <a href="/academy/ranking-drop/#impact">step 3</a> you made a list of search queries that your positions dropped for; now dig in and see who benefited from this. After you've done so, try to find out how they managed to beat you.</p>
<p>Examples:</p>
<ul>
<li>Did they win featured snippets for your targeted queries?</li>
<li>Do they have better content?</li>
<li>Do they have more and better links?</li>
<li>Do their pages load faster, and do they provide a better user experience overall?</li>
</ul>
<p>Gather your findings, and make a plan to win back those rankings!</p>
<section aria-label="Pro tip" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--protip-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Pro tip</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Note that not all backlink tools alert you in case a redirected domain is in fact no longer redirected. Keep a list of redirecting domains, and check from time to time whether they're still properly redirecting.</p>
</div></section>
<h2 id="lost-backlinks">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#lost-backlinks" tabindex="-1"></a>Step 13: Lost backlinks</h2>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-16" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style=""><img alt="Lost backlinks can have a big impact on your Google rankings" class="image" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/contentking-en-academy-backlinks-lost@1x%20(1).png" title="Lost backlinks can have a big impact on your Google rankings"/></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-16">Lost backlinks can have a big impact on your Google rankings</figcaption></figure>
<p>Since backlinks are still the most important ranking factor in SEO, losing backlinks that really packed a punch can have a huge impact on your rankings.</p>
<p>Check whether you've lost any backlinks, using an application such as Ahrefs, Majestic, Monitor Backlinks or Kerboo. Please note that there's always some delay before these applications pick up lost (or new) links, so take that into account.</p>
<p>Moving forward, it's recommended that you monitor your backlinks with one of the tools mentioned above so you get alerts when backlinks are lost. You can then reach out to the linking websites to see if you can get the backlinks restored.</p>
<h2 class="toc-hidden" id="look-into-this">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#look-into-this" tabindex="-1"></a>Came up empty? Look into this!</h2>
<p>You've gone through all the steps above, and you still came up empty. This too is part of SEO. It's not always straightforward to find out why your website's rankings have dropped.</p>
<h3 id="analyze-user-engagement">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#analyze-user-engagement" tabindex="-1"></a>Analyze user engagement</h3>
<p>Another area to look into to is the user engagement with your website. If users leave quickly because they're bombarded with pop-ups or ads, this will hurt your rankings. The same goes for not satisfying a user's search queries with your content. If they don't find what they were looking for, and they go back to the search result page to click another result, Google knows that your answer to their search query wasn't completely satisfactory.</p>
<p>Factors like this are playing an increasingly large role in Google's algorithms. You won't see these issues listed in Google Search Console, but they do impact your rankings.</p>
<p>Google has published an extensive document on this, called <a href="https://static.googleusercontent.com/media/www.google.com/cs//insidesearch/howsearchworks/assets/searchqualityevaluatorguidelines.pdf" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Search Quality Evaluator Guidelines</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>. It's quite complex, but it could be really useful for coming up with theories as to why your rankings have dropped.</p>
<h3 id="algorithmic-penalties">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#algorithmic-penalties" tabindex="-1"></a>Algorithmic penalties</h3>
<p>While they are rare, they do happen: algorithmic penalties â€” penalties that are given by algorithms without human intervention.</p>
<p>As you may know, Google heavily leans on machine learning to continously learn and improve their search results, which â€” if you don't play by the rules â€” may result in an algorithmic penalty. The ones we've seen in the wild are not reported in Google Search Console, and usually result in a demotion of a URL in Google's search results. This demotion lasts until you resolve the issue. Fixing it, and filing a <a href="https://support.google.com/webmasters/answer/35843?hl=en" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">reconsideration request</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> may speed up the re-evaluation process.</p>
<p>At this point, it makes sense to hire outside help as well, as it seems your ranking drop is an enigma. Hire a SEO consultant to puzzle it out.</p>

<h2 class="toc-hidden" id="frequently-asked-questions">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#frequently-asked-questions" tabindex="-1"></a>Frequently asked questions about Google Ranking Drops</h2>
<h3 id="-why-did-my-rankings-drop-in-google">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#-why-did-my-rankings-drop-in-google" tabindex="-1"></a>ðŸ¤· Why did my rankings drop in Google?</h3>
<p>Many factors can cause your rankings to drop in Google. Most frequently, we see drops because of changes that were made to the website, but they can also be caused by an algorithm update, technical issues, improvements competitors made, SERP lay-out changes or a Google penalty.</p>
<p>Whatever caused it, you'll quickly find it and recover your rankings using <a href="/academy/ranking-drop/#verify">the checklist</a> we included in this article!</p>
<h3 id="-can-it-be-my-rank-tracker-is-on-the-fritz">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#-can-it-be-my-rank-tracker-is-on-the-fritz" tabindex="-1"></a>ðŸ¤ª Can it be my rank tracker is on the fritz?</h3>
<p>Yes, that may very well be the case â€” rank trackers play a cat and mouse game with Google. Google doesn't want them scraping their search result pages, so they deploy countermeasures aimed to throw off rank trackers. On top of that, Google frequently rolls out SERP updates which can cause rank trackers to report the wrong information too.</p>
<h3 id="-can-serp-features-influence-rankings">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#-can-serp-features-influence-rankings" tabindex="-1"></a>ðŸ§¾ Can SERP features influence rankings?</h3>
<p>Yes, they sure can. Rank trackers interpret SERP features differently; some may for instance report a featured snippet at the top of the SERP as number 1, while others will count the first "regular" snippet as number 1.</p>
