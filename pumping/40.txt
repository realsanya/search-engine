<h1 class="heading__title">Protecting staging environments: the ultimate guide</h1>
<div class="heading__params">
<span aria-hidden="true" class="heading__label">
										Last updated:
									</span>
<span aria-label="The date of last update" class="heading__caption">
										October 12, 2021
									</span>
</div>
</div>
<div class="post-detail__text typo">
<section aria-label="Protecting staging environments in short" class="highlighted-box highlighted-box--with-background typo-block"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">
<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/protect-staging--reversed.svg)"></i></span>Protecting staging environments in short
	</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Prevent embarrassing situations by making sure you properly protect your staging environments.</p>
<p>The best way to go about this is using HTTP authentication.</p>
<p>Is your staging environment already indexed by search engines? No sweat. With this guide, you'll learn how to quickly and effectively reverse that.</p>
</div>
</section>
<div aria-label="Table of contents" class="highlighted-box" role="navigation">
<div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Table of contents</div>

<li class="navigation-list__item navigation-list__item--primary"><a href="#protect">How to protect your development and staging environments</a></li>

<p>We see it happen all the time: staging or development environments containing websites that are works in progress, left available for all the world to see. And often they're getting indexed by search engines too!</p>
<p>Don't believe me?</p>
<p>Check out <a href="https://www.google.com/search?q=site%3Astaging.*.com+OR+site%3Adev.*.com+OR+site%3Atest.*.com+OR+site%3Atesting.*.com&amp;btnK=Google+Search&amp;oq=site%3Astaging.*.com+OR+site%3Adev.*.com+OR+site%3Atest.*.com+OR+site%3Atesting.*.com" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">this query</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> – inspired by <a href="https://twitter.com/PeterNikolow/status/923496139105755136" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Peter Nikolow's tweet</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> (follow him on Twitter, he's both funny and smart!).</p>
<h2 id="why">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#why" tabindex="-1"></a>Why having accessible staging environments is a bad thing</h2>
<p>Or rather, a doubly-bad thing: bad from the viewpoints of both business and SEO.</p>
<h3 id="the-business-viewpoint">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#the-business-viewpoint" tabindex="-1"></a>The business viewpoint</h3>
<p>Do you want others to see your "lorem ipsum" content and laugh, or even—god forbid—read about a huge announcement such as an acquisition or rebranding that should have been kept secret until the new site was launched?</p>
<p>It's unprofessional, and above all it's not very smart. It's even a sales tactic for certain agencies: they look for other agencies that are making these mistakes and then pitch to their clients, leveraging the embarrassing situation.</p>
<div aria-label="Embedded quote from our customer" class="quote-box typo-block">
<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/dawn-anderson@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/dawn-anderson@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Dawn Anderson" class="image" height="100" src="/media/people/dawn-anderson@1x.png" title="Dawn Anderson" width="100"/></picture></span></span></div>
<div class="quote-box__content typo">
<blockquote class="quote-box__blockquote">
<p>(Indexed) staging URLs allow competitors to see future plans for development. They only need to follow a discovered development URL to access the whole staging site meaning your future web strategy / design is revealed. A simple search in Google for <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">site:staging.*</code> or <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">site:test.*</code> or any number of other common names which dev teams use as subdomains for their staging environments spits out a whole plethora of even well known brands staging environments which have not protected their environment sufficiently.</p>
<p>Plus, since staging environments are often - at best - a half-built product, when visitors stumble across them the user experience will be terrible and isn't likely to leave a good impression.</p>
</blockquote>
<div aria-label="The author of this quote" class="quote-box__info"><div class="user-info">
<span class="user-info__name">Dawn Anderson</span><span class="user-info__additional-info">, <a href="https://bertey.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Bertey</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></span>
</div></div>
</div>
</div>
<h3 id="the-seo-viewpoint">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#the-seo-viewpoint" tabindex="-1"></a>The SEO viewpoint</h3>
<p>Besides the embarrassment, having a staging environment that's indexed by search engines can lead to <a href="/academy/duplicate-content/">duplicate content issues</a> when the staging environment and the production environment are highly similar.</p>
<p>Having accessible and indexed staging environments is totally unnecessary, because it's easy to prevent. In this article we'll show you how to do it, what methods you can use, and what to do if your staging environment is already indexed.</p>
<p>From here forward, when we refer to <em>staging environments</em>, we may be referring to both development and staging environments.</p>
<div aria-label="Embedded quote from our customer" class="quote-box typo-block">
<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/barry-adams@1x.avif, /media/people/barry-adams@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/barry-adams@1x.webp, /media/people/barry-adams@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Barry Adams" class="image" height="100" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/barry-adams@1x.jpg" srcset="/media/people/barry-adams@1x.jpg, /media/people/barry-adams@2x.jpg 2x" title="Barry Adams" width="100"/></picture></span></span></div>
<div class="quote-box__content typo">
<blockquote class="quote-box__blockquote">
<p>When checking for duplicate content as part of a site audit, I regularly uncover staging sites that haven't been properly protected. Sometimes these are outdated staging sites from previous versions of a site, but once in a while I uncover unlaunched development sites that contain sensitive information that's not yet ready to be published. Unprotected staging environments aren't just SEO issues - they're business risks and should be handled with the appropriate care.</p>
</blockquote>
<div aria-label="The author of this quote" class="quote-box__info"><div class="user-info">
<span class="user-info__name">Barry Adams</span><span class="user-info__additional-info">, Independent SEO Consultant, <a href="https://www.polemicdigital.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Polemic Digital</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></span>
</div></div>
</div>
</div>
<div aria-label="Embedded quote from our customer" class="quote-box typo-block">
<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/kerstin-reichert@1x.avif, /media/people/kerstin-reichert@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/kerstin-reichert@1x.webp, /media/people/kerstin-reichert@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Kerstin Reichert" class="image" height="100" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/kerstin-reichert@1x.jpg" srcset="/media/people/kerstin-reichert@1x.jpg, /media/people/kerstin-reichert@2x.jpg 2x" title="Kerstin Reichert" width="100"/></picture></span></span></div>
<div class="quote-box__content typo">
<blockquote class="quote-box__blockquote">
<p>I have seen plenty of staging sites indexed. I think one of the issues is that indexing and crawling are not fully understood and that the business impact is often ignored.</p>
<p>Make sure to always password protect staging BEFORE you do anything else. Once indexed it is a real pain to remove those URLs in a timely manner. It might seem to be a good solution, but in the case of fully protecting your staging environment from bots and humans alike canonicals do not work, noindex doesn't work and neither does robots.txt as there are other ways humans, Google (and other search engines) can find your content.</p>
<p>Apart from the technical issues think about the business impact of products and services being discovered before launch or any other sensitive information. This could have very serious consequences.</p>
<p>If you do find your pages indexed think of an efficient way to remove them. Create lists to remove them in bulk and make sure search engines can "see" the noindex, so don't block crawlers through robots.txt.</p>
</blockquote>
<div aria-label="The author of this quote" class="quote-box__info"><div class="user-info">
<a class="user-info__social-icon social-icon social-icon--link social-icon--linkedin" href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/kerstinreichert/" rel="noopener" target="_blank" title="See my profile on LinkedIn"><span class="extlink">LinkedIn</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a><span class="user-info__name">Kerstin Reichert</span><span class="user-info__additional-info">, SEO and Content Lead, SeedLegals</span>
</div></div>
</div>
</div>
<div aria-label="Embedded quote from our customer" class="quote-box typo-block">
<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/sam-mcroberts@1x.avif, /media/people/sam-mcroberts@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/sam-mcroberts@1x.webp, /media/people/sam-mcroberts@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Sam McRoberts" class="image" height="100" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/sam-mcroberts@1x.jpg" srcset="/media/people/sam-mcroberts@1x.jpg, /media/people/sam-mcroberts@2x.jpg 2x" title="Sam McRoberts" width="100"/></picture></span></span></div>
<div class="quote-box__content typo">
<blockquote class="quote-box__blockquote">
<p>I routinely run into two problems on this front: self-hosted staging sites (such as WPEngine that automatically generates staging.domain.com), and staging sites on web developer's own domains (e.g. client.developer.com and developer.com/client/).</p>
<p>In the first scenario, you run the risk of having indexable duplicate content on your own domain, and just blocking a domain via robots.txt doesn't guarantee it won't get indexed, especially if you or others link to it (which is common, since links to dev site resources often find their way into the site code and get missed at launch).</p>
<p>In the second scenario, with a mirror of your site on a developer's domain, you run the risk of cross-domain duplicate content, and since the content was up on the developer's domain first, there's a risk Google may see you as the duplicator and them as the original source (not a good situation).</p>
<p>Always, always block bots from reaching your staging environments. Not just with a robots.txt file, but with something more secure (login/password, IP restricted access or both).</p>
<p>Second, always make sure your developers check all the code on your site at launch and remove any links to the staging environment. You want your internal links pointing to the live site, not somewhere else.</p>
<p>If you do both, you'll be in a good spot!</p>
</blockquote>

<h2 id="what-are">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-are" tabindex="-1"></a>What are development and staging environments?</h2>
<p>When you're working on a completely new website or new functionality, you don't just do that on your live website (often called your "production environment"), because websites are easy to break. It's a best practice to work with different, separate environments where you develop and test new functionality.</p>
<p>So what different environments are there besides a production environment?</p>
<ul>
<li>
<strong>Development environment:</strong> this is where developers initially test their code. Often they do this on their local machines, so if that's the case, there isn't any danger at all of this environment being accessible to others and getting indexed by search engines. If it's not kept locally, but instead for instance on a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">dev.example.com</code> subdomain, there is a risk of it being accessible to others and being indexed.</li>
<li>
<strong>Staging environment (often also called the "test environment"):</strong> this is where releases are staged and new functionality is tested before a release. New content is published here so it can be checked to ensure it looks as intended. Staging environments often aren't run locally: different team members need to be able to easily access it, and so it usually runs on a subdomain or a separate domain.</li>
</ul>
<section class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-background"><div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p><strong>Pro-tip:</strong> since you're reading up on protecting staging environments, it's likely you're going to be doing a website migration soon. To migrate flawlessly, check <a href="/academy/website-migrations/">our website migration guide</a> to make sure you aren't forgetting any crucial steps in the migration process!</p>
</div></section>
<h2 id="obscurity">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#obscurity" tabindex="-1"></a>Security through obscurity is not a feasible strategy</h2>
<p>Not telling anyone about your "secret" staging environment is a case of "<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Security_through_obscurity" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">security through obscurity</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>". It's not a feasible strategy to use. Especially not as the only layer of protection.</p>
<p>What if someone accidentally publishes a link to the staging environment? Or pushes some code to production that accidentally includes canonical or hreflang references to the staging environment?</p>
<p>Not only does this create issues in your production environment, it also leads to search engines picking up your staging environment's scent. And they will queue it up for crawling unless you make it impossible for them to access the staging environment, or give them rules of engagement to follow.</p>

<h3 id="method-1-http-auth">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#method-1-http-auth" tabindex="-1"></a>Method 1: HTTP authentication - your best choice 🏆</h3>
<p>The best way to prevent both users and search engines from gaining access to your development and staging environments is to use HTTP Authentication. Be sure, meanwhile, to implement it using HTTPS, because you don't want usernames and passwords to travel in plaintext over the wire.</p>
<p>We recommend whitelisting the IP addresses at your office, and providing external parties and remote team members access via a username/password combination.</p>
<p>This way search engines can't access anything, and you have total control over who can see what. You can prepare your staging environment with the same robots.txt that you'll be using on the production environment, as well as the correct robots directives and canonicals. This lets you gain a representative picture of your staging environment when you're monitoring it for issues and changes prior to launching.</p>
<p>Another benefit of this is that it's not prone to developers forgetting to publish the right robots.txt, robots directives, and canonicals on the production environment.</p>
<p>This is a much better approach than using robots.txt and/or robots noindex directives and canonical links, because those don't prevent other people from accessing them, and search engines will not always honor such directives.</p>
<p>What's more, when using HTTP authentication it's still possible to use Google's testing tools such as AMP, Mobile-friendliness, and Structured Data Testing Tool. Just <a href="https://developers.google.com/search/docs/guides/debug#testing-firewalled-pages" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">set up a tunnel</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>

<h3 id="method-1-vpn">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#method-1-vpn" tabindex="-1"></a>Method 2: VPN access</h3>
<p>VPN stands for "virtual private network." You basically connect your local machine so as to become part of the company network. And now that you're part of the company network, you can access the staging environment. Anyone who's not part of the network cannot access it. This means that neither third parties nor search engines can access the staging environment.</p>
<p>Having access through a VPN offers most of the benefits of HTTP authentication. However, there's one big drawback: SEO monitoring solutions that aren't running locally may not work out of the box, or at all. Not being able to track your development team's progress is troublesome, and it becomes truly problematic when you're dealing with truly big websites.</p>
<h3 id="method-3-robots-directives">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#method-3-robots-directives" tabindex="-1"></a>Method 3: Robots directives</h3>
<p><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/">Robots directives</a> are used to communicate preferences surrounding crawling and indexing. You can for instance ask search engines not to index certain pages, and not to follow (certain) links.</p>
<p>You can define robots directives in a page's HTTP header (<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">X-Robots-Tag header</code>), or via the meta robots directive in its <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;head&gt;</code> section. Because you'll have other content types besides just pages on your staging environment, it's recommended that you use the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">X-Robots-Tag header</code> to make sure that PDF files, for instance, don't get indexed.</p>
<p>Robots directives, like the name implies, are meant for robots ("crawlers"). They don't prevent 3rd party access. They do send a moderately strong signal to search engines not to index pages. I say "moderately" because search engines can still decide to ignore the robots directives and index your pages. It's also not a monitoring-friendly solution, as—similar to robots.txt—it may lead to false positives being reported by SEO tools.</p>
<p>On top of that, there's a huge risk of human error, as staging robots directives are often accidentally carried over into the production environment.</p>
<h3 id="method-4-robotstxt">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#method-4-robotstxt" tabindex="-1"></a>Method 4: Robots.txt</h3>
<p>The <a href="/academy/robotstxt/">robots.txt file</a> states the rules of engagement for crawlers, so by using robots.txt, you can ask search engines to keep out of your staging environment. The most common way to do this is by including the following contents in robots.txt:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: /</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>This prevents search engine crawlers from crawling the site, but they may <a href="/academy/robotstxt/#will-using-a-robots-txt-file-prevent-search-engines-from-showing-disallowed-pages-in-the-search-engine-result-pages">still index it if they find links to it</a>, leading to listings like these:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 593px;"><img alt="Google description not available robots.txt" class="image" height="82" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/snippet-in-google-excluded.png" title="Google description not available robots.txt" width="593"/></div>
<p>Some people include the unofficial <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Noindex</code> directive in their robots.txt. We don't recommend doing this, as it's a worse way to prevent your staging environment from being accessible than using a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code> directive, since it's really an unofficial directive.</p>
<p>Your robots.txt doesn't offer any actual protection against third-party access to the site, and it throws off SEO monitoring tools as well, potentially leading to false positives. Plus, you're creating a huge risk of human error: here once again, the robots.txt from the staging environment is often accidentally carried over into the production environment.</p>

<h3 id="method-5-canonical">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#method-5-canonical" tabindex="-1"></a>Method 5: Canonical links</h3>
<p>The <a href="/academy/canonical/">canonical link</a> informs search engines of the canonical version of a page. If the staging environment is referencing the production environment, all signals should be consolidated with the production environment.</p>
<p>Otherwise, canonical links resemble <a href="/academy/protect-staging-environment/#method-3-robots-directives">robots directives</a>, especially in their downsides:</p>
<ul>
<li>They still let third parties access the staging environment.</li>
<li>They're not a monitoring-friendly solution, as they may lead to false positives being reported by SEO tools.</li>
<li>There's a risk of human error, as canonical directives from staging are sometimes accidentally carried over into the production environment.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="method-6-whitelisting">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#method-6-whitelisting" tabindex="-1"></a>Method 6: Whitelisting specific user agents</h3>
<p>The whitelisting of specific user agents for access to a staging environment can be used to allow SEO specialists to monitor a staging environment, as long as their SEO tooling <a href="/support/user-agent-string/#changing-ua">supports setting custom user agents</a>. They could create a made-up user agent and use that, while blocking all other user agents (including browsers).</p>
<p>But this isn't a very user-friendly approach, because manual verification through your browser is made harder. It's not a very secure approach either: When third parties know you're working for or at company X, and they're aware of your user agent (perhaps because they're a disgruntled customer)—they may be able to gain access to the staging environment.</p>

<h2 id="find-out-indexed">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#find-out-indexed" tabindex="-1"></a>How can you find out if your staging environment is being indexed?</h2>
<p>Here are a few ways to find out if your staging environment is being indexed. Here are the two most common ones:</p>
<h3 id="option-1-site-query">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#option-1-site-query" tabindex="-1"></a>Option 1: site query</h3>
<p>If you know that your staging environment is running on a subdomain, you can try a site query such as: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">site:example.com -inurl:www</code></p>
<p>This query returns all the Google-indexed pages for the domain example.com except the ones containing "<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">www</code>".</p>
<p>Here's a link to an <a href="https://www.google.com/search?q=site%3Aexample.com+-inurl%3Awww" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">example query</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></p>
<h3 id="option-2-google-analytics">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#option-2-google-analytics" tabindex="-1"></a>Option 2: Google Analytics</h3>
<p>If you don't know the URL of your staging environment, you can try checking in Google Analytics:</p>
<ul>
<li>Navigate to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Audience</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Technology</code> and choose <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Network</code>.</li>
<li>Select <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Hostname</code> as the Primary Dimension.</li>
<li>Look for hostnames that have a different domain, or contain subdomains such as <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">staging</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">test</code> or <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">dev</code>.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="option-3-google-search-console">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#option-3-google-search-console" tabindex="-1"></a>Option 3: Google Search Console</h3>
<p>With the consolidation of properties in Google Search Console, it's now much easier to spot pages that shouldn't be indexed.</p>
<p>Whether your staging environment is set up a separate domain, a subdomain or subfolder: if you've verified the domain you'll be able to see all pages that are indexed, and all queries that your domain is ranking for. Right in the overviews you're used to look at;</p>
<ul>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Performance</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Queries</code>
</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Performance</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Pages</code>
</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Index</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Coverage</code>
</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="get-staging-removed">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#get-staging-removed" tabindex="-1"></a>Getting your already indexed staging environment removed from the index</h2>
<p>Uh-oh. Your staging environment has already been indexed by search engines, and you're the one who has to fix it. Well, the good news is: if you follow the steps below, you're good. And they're easy.</p>
<h3 id="step-1-hide-search-results">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#step-1-hide-search-results" tabindex="-1"></a>Step 1: hide search results</h3>
<p>Verify the staging environment in webmaster tools such as Google Search Console and Bing Webmaster Tools and URL Removal (see <a href="https://support.google.com/webmasters/answer/1663419?hl=en" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Google's documentation</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> and <a href="https://www.bing.com/webmaster/help/bing-content-removal-tool-cb6c294d" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Bing's documentation</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> on this). For Google, this request is often granted within hours (Bing takes a little longer), and then your staging environment won't show up in any search results. But here's the catch: it's still in Google's and Bing's indexes; it's just not shown. In Google's case, the staging environment is only hidden for 90 days. So within this timeframe, you need to make sure to request removal of your pages from search engines' indexes in the right way: via the <a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#noindex">robots noindex directive</a>.</p>
<h3 id="step-2-applying-the-noindex-directive-and-getting-pages-recrawled">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#step-2-applying-the-noindex-directive-and-getting-pages-recrawled" tabindex="-1"></a>Step 2: applying the noindex directive and getting pages recrawled</h3>
<p>Make sure you apply the robots noindex directive on every page in your staging environment. To speed up the process of search engines recrawling these pages, submit an XML sitemap. Now watch your server logs for search engine crawlers' requests for your previously indexed (and now "noindexed") pages, to make sure they've "gotten the message."</p>
<p>In most cases, these 90 days are enough time to signal to search engines that they should remove the staging-environment pages from their index. But if they aren't, just rinse and repeat.</p>
<p>Once it's all done, <a href="/academy/protect-staging-environment/#method-1-http-auth">protect the staging environment using HTTP authentication </a>to make sure this doesn't happen again and remove the XML sitemap from Google Search Console and Bing Webmaster Tools.</p>

<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/suganthan-mohanadasan@1x.avif, /media/people/suganthan-mohanadasan@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/suganthan-mohanadasan@1x.webp, /media/people/suganthan-mohanadasan@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Suganthan Mohanadasan" class="image" height="200" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/suganthan-mohanadasan@1x.jpg" srcset="/media/people/suganthan-mohanadasan@1x.jpg, /media/people/suganthan-mohanadasan@2x.jpg 2x" title="Suganthan Mohanadasan" width="200"/></picture></span></span></div>

<p>You can add a "noindex" tag to the indexed pages on the staging environment and submit a new sitemap via Google Search Console and Bing Webmaster Tools. The reason I recommend submitting the sitemap is to ensure search engines can see the "noindex tag". Once the search engines re-crawl the indexed pages on the staging environment, they should start removing these pages from its index. Once that's done you can get rid of the sitemap. You can go ahead and secure the staging by blocking search engines from re-crawling (and indexing). The best way to do this is to add HTTP authentication.</p>
