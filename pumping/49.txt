<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/shopify-seo--reversed.svg)"></i></span>Shopify SEO Guide in short

<p>Shopify, the plug-and-play eCommerce platform from Canada, has been growing at the rapid pace of around 86% year-over-year. Their <a href="https://news.shopify.com/shopify-announces-third-quarter-2021-financial-results" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Q3 2021 financial results</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> were nothing short of impressive too: their revenue grew to $1.1 billion (up 46% compared to Q3 20201 and their Gross Merchant Volume grew to $41.8 billion, (up 35% compared to Q3 2020).</p>
<p>And as we’re all aware, eCommerce spending has increased dramatically during the COVID pandemic, which Shopify has undoubtedly benefited from as they helped existing merchants sell more and helped new merchants start selling quickly. <a href="https://w3techs.com/blog/entry/shopify_is_now_the_second_most_popular_content_management_system" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">According to W3 Techs</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>, Shopify is now the second most popular CMS (WordPress is still number one).</p>
<p>Shopify is booming, and meanwhile its (frustrating) lack of flexibility has been a topic of discussion in the SEO community. SEO Specialist <a href="https://twitter.com/nickwilsdon" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Nick Wilsdon</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> has suggested on Twitter that Shopify should start focusing more on making the platform perform better in search, and their CEO <a href="https://twitter.com/tobi" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Tobi Lutke</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> noted that 9 out of 10 recommendations SEOs make regarding Shopify are snake oil:</p>

<h2 id="improve">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#improve" tabindex="-1"></a>How can you improve your Shopify SEO?</h2>
<p>Doing SEO on Shopify is very similar to doing SEO for any other platform. <strong>The same SEO best practices apply.</strong></p>
<p>In a nutshell, all SEO success comes down to:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<strong>Technology:</strong> making it easy for search engines to quickly find and easily understand your content.</li>
<li>
<strong>Relevance:</strong> creating content that satisfies your visitors, which – if done right – sends the correct signals to search engines so they can figure out what search queries your content needs to rank for.</li>
<li>
<strong>Authority &amp; Trust:</strong> gaining high-authority and relevant <a href="/academy/ecommerce-link-building/">links to your store</a>.</li>
</ol>
<p>So, what does it take to make your Shopify store rank well? Let’s dig in!</p>

<h3 id="a-quick-note-on-responsive-design-and-https">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#a-quick-note-on-responsive-design-and-https" tabindex="-1"></a>A quick note on responsive design and HTTPS</h3>
<p>We hardly need to explain the importance of responsive design and using an SSL certificate to serve visitors well. But they are important from a search engine standpoint too, as both HTTPS (<a href="https://webmasters.googleblog.com/2014/08/https-as-ranking-signal.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">source</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>) and mobile-friendliness (<a href="https://webmasters.googleblog.com/2015/02/finding-more-mobile-friendly-search.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">source</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>) have been Google ranking factors for years now.</p>
<p>Oh, and some good news: all the 1,000+ themes offered by Shopify itself are fully responsive, and every Shopify subscription level includes an SSL certificate. If you’d like to double check whether your theme is fully responsive, check out <a href="https://search.google.com/test/mobile-friendly" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Google’s mobile-friendliness test</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>
<h3 class="toc" id="url-structure">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#url-structure" tabindex="-1"></a>URL structure</h3>
<p>A clear URL structure helps both visitors and search engines in navigating your site.</p>
<p>A URL is generally considered good if it’s:</p>
<ul>
<li>descriptive and easy to read</li>
<li>brief</li>
<li>consistent</li>
<li>lowercase</li>
</ul>
<p>Shopify’s URL structure is rigid; there’s not much you can change about it. Only the <a href="/academy/urls/faq/slug/">slug</a> – the last part of a URL – can be modified.</p>
<p>Before we jump into the URL structure of the different content types, let’s first look at how people can reach your domain.</p>
<h4 id="domain-canonicalization">Domain canonicalization</h4>
<p>Domain canonicalization means picking one preferred way to reach your domain and explicitly communicating it. You want there to be no doubt about this, because you want to avoid duplicate content and dilution of your website’s link equity.</p>
<p>So it’s important to get this right. But you may be wondering why this is even an issue. Well, some websites choose not to use the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">www</code> subdomain and may still only be available through <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">HTTP</code> instead of <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">HTTPS</code>.</p>
<p>This means there are four ways of reaching a website’s homepage in theory:</p>
<ul>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">http://example.com</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">http://www.example.com</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com</code></li>
</ul>
<p>Additionally, your Shopify store is available under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">example.myshopify.com</code>.</p>
<p>Pick a preferred URL for your domain and then set up your Shopify store to only be available that way, by enabling redirection. This will result in all non-preferred URLs <a href="/academy/redirects/faq/301-redirect/">301 redirecting</a> to the preferred URL.</p>
<p>If you don’t have your domain connected, do so using <a href="https://help.shopify.com/en/manual/domains/add-a-domain/using-existing-domains/connecting-domains" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">this handy guide</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>. If you do already have it connected, follow the steps below.</p>
<section aria-label="Setting up domain canonicalization in Shopify" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--steps-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Setting up domain canonicalization in Shopify</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<ol>
<li>Log on to the Shopify admin console.</li>
<li>Go to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Online Store</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Domains</code>.</li>
<li>If it says there <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Traffic from your domains is not being redirected to this primary domain.</code>, you still need to enable redirection by clicking the blue link <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Enable redirection</code>.</li>
<li>A new screen titled <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Enable redirection?</code> will come up. Confirm redirection by clicking on the purple <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Enable redirection</code> button.</li>
<li>Verify if everything is correct by checking if you now see the following message under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Online Store</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Domains</code>: “Traffic from all your domains redirects to this primary domain”.</li>
</ol>

<p>Now, on to the URL structure for the different content types!</p>
<h4 id="collection-urls">Collection URLs</h4>
<p>In Shopify, product categories are called “collections”. By default, collections have the following URL structure: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/collections/$collectionName</code>. You can define the collection name yourself.</p>
<p>So, if you’ve got a collection called “T-shirts”, that collection’s URL becomes <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/collections/t-shirts</code>.</p>

<h4 id="tag-urls">Tag URLs</h4>
<p>Besides grouping products in collections, you can also tag related products. For instance, you may want to tag all T-shirts that have an US flag on them with the tag “USA flag”.</p>
<p>Now, you can look up all T-shirts with the US flag by going to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/collections/t-shirts/usa-flag</code>.</p>
<h4 id="product-urls">Product URLs</h4>
<p>Your product URLs have this URL structure: <br/>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/products/$productName</code></p>
<p>For example, a product named “Rocky Mountains T-shirt” will have this URL: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/products/rocky-mountains-t-shirt</code>.</p>
<p>Similarly as with collections, you can only change the URL slug – not the path leading up to it. Additionally, it’s not possible to include categorization in URLs, but that’s not going to have a negative impact SEO wise, because your internal link structure is leading – not your URL structure.</p>
<h5 id="product-available-under-a-different-url">Product available under a different URL</h5>
<p>Since we also created a collection for T-shirts, the Rocky Mountains product is available under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/collections/t-shirts/products/rocky-mountains-t-shirt</code>, which is canonicalized to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/products/rocky-mountains-t-shirt</code>.</p>
<p>This isn’t great, because you need to consistently send the right signals to search engines about what URLs they should crawl and index. Having them first crawl <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/collections/t-shirts/products/rocky-mountains-t-shirt</code>, and then canonicalize that URL to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/products/rocky-mountains-t-shirt</code> creates unnecessary overhead.</p>
<p>Plus, having products available through another URL may lead to backlinks being placed to the <em>wrong</em> URL — the one that’s canonicalized. This results in less <a href="/academy/authority/#link-authority">link authority</a> being transferred to the product page that needs to rank.</p>
<p><strong>How do you fix this?</strong><br/>
You can fix this by updating your theme’s code to directly reference the canonical URL, in this case <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/products/rocky-mountains-t-shirt</code>. You’ll thereby stop Shopify from linking to the product URL that’s canonicalized. This tweak is pretty straightforward, so if you’re comfortable with making code changes, you can do it yourself. Otherwise we recommend requesting help from someone in <a href="https://experts.shopify.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Shopify’s expert network</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>

<h5 id="product-variants">Product variants</h5>
<p>When it comes to products, Shopify has a second quirk: how they deal with product variants. Product variants are products that are available with slight differences, for example: shoes in different sizes and colors.</p>
<p>If you define product variants in Shopify, the platform will automatically append a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">?variant=$id</code> to the end of the URL so you can easily save the URL or share it on social media.</p>
<p>Example: our “Rocky Mountains T-shirt” is available in white and grey. The grey version is defined as a variant, and is available through <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/products/rocky-mountains-t-shirt?variant=123456789</code>.</p>
<p>From an SEO point of view there’s a drawback: the URL is canonicalized to the main product, meaning it won’t be indexed and ranked by search engines.</p>
<p>If visitors are specifically searching for your product variants, we highly recommend creating these products as <em>separate products</em> rather than <em>product variants</em>.</p>
<h5 id="redirect-from-collections-overview">Redirect from collections overview</h5>
<p>For some reason, on <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/collections/all</code> there’s a link to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/collections/all/products/rocky-mountains-t-shirt</code>. This URL 301 redirects to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/products/rocky-mountains-t-shirt</code>.</p>
<p>We highly recommend adjusting your theme to link directly from the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/collections/all</code> product overview to the right product URL, because this default behavior is highly inefficient from a <a href="/academy/control-crawl-indexing/">crawling and indexing</a> standpoint.</p>
<h4 id="page-urls">Page URLs</h4>
<p>Not all your store’s content comprises collections, products, and tags. You’ll probably want to add some “About Us” and “Frequently Asked Questions” content too.</p>
<p>For this content type, you’d use Pages, which have the following URL structure: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/pages/$pageName</code>.</p>
<p>For example, a page named “About Us” will have this URL: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/pages/about-us</code>.</p>
<h4 id="blog-urls">Blog article URLs</h4>
<p>By default, even if you don’t have any blog articles yet, Shopify will create a blog overview page for you at <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/blogs/news</code>.</p>
<p>Your blog article URLs have the following URL structure: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/blogs/news/$articleName</code>.</p>
<p>For example, a blog article named “Just launched” will have the following URL: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/blogs/news/just-launched</code>.</p>

<h4 id="what-we-think-about-shopifys-url-structure">What we think about Shopify’s URL structure</h4>

<h3 class="toc" id="manage-redirects">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#manage-redirects" tabindex="-1"></a>Managing URL redirects</h3>
<p>When you’re changing URLs, Shopify will ask if you want to automatically create a 301 redirect. If your store is already live, we highly recommend you use this option. Every redirect that’s added this way can be managed in the redirect manager.</p>
<p>This redirect manager also lets you import and export redirects – a useful feature when you’re migrating to or from Shopify.</p>
<p>This feature has a big drawback though: it does not empower you to set up redirects using Regular Expressions (RegEx), and so you have to either fill in all redirects manually or get creative and generate them yourself and then import them. The lack of support for RegEx may result in your having to define thousands of redirects, when you’d have otherwise just defined them with a few lines of RegEx in your web server configuration (like you would on an Apache web server in the <a href="/academy/redirects/faq/htaccess/">.htaccess file</a>). Shopify's support team confirmed they support up to 100,000 redirects, so in case you need to implement more redirects you'll have to prioritize them.</p>

<h3 class="toc" id="meta-information">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#meta-information" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta information</h3>
<p>When we talk about meta information within the SEO field, we mean the <strong>page title</strong> and <strong>meta description</strong>. Both play an essential role in how well your pages rank and how they are presented to searchers.</p>
<p>The illustration below highlights their role in what your snippet will look like:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.png"/><img alt="Screenshot of a search engine result snippet from Google, with the title, URL and description highlighted." class="image" height="158" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@2x.png 2x" title="Screenshot of a search engine result snippet from Google, with the title, URL and description highlighted." width="600"/></picture></div>
<p>A good title is easy to read, contains important keywords at its start, has a call to action, is unique, and has a length between <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">285</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">575</code> pixels (<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">30</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">60</code> characters respectively).</p>
<p>The same applies for a good meta description – except that the preferred length is between <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">430</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">920</code> pixels (<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">70</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">155</code> characters respectively).</p>
<p>Below we’ll describe how Shopify handles meta information out of the box for every content type.</p>
<h4 id="meta-information-collections">Collections</h4>
<p>Shopify generates the following title and meta description for collections:</p>
<ul>
<li>Title: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$collectionName - $shopName</code>, e.g. <br/>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Heavy Duty t-shirts - Millennial T-shirts</code>
</li>
<li>Meta description: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$collectionDescription</code>, e.g. <br/>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Beautiful, heavy duty t-shirts for every occasion.</code>
</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="meta-information-tags">Tags</h4>
<p>Shopify doesn’t offer a central place to manage tags, and so titles and meta descriptions can’t be customized. It automatically generates:</p>
<ul>
<li>Title: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$collectionName - Tagged: $tagName - $shopName</code>, e.g. <br/>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Heavy Duty t-shirts - Tagged: “usa flag” - Millennial T-shirts</code>
</li>
<li>Meta description: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$collectionDescription</code>, e.g. <br/>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Beautiful, heavy duty t-shirts for every occasion.</code> – please note that this is the exact same description as for the collection.</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="meta-information-products">Products</h4>
<p>Shopify generates the following titles and meta descriptions for products:</p>
<ul>
<li>Title: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$productName - $shopName</code>, e.g. <br/>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Rocky Mountains t-shirt - Millennial T-shirts</code>
</li>
<li>Meta description: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$productDescription</code>, e.g. <br/>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Always wanted to take the beautiful Rocky Mountains with you everywhere you go? Now you can!</code>
</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="meta-information-pages-blogs">Pages and blog articles</h4>
<p>Shopify generates the titles and meta descriptions for pages and blog articles in the same way:</p>
<ul>
<li>Title: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$pageName - $shopName</code>, e.g. <br/>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">About us - Millennial T-shirts</code>
</li>
<li>Meta description: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$pageDescription</code>, e.g. <br/>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">What’s the idea behind the Millennial T-shirt store? We’re glad you asked….</code>
</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="meta-description-length-cut-off-at-320-characters-by-shopify">Meta description length cut off at 320 characters by Shopify</h4>
<p>For collections, products, pages, and blog articles, their description is used by default as their meta description. If that description is longer than <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">320</code> characters, it’ll be cut off at that length.</p>
<p>Google usually won’t show a snippet with a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">320</code>-character description; it will rewrite or truncate it instead.</p>
<h4 id="defining-title-and-meta-description-templates">Defining title and meta description templates</h4>
<p>Being able to define title and meta description via templates rather than manually for every product is a huge time saver.</p>
<p>Shopify doesn’t support this out of the box, but apps enabling it do exist, and we highly recommend using one.</p>
<p>For example, you could define the following templates for each collections:</p>
<ul>
<li>Title: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$collectionName</code>
</li>
<li>Meta description: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Looking for $collectionName? Check out our massive selection at $shopName!</code>
</li>
</ul>
<p>And for products:</p>
<ul>
<li>Title: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$productName</code>
</li>
<li>Meta description: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Looking for a $productName from $vendor? Don’t like any further, order it now from $minimumPrice!</code>
</li>
</ul>
<p>We recommend using <a href="https://apps.shopify.com/smart-seo" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Smart SEO app</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> or <a href="https://apps.shopify.com/seo-meta-manager" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">SEO Manager</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> to handle this efficiently.</p>
<h4 id="but-what-about-adding-meta-keywords-you-ask">But what about adding meta keywords, you ask?</h4>
<p>We thought we’d include a section on meta keywords, because we’ve found a lot of people search for queries like <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">meta keywords shopify</code>. Meta keywords haven’t been used by Google and Bing for over 10 years. Don’t bother with them, unless you’re targeting Yandex – they still do assign some weight to the meta keyword.</p>
<p>Instead of focusing on meta keywords, optimize pages for relevant keywords by including them in the title, meta description, headings, and body content (for example the collection description and product description).</p>
<h4 id="what-we-think-about-shopifys-meta-information-management">What we think about Shopify’s meta information management</h4>
<p>While it would have been great for Shopify to have native functionality to define meta information templates, you can add this functionality using an app. Once again, not a show stopper for SEO success.</p>

<h3 class="toc" id="headings">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#headings" tabindex="-1"></a>Headings</h3>
<p>Headings help visitors and search engines to quickly scan what a page is about and how it’s structured.</p>
<p>Stick to these best practices when it comes to headings:</p>
<ul>
<li>Use only one <a href="/academy/headings/faq/h1/">H1 heading</a> per page.</li>
<li>Don’t wrap logos or images in H1 heading tags (as the H1 heading conveys a page’s main topic). This is a frequent mistake.</li>
<li>For readability, stick to a maximum heading length of <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">60</code> characters.</li>
<li>Don’t use headings for elements that are present on every page, such as search functionality.</li>
<li>Don’t apply H2 headings to product names on collection pages.</li>
<li>Build up your heading structure using a logical hierarchy, as illustrated below:</li>
</ul>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-headings-en@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-headings-en@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.png"/><img alt="Illustration of a correct heading structure" class="image" height="355" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-headings-en@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-headings-en@2x.png 2x" title="Illustration of a correct heading structure" width="600"/></picture></div>
<p>To a large degree, your Shopify theme determines which text is placed within your headings. For the H1 heading, this is what you’ll see in most themes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Home page: the store name</li>
<li>Collection pages: the collection name</li>
<li>Product pages: the product name</li>
<li>Blog article: the blog article name</li>
<li>Pages: the page name</li>
</ul>
<p>When pages allow for additional content, you can mark these up with H2–H6 headings as you please – but stick to best practices!</p>

<h4 id="faceted-navigation">Faceted navigation and product filters</h4>
<p>Faceted navigation refers to how visitors can navigate your product offering by filtering and sorting. The topic of faceted navigation is one of the toughest eCommerce SEO subjects for people to grasp and implement well.</p>
<p>Let’s take the “T-shirts for Kids” collection as an example. Say this collection contains 300 T-shirts. You can filter these T-shirts by color, size, sex, and theme. You’ll want <strong>some</strong> of these filtered products to be indexable by search engines because you know people actively seek them. You’ll want to keep search engines from crawling and indexing the other filtered product pages to prevent <a href="/academy/duplicate-content/">duplicate content</a> and <a href="/academy/crawl-budget/">crawl budget</a> issues.</p>
<p>Ideally you’d make selected facets accessible to search engines and keep the rest inaccessible. That’s not possible on Shopify however. We thus recommend creating <strong>collections</strong> to group the products people search for. By default, these are accessible.</p>
<div aria-label="Embedded quote from our customer" class="quote-box typo-block">
<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/fiona-soum@1x.avif, /media/people/fiona-soum@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/fiona-soum@1x.webp, /media/people/fiona-soum@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Fiona Soum" class="image" height="200" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/fiona-soum@1x.jpg" srcset="/media/people/fiona-soum@1x.jpg, /media/people/fiona-soum@2x.jpg 2x" title="Fiona Soum" width="200"/></picture></span></span></div>

<blockquote class="quote-box__blockquote">
<p>Implementing multi-faceted navigation for your Shopify store can be challenging. The platform uses a basic filtering functionality that doesn't give much customisation options and an improper faceted navigation set-up could be leading to a waste of crawl budget.</p>
<p>I previously encountered an issue with multiple facets selection leading to static URLs. Whilst these pages were not indexed (a disallow rule was in place in the robots.txt file), crawlers were still able to find them through internal links from the filter feature automatically set as "follow".</p>
<p>This led to high amount of pages being crawled. And to add to the issue, multiple versions of pages with the same filters were created. E.g.</p>
<ul>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/collections/[product]/[facet1]+[facet2]+[facet3]</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/collections/[product]/[facet2]+[facet1]+[facet3]</code></li>

Instead, we set links within the same filter group as <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">nofollow</code> when a first filter has been selected, added a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex meta tag</code> to extraneous pages that filtered the same set of facets and added a rule to enforce an order on facets.</p>

<h4 id="what-we-think-about-shopifys-site-structure">What we think about Shopify’s site structure</h4>
<p>While Shopify provides some control over its site structure, it’s not great. If you’re a more advanced SEO, you’ll often want more control and easy customization. There’s definitely room for improvement for Shopify here if they want to appeal to advanced SEO specialists.</p>
<h3 class="toc" id="crawling-indexing">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#crawling-indexing" tabindex="-1"></a>Crawling &amp; indexing directives</h3>
<p>Crawling and indexing directives help search engines understand what content you want them to index and bring up in the search results. It’s essential you send unequivocal, consistent signals to search engines. There are various ways to do this, which we’ll describe in this section.</p>
<h4 id="meta-robots-tag">Meta robots tags</h4>
<p>The most common meta robots tags tell search engines what pages not to index (<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive) and what links shouldn’t be followed (<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">nofollow</code> directive). Out of the box, Shopify doesn’t provide functionality to define these directives.</p>
<p>For instance, if you’ve set up some specific landing pages for PPC that contain highly similar content, you don’t want search engines to index these. Alternatively, maybe you’ve duplicated a collection and made some slight changes. This works great from a commercial point of view, as visitors may find this very useful in their online journey, but these collections add no value from an SEO perspective.</p>
<p>On Shopify, there are two ways to make sure search engines won’t be indexed:</p>
<ol>
<li>The less scalable and more error-prone way: by making code changes, as explained <a href="https://help.shopify.com/en/manual/promoting-marketing/seo/hide-a-page-from-search-engines" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">in Shopify’s documentation</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</li>
<li>By using an app that adds a setting for this within Shopify’s admin console.</li>
</ol>
<p>We highly recommend going for option two, as that’s the most user-friendly way of managing this.</p>

<h4 id="canonicals">Canonicals</h4>
<p>When there are multiple versions of a page (or highly similar versions), canonical URLs can be used to tell search engines they need to consider only one version for ranking purposes. Search engines will then assign the relevance and authority from the other versions to the canonical version.</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 1200px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/contentking-en-academy-canonicalpage@1x.png"/><img alt="Use of canonical link illustrated" class="image" height="570" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/contentking-en-academy-canonicalpage@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/contentking-en-academy-canonicalpage@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2016/05/contentking-en-academy-canonicalpage@2x.png 2x" title="Use of canonical link illustrated" width="1200"/></picture></div>
<p>For URLs that contain parameters, Shopify always includes a self-referencing canonical to the URL without the parameters. So for instance, if someone posts a link to your collection page on Facebook, visitors coming to your site will land on a URL like <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/collections/t-shirts?fbclid=XXXXXX</code>. No need to worry about duplicate content, because of the self-referencing canonical that points to the URL variant without <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">?fbclid=XXXXXX</code>, namely: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/collections/t-shirts</code>.</p>
<p>Some other situations where Shopify uses the canonical URL:</p>
<ul>
<li>To prevent duplicate content because products are available <a href="/academy/shopify-seo/#product-available-under-a-different-url">under different URLs</a>
</li>
<li>When <a href="/academy/shopify-seo/#product-variants">product variants are used</a>
</li>
<li>For a series of paginated pages (this depends on your theme though; see the next section on pagination).</li>
</ul>
<p>There are no options within the Shopify admin console to adjust canonicalization preferences, but you can of course adjust the theme’s code, as we’ve seen with products that are available under different URLs.</p>

<h4 id="pagination">Pagination attributes</h4>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">rel="next"</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">rel="prev"</code> pagination attributes signal to search engines that there’s content spread across a series of pages.</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 1200px;"><img alt="Pagination for a Shopify store" class="image" height="686" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/contentking-en-academy-pagination@2x.png" title="Pagination for a Shopify store" width="1200"/></div>
<p>Although Google’s John Mueller <a href="https://twitter.com/JohnMu/status/1108717486424363009" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">said on March 21, 2019</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> that they haven’t been using the pagination attributes for a while, these attributes <strong>are still useful</strong> because:</p>
<ul>
<li>They help Google discover content.</li>
<li>Browsers use them for prefetching purposes (to speed up loading of the next page).</li>
<li>Bing still uses the pagination attributes to help them understand that pages are part of a series.</li>
<li>They are useful from an accessibility standpoint.</li>
</ul>
<p>Most Shopify themes support the pagination attributes out of the box, so in most cases you won’t have to take action here. If your theme doesn’t support these attributes but canonicalizes the paginated pages to the first page instead, then we’d recommend assigning low priority to implementing the pagination attributes, because chances are you’re not going to get a good ROI on the investment of adding them.</p>

<blockquote class="quote-box__blockquote">
<p>Even if you don’t use <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">rel="next"</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">rel="prev"</code> for your pagination, make sure that your paginated URLs have canonicals pointing to themselves, especially if you have many products or your stock is dynamic.</p>
<p>The problem with canonicalizing all the URLs in a paginated sequence to the first page is that you "cut off" Page 2, Page 3, Page N, they might just as well not exist for Google. But these pages are technically not duplicates, so Google crawling and indexing them is not an issue. They perform a very important task for you - support a healthy internal linking structure so that Google can follow all the internal links found on them, index/re-index products and properly see the relations between the internal pages.</p>
</blockquote>

<h4 id="robotstxt">Robots.txt</h4>
<p>A robots.txt file contains the rules of engagement for crawlers, telling them what they can and can’t access. If there is no robots.txt file or if it’s left empty, crawlers will do as they please.</p>
<p>Here is the first part of the robots.txt, which impacts search engine behaviour:</p>

Disallow: /$uniqueValue/checkouts
Disallow: /$uniqueValue/orders

Disallow: /blogs/*%2b*

Disallow: /*/blogs/*%2b*
Disallow: /*design_theme_id*

Disallow: /policies/
Disallow: /search
Disallow: /apple-app-site-association

Sitemap: https://example.com/sitemap.xml</code></pre></figure>

<p>Shopify comes with a default robots.txt that’s the same for every store on their platform. It’s set up to prevent access to account, checkout, and cart pages, internal search result pages, and a few other pages that may lead to duplicate content issues.</p>
<p>It also references the XML sitemap, so it’s easy-to-find for search engines and team members.</p>
<p>For a lot of sites, this out-of-the-box setup is good. For more advanced users who are, for instance, looking to leverage faceted navigation, it can prove quite limiting.</p>
<h5 id="workarounds-to-edit-your-robotstxt">Workarounds to edit your robots.txt</h5>
<p>What are your options (besides moving to a different platform) if you really need to edit robots.txt?</p>
<ol>
<li>Use Cloudflare Workers – read more on that in <a href="/academy/shopify-seo/#cloudflare-workers">the Cloudflare workers section</a>.</li>
<li>We’d only recommend doing this <strong>as a last resort</strong>, but you can redirect your robots.txt file to another domain that you control. There you can adjust the robots.txt as you please.</li>
</ol>

<h4 id="xml-sitemaps">XML Sitemaps</h4>
<p>XML Sitemaps are special documents used as an efficient way to communicate to search engines what content on a website should be crawled and indexed. Search engines will look for XML sitemaps within the robots.txt file, but it’s always recommended to also <a href="https://help.shopify.com/en/manual/promoting-marketing/seo/find-site-map#submit-your-sitemap-to-google-search-console" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">submit them to Google Search Console</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> and <a href="https://www.bing.com/webmaster/help/how-to-verify-ownership-of-your-site-afcfefc6" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Bing Webmaster Tools</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>
<p>Shopify creates these automatically. On our test environment, we have <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://contentkingtest.myshopify.com/sitemap.xml</code> – the XML sitemap index that lists the other XML sitemaps:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://contentkingtest.myshopify.com/sitemap_blogs_1.xml</code> listing the blog overview page and any blog articles. If you don’t have any articles, only the overview page is listed.</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://contentkingtest.myshopify.com/sitemap_collections_1.xml</code>, which doesn’t contain any collections (just the front page) if you don’t have any.</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://contentkingtest.myshopify.com/sitemap_products_1.xml</code>, which contains the home page as well as all products with their images.</li>
</ul>
<p>There are two caveats here for advanced users:</p>
<ol>
<li>There are no separate XML sitemaps for images and videos. Check out the <a href="/academy/shopify-seo/#images-and-xml-sitemaps">Images and XML sitemaps section</a> on how to fix this.</li>
<li>There is no way to change the XML sitemaps, or their content.</li>
</ol>

<h3 class="toc" id="image-optimization">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#image-optimization" tabindex="-1"></a>Image optimization</h3>
<p>Especially for eCommerce, optimizing your images for organic search is important. You’d be surprised how many people are visual thinkers – and searchers. They’ll type in a query into Google, switch to images search, and their buyer journey begins.</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopify-seo-images-search-en@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopify-seo-images-search-en@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-shopify-seo-images-search-en@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopify-seo-images-search-en@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopify-seo-images-search-en@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-shopify-seo-images-search-en@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopify-seo-images-search-en@1x.png"/><img alt="Google Images search as start of customer journey" class="image" height="343" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-shopify-seo-images-search-en@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopify-seo-images-search-en@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-shopify-seo-images-search-en@2x.png 2x" title="Google Images search as start of customer journey" width="600"/></picture></div>
<p>To make sure your images load fast and search engines can easily “understand” what your images are about, apply these best practices:</p>
<ol>
<li>Serve images through a CDN – Shopify does this out-of-the box.</li>
<li>Include images in your XML sitemap – there are some caveats, described below.</li>
<li>Compress images – Shopify doesn’t do this out-of-the box.</li>
<li>Use a descriptive file name and <a href="/academy/image-seo/#image-formats">the right image file type for the job</a> and resize images to the dimensions required – you can do all this yourself before uploading.</li>
<li>Define the image alt attribute and have relevant text close to the image – you can do this within the Shopify admin console.</li>
</ol>
<h4 id="images-and-xml-sitemaps">Images and XML sitemaps</h4>
<p>Shopify automatically includes images in your product XML sitemap so that search engines can find and index your images. There are two drawbacks though:</p>
<ol>
<li>By including the images in your product XML sitemap, it becomes more difficult to track how many of your images have been indexed in, for instance, Google Search Console. Ideally you’d have a dedicated XML sitemap just for your images.</li>
<li>Out of the box, Shopify only includes one image for each product in the XML sitemap, hurting discovery and indexing of its other image(s).</li>
</ol>
<p><a href="https://apps.shopify.com/avada-seo-suite" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">The Image Sitemap app</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> fixes these shortcomings.</p>
<p>There are Shopify apps that automatically compress images and generate image alt attributes. We recommend checking these ones out:</p>
<ul>

</ul>

But for the more ambitious store owner and SEO, you’ll want to gain more control over the image optimization process and make it more efficient all around – which you can do with the apps listed in this section. In summary, on Shopify you can perform all the image optimization you need to.</p>

<h3 class="toc" id="structured-data">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#structured-data" tabindex="-1"></a>Structured Data</h3>
<p><a href="/academy/schema/">Structured data</a> can be described as data that’s organized in a certain predefined format. Common examples of structured data are Schema, Open Graph, and Twitter Cards.</p>
<p>Schema.org is structured markup that helps search engines understand your content, and it often renders enhanced snippets that will make you stand out in search engine result pages.</p>
<p>Implementing Schema in Shopify</p>
<p>Depending on the theme you’re using, you may have out-of-the-box support for Schema markup because it’s embedded in your theme. As long as you’ve filled in all your company information and product information, this will be handled automatically.</p>
<p>If the Schema markup is not present out of the box, you can either have a Shopify expert add it to your theme or use an app for this. Using an app is usually the best way to go, because it allows for more granular control over what markup is added to your pages.</p>
<p>Useful Schema types for stores:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://developers.google.com/search/docs/data-types/product" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Product</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
<li><a href="https://developers.google.com/search/docs/data-types/review-snippet" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Review</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
<li><a href="https://developers.google.com/search/docs/data-types/article" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Articles</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
<li><a href="https://developers.google.com/search/docs/data-types/local-business" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">LocalBusiness</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
</ul>
<p>Here is an example plugin that adds JSON-LD markup: <a href="https://apps.shopify.com/seo-meta-manager" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">SEO Meta Manager</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>

<h4 id="og-tc">Implementing Open Graph and Twitter Cards</h4>
<p>Open Graph and Twitter Cards are forms of structured data too. They let you control your snippets when your URLs are shared on social media. If your snippets look appealing, you’re going to win big in this channel.</p>
<h5 id="homepage">Homepage</h5>
<p>For your Open Graph and Twitter Card data, shopify re-uses what’s been filled in for your <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">homepage title</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">homepage meta description</code>.</p>
<section aria-label="Defining default Open Graph and Twitter Card image" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--steps-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Defining default Open Graph and Twitter Card image</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<ol>
<li>Log on to the Shopify admin console</li>
<li>Go to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Online Store</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Preferences</code>
</li>
<li>Upload your image in the section <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Social sharing image</code>

<h3 class="toc" id="page-speed">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#page-speed" tabindex="-1"></a>Page speed</h3>
<p>Shopify is a hosted service that runs on a massive infrastructure, including a powerful CDN and it uses caching heavily. That’s great: you’ve got the basics covered!</p>
<p>While Shopify is constantly working on <a href="https://engineering.shopify.com/blogs/engineering/how-shopify-reduced-storefront-response-times-rewrite" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">improving storefront response times</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>, —like with any platform— if you clutter up your theme, upload oversized images, and install numerous Shopify apps, you can still grind your store to a halt.</p>
<p>Recommendations:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<strong>Theme:</strong> if you have the budget, use a custom theme and build it from scratch so you know what’s gone into it. If you can’t afford that, choose the most lightweight theme you can find. While all themes you can choose from on Shopify are responsive, there is no quality-control when it comes to page speed.</li>
<li>
<strong>Apps:</strong> if you can avoid using apps, then by all means do. Apps are one of the most common reasons why Shopify stores load slowly. For example, adding code snippets into the page source is much better than going through an app, as apps only add to the number of dependencies the store must load.</li>
<li>
<strong>Image optimization:</strong> as we’ve already described in <a href="/academy/shopify-seo/#image-optimization">the image optimization section</a>, sticking to basic best practices goes a long way. Additionally, <a href="https://www.shopify.com/partners/blog/lazy-loading" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">implement lazy loading</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> for your images.</li>
<li>
<strong>Tracking:</strong> move all tracking scripts to Google Tag Manager to reduce the number of requests that pages make.</li>
</ul>

<h3 class="toc" id="javascript">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#javascript" tabindex="-1"></a>Client-side JavaScript</h3>
<p>Although search engines can now render JavaScript, it’s recommended that you use server-side rendering or a prerendering solution so that you can serve regular HTML to search engines to make their crawling and indexing process as smooth and quick as possible. When your website has search engines’ attention, you need to make the most of it!</p>
<p>Rendering javascript takes up a lot of resources, so search engines only allocate a relatively low amount of resources to it. Because of this, don’t be surprised if search engines need weeks if not months (in the case of 1,000+ products) to fully crawl and index your store.</p>

<h2 id="cloudflare-workers">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#cloudflare-workers" tabindex="-1"></a>Cloudflare Workers to make up for Shopify’s shortcomings</h2>
<p>In several of the sections above we’ve mentioned that Cloudflare Workers can make up for some of Shopify’s shortcomings. For those unfamiliar with Cloudflare Workers, they’re a service provided by Cloudflare that is positioned in between Shopify (the server) and visitors and search engines (clients), allowing you to modify what content clients receive.</p>
<p>This sounds very technical, so let’s look at a few examples of what you can do with Cloudflare Workers:</p>
<ul>
<li>Adjust the robots.txt, so that you can allow selected crawl paths only and make your faceted navigation better accessible to search engines.</li>
<li>Take full control of the other crawling and indexing directives, such as for example canonical URLs, robots directives, hreflang, and pagination attributes.</li>
<li>Adjust the XML sitemap, so that you can include hreflang and canonical URLs.</li>
<li>Collect log files, to get a better understanding of how search engines crawl your Shopify store. This is done through request chain filters at a website level, so whilst they are the same as server log files, they don’t <em>really</em> come from the server.</li>
<li>Rewrite your URL structure, if you want to get rid of the <br/>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/products</code>/, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/collections</code>/, or <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/pages/</code> folders for instance. This however can add latency and potential complications, so we wouldn’t advise this unless you really need to change URL structures.</li>
<li>Modify HTTP headers, so you can return <a href="/academy/http-status-codes/#410">HTTP status code 410</a> for example.</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="added-complexity-and-costs">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#added-complexity-and-costs" tabindex="-1"></a>Added complexity and costs</h3>
<p>While you gain much more control over your Shopify platform using Cloudflare Workers, it does add to the complexity of your store. If something goes wrong, where did that happen? Was it an app, a theme issue, a user issue, or incorrectly configured Cloudflare Workers?</p>
<p>Because it’s more complex, you may need a developer to configure Cloudflare Workers — or alternatively work with <a href="https://sloth.cloud/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Sloth</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>, which enables you to make changes without coding. If you do choose to use Sloth or another Edge SEO tool, it’s important to consultant your development teams and align with processes were possible to mitigate and avoid risks.</p>
<p>If you’ve come so far that you need Cloudflare Workers to get control over parts of your store that you wouldn’t be able to control otherwise, Shopify may actually no longer be the right platform for you. We recommend looking into other platforms such as WooCommerce, Magento, and ShopWare. They may be a better fit for the requirements you have right now.</p>
<p>All three of them let you be successful at SEO, and we’ve even written detailed guides on <a href="/academy/magento-seo/">Magento SEO</a> and <a href="/academy/shopware-seo/">ShopWare SEO</a>.</p>
<section aria-label="Pro tip" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--protip-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Pro tip</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>It’s also worth noting that at present, Cloudflare Workers can only be enabled by your CF account manager if you are on an Enterprise plan, which is expensive.
Enabling Cloudflare Workers will also change your stack configuration, so this decision needs to be made in conjunction with your systems and infrastructure teams.</p>
</div></section>
<h3 id="future-opportunities">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#future-opportunities" tabindex="-1"></a>Future opportunities</h3>
<p>Other CDNs including Akamai and Fastly have also upped their game in the JavaScript workers space. Akamai EdgeWorkers are fast becoming a capable edge solution to rival the capabilities of Cloudflare Workers, and could soon offer the same benefits to Shopify store owners.</p>
<p>Mohammed Aboul-Magd, Director of Product at Akamai, explained more on Akamai EdgeWorkers in <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K2HOeoky2Bk" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">this conference talk</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>
<p>Fastly’s WASM solution is also developing and is currently capable of the same edge changes that Cloudflare Workers is. This was confirmed to me by Fastly’s Sara Lasseter in October 2019.</p>

<h2 class="toc-hidden" id="in-page-faq">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#in-page-faq" tabindex="-1"></a>Frequently asked questions about Shopify SEO</h2>
<h3 id="️-is-shopify-good-for-seo">⚖️ Is Shopify good for SEO?</h3>
<p>Yes, but – as with any platform – you’ll have to make adjustments so the platform works like you need. Out-of-the box, without any theme customization or apps, more advanced SEOs will miss important functionality.</p>
<h3 id="-how-do-i-adjust-my-robotstxt">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#-how-do-i-adjust-my-robotstxt" tabindex="-1"></a>🤖 How do I adjust my robots.txt?</h3>
<p>Unfortunately for more advanced SEOs, Shopify <a href="/academy/shopify-seo/#robotstxt">doesn’t support adjusting your robots.txt</a>. There is a workaround though; you can use Cloudflare Workers to make adjustments to your robots.txt.</p>
<h3 id="️-are-shopify-stores-optimized-for-speed">🏎️ Are Shopify stores optimized for speed?</h3>
<p>The Shopify platform itself is, but you’ll need to keep your theme clean, optimize your images, and keep apps to a minimum to make sure your store loads quickly.</p>
<h3 id="-how-do-i-add-seo-to-shopify">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#-how-do-i-add-seo-to-shopify" tabindex="-1"></a>🔧 How do I add SEO to Shopify?</h3>
<p>SEO isn’t just a plugin you add to Shopify. If you want to drive organic traffic to your website, you need to have an SEO strategy in place. In this article, we do describe how to get the Shopify platform in good shape from a technical standpoint; after that, you’ll need to create relevant content and put it in all the right places, and of course: gain links from other sites to your store.</p>
