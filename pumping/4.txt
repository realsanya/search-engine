<h1 class="heading__title">Robots.txt for SEO: The Ultimate Guide</h1>
<div class="heading__params">
<span aria-hidden="true" class="heading__label">
										Last updated:
									</span>
<span aria-label="The date of last update" class="heading__caption">
										October 12, 2021
									</span>
</div>
</div>
<div class="post-detail__text typo">
<section aria-label="Robots.txt in short" class="highlighted-box highlighted-box--with-background typo-block"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">
<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/robots-txt--reversed.svg)"></i></span>Robots.txt in short
	</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>A robots.txt file contains directives for search engines. You can use it to prevent search engines from crawling specific parts of your website and to give search engines helpful tips on how they can best crawl your website. The robots.txt file plays a big role in SEO.</p>
<p>When implementing robots.txt, keep the following best practices in mind:</p>
<ul>
<li>Be careful when making changes to your robots.txt: this file has the potential to make big parts of your website inaccessible for search engines.</li>
<li>The robots.txt file should reside in the root of your website (e.g. <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">http://www.example.com/robots.txt</code>).</li>
<li>The robots.txt file is only valid for the full domain it resides on, including the protocol (<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">http</code> or <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https</code>).</li>
<li>Different search engines interpret directives differently. By default, the first matching directive always wins. But, with Google and Bing, specificity wins.</li>
<li>Avoid using the crawl-delay directive for search engines as much as possible.</li>
</ul>
</div>
</section>

<h2 id="what-is-a-robots-txt-file">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-is-a-robots-txt-file" tabindex="-1"></a>What is a robots.txt file?</h2>
<p>A robots.txt file tells search engines what your website's rules of engagement are. A big part of doing SEO is about sending the right signals to search engines, and the robots.txt is one of the ways to communicate your crawling preferences to search engines.</p>
<p>In 2019, we've seen quite some developments around the robots.txt standard: <a href="/blog/implications-of-new-robots-txt-rfc/">Google proposed</a> an extension to the Robots Exclusion Protocol and <a href="/blog/googles-robots-txt-interpretation-flexible/">open-sourced its robots.txt parser</a>.</p>
<p>TL;DR</p>
<ul>
<li>Google's robots.txt interpreter is quite flexible and surprisingly forgiving.</li>
<li>In case of confusion directives, Google errs on the safe sides and assumes sections should be restricted rather than unrestricted.</li>
</ul>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-1" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/contentking-en-academy-robotstxt-entering@1x.png"/><img alt="The robots.txt file tells search engines what URLs not to access." class="image" height="401" src="/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/contentking-en-academy-robotstxt-entering@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2018/04/contentking-en-academy-robotstxt-entering@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2018/04/contentking-en-academy-robotstxt-entering@2x.png 2x" title="The robots.txt file tells search engines what URLs not to access." width="600"/></picture></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-1">The robots.txt file tells search engines what URLs not to access.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Search engines regularly check a website's robots.txt file to see if there are any instructions for crawling the website. We call these instructions <em>directives</em>.</p>
<p>If there's no robots.txt file present or if there are no applicable directives, search engines will crawl the entire website.</p>
<p>Although all major search engines respect the robots.txt file, search engines may choose to ignore (parts of) your robots.txt file. While directives in the robots.txt file are a strong signal to search engines, it's important to remember the robots.txt file is a set of optional directives to search engines rather than a mandate.</p>

<h3 id="synonyms-for-robots-txt">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#synonyms-for-robots-txt" tabindex="-1"></a>Terminology around robots.txt file</h3>
<p>The robots.txt file is the implementation of the <strong>robots exclusion standard</strong>, or also called the <strong>robots exclusion protocol</strong>.</p>
<h2 id="why-should-you-care-about-robots-txt">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#why-should-you-care-about-robots-txt" tabindex="-1"></a>Why should you care about robots.txt?</h2>
<p>The robots.txt plays an essential role from a SEO point of view. It tells search engines how they can best crawl your website.</p>
<p>Using the robots.txt file you can <mark>prevent search engines from accessing certain parts of your website</mark>, <mark>prevent duplicate content</mark> and give search engines helpful tips on how they can <mark>crawl your website more efficiently</mark>.</p>
<p><strong>Be careful</strong> when making changes to your robots.txt though: this file has the potential to make big parts of your website inaccessible for search engines.</p>
<div aria-label="Embedded quote from our customer" class="quote-box typo-block">
<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/gerry-white@1x.avif, /media/people/gerry-white@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/gerry-white@1x.webp, /media/people/gerry-white@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Gerry White" class="image" height="100" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/gerry-white@1x.jpg" srcset="/media/people/gerry-white@1x.jpg, /media/people/gerry-white@2x.jpg 2x" title="Gerry White" width="100"/></picture></span></span></div>
<div class="quote-box__content typo">
<blockquote class="quote-box__blockquote">
<p>Robots.txt is often over used to reduce duplicate content, thereby killing internal linking so be really careful with it. My advice is to only ever use it for files or pages that search engines should never see, or can significantly impact crawling by being allowed into. Common examples: log-in areas that generate many different urls, test areas or where multiple facetted navigation can exist. And make sure to monitor your robots.txt file for any issues or changes.</p>
</blockquote>

<p>The mass majority of issues I see with robots.txt files fall into three buckets:</p>
<ol>
<li>The mishandling of wildcards. It's fairly common to see parts of the site blocked off that were intended to be blocked off. Sometimes, if you aren't careful, directives can also conflict with one another.</li>
<li>Someone, such as a developer, has made a change out of the blue (often when pushing new code) and has inadvertently altered the robots.txt without your knowledge.</li>
<li>The inclusion of directives that don't belong in a robots.txt file. Robots.txt is web standard, and is somewhat limited. I oftentimes see developers making directives up that simply won't work (at least for the mass majority of crawlers). Sometimes that's harmless, sometimes not so much.</li>

<h3 id="example">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#example" tabindex="-1"></a>Example</h3>
<p>Let's look at an example to illustrate this:</p>
<p>You're running an eCommerce website and visitors can use a filter to quickly search through your products. This filter generates pages which basically show the same content as other pages do. This works great for users, but confuses search engines because it creates <a href="/academy/duplicate-content/">duplicate content</a>.</p>
<p>You don't want search engines to index these filtered pages and waste their valuable time on these URLs with filtered content. Therefor, you should set up <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code> rules so search engines don't access these filtered product pages.</p>
<p>Preventing duplicate content can also be done using the <a href="/academy/canonical/">canonical URL</a> or the meta robots tag, however these don't address letting search engines only crawl pages that matter.</p>
<p>Using a canonical URL or meta robots tag will not prevent search engines from crawling these pages. It will only prevent search engines from showing these pages in the search results. Since search engines have <a href="/academy/crawl-budget/">limited time to crawl a website</a>, this time should be spend on pages that you want to appear in search engines.</p>

<h2 id="what-does-a-robots-txt-file-look-like">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-does-a-robots-txt-file-look-like" tabindex="-1"></a>What does a robots.txt file look like?</h2>
<p>An example of what a simple robots.txt file for a WordPress website may look like:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: /wp-admin/</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>Let's explain the anatomy of a robots.txt file based on the example above:</p>
<ul>
<li>User-agent: the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">user-agent</code> indicates for which search engines the directives that follow are meant.</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">*</code>: this indicates that the directives are meant for all search engines.</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code>: this is a directive indicating what content is not accessible to the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">user-agent</code>.</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/wp-admin/</code>: this is the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">path</code> which is inaccessible for the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">user-agent</code>.</li>
</ul>
<p>In summary: this robots.txt file tells all search engines to stay out of the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/wp-admin/</code> directory.</p>
<p>Let's analyze the different components of robots.txt files in more detail:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#user-agent">User-agent</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#disallow">Disallow</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#allow">Allow</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#sitemap">Sitemap</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#crawl-delay">Crawl-delay</a></li>
</ul>
<h3 id="user-agent">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#user-agent" tabindex="-1"></a>User-agent in robots.txt</h3>
<p>Each search engine should identify themself with a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">user-agent</code>. Google's robots identify as <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Googlebot</code> for example, Yahoo's robots as <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Slurp</code> and Bing's robot as <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">BingBot</code> and so on.</p>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">user-agent</code> record defines the start of a group of directives. All directives in between the first <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">user-agent</code> and the next <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">user-agent</code> record are treated as directives for the first <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">user-agent</code>.</p>
<p>Directives can apply to specific user-agents, but they can also be applicable to all user-agents. In that case, a wildcard is used: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">User-agent: *</code>.</p>
<h3 id="disallow">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#disallow" tabindex="-1"></a>Disallow directive in robots.txt</h3>
<p>You can tell search engines not to access certain files, pages or sections of your website. This is done using the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code> directive. The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code> directive is followed by the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">path</code> that should not be accessed. If no <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">path</code> is defined, the directive is ignored.</p>
<h4 id="disallow-directive-in-robots-txt-example">Example</h4>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: /wp-admin/</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>In this example all search engines are told not to access the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/wp-admin/</code> directory.</p>
<h3 id="allow">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#allow" tabindex="-1"></a>Allow directive in robots.txt</h3>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Allow</code> directive is used to counteract a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code> directive. The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Allow</code> directive is supported by Google and Bing. Using the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Allow</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code> directives together you can tell search engines they can access a specific file or page within a directory that's otherwise disallowed. The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Allow</code> directive is followed by the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">path</code> that can be accessed. If no <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">path</code> is defined, the directive is ignored.</p>
<h4 id="allow-directive-in-robots-txt-example">Example</h4>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Allow: /media/terms-and-conditions.pdf
Disallow: /media/</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>In the example above all search engines are not allowed to access the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/media/</code> directory, except for the file <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/media/terms-and-conditions.pdf</code>.</p>
<p>Important: when using <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Allow</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code> directives together, be sure not to use wildcards since this may lead to conflicting directives.</p>
<h4 id="example-of-conflicting-directives">Example of conflicting directives</h4>
<section aria-label="Bad example of code" class="code-box code-box--negative"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Allow: /directory
Disallow: *.html</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>Search engines will not know what to do with the URL <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">http://www.domain.com/directory.html</code>. It's unclear to them whether they're allowed to access. When directives aren't clear to Google, they will go with the least restrictive directive, which in this case means that they would in fact access <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">http://www.domain.com/directory.html</code>.</p>

<h3 id="separate-line-for-each-directive">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#separate-line-for-each-directive" tabindex="-1"></a>A separate line for each directive</h3>
<p>Each directive should be on a separate line, otherwise search engines may get confused when parsing the robots.txt file.</p>
<p><strong>Example of incorrect robots.txt file</strong></p>

<section aria-label="Bad example of code" class="code-box code-box--negative"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: * Disallow: /directory-1/ Disallow: /directory-2/ Disallow: /directory-3/</code></pre></figure>

<h3 id="using-wildcard">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#using-wildcard" tabindex="-1"></a>Using wildcard *</h3>
<p>Not only can the wildcard be used for defining the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">user-agent</code>, it can also be used to match URLs. The wildcard is supported by Google, Bing, Yahoo and Ask.</p>
<h4 id="wildcard-example">Example</h4>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: *?</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>In the example above all search engines aren't allowed access to URLs which include a question mark (<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">?</code>).</p>

<h3 id="using-end-of-url-dollar">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#using-end-of-url-dollar" tabindex="-1"></a>Using end of URL $</h3>
<p>To indicate the end of a URL, you can use the dollar sign (<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$</code>) at the end of the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">path</code>.</p>
<h4 id="end-of-url-example">Example</h4>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: *.php$</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>In the example above search engines aren't allowed to access all URLs which end with .php. URLs with parameters, e.g. <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/page.php?lang=en</code> would not be disallowed, as the URL doesn't end after <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">.php</code>.</p>
<h3 id="sitemap">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#sitemap" tabindex="-1"></a>Add sitemap to robots.txt</h3>
<p>Even though the robots.txt file was invented to tell search engines what pages <strong>not to crawl</strong>, the robots.txt file can also be used to point search engines to the <a href="/academy/xml-sitemap/">XML sitemap</a>. This is supported by Google, Bing, Yahoo and Ask.</p>
<p>The XML sitemap should be referenced as an absolute URL. The URL does <strong>not</strong> have to be on the same host as the robots.txt file.</p>
<p>Referencing the XML sitemap in the robots.txt file is one of the best practices we advise you to always do, even though you may have already submitted your XML sitemap in Google Search Console or Bing Webmaster Tools. Remember, there are more search engines out there.</p>
<p>Please note that it's possible to reference multiple XML sitemaps in a robots.txt file.</p>
<h4 id="sitemap-examples">Examples</h4>
<p>Multiple XML sitemaps defined in a robots.txt file:</p>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: /wp-admin/
Sitemap: https://www.example.com/sitemap1.xml
Sitemap: https://www.example.com/sitemap2.xml</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>The example above tells all search engines not to access the directory <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/wp-admin/</code> and that there are two XML sitemaps which can be found at <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/sitemap1.xml</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/sitemap2.xml</code>.</p>
<p>A single XML sitemap defined in a robots.txt file:</p>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: /wp-admin/

Sitemap: https://www.example.com/sitemap_index.xml</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>The example above tells all search engines not to access the directory <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/wp-admin/</code> and that the XML sitemap can be found at <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/sitemap_index.xml</code>.</p>

<h3 id="comments">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#comments" tabindex="-1"></a>Comments</h3>
<p>Comments are preceded by a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">#</code> and can either be placed at the start of a line or after a directive on the same line. Everything after the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">#</code> will be ignored. These comments are meant for humans only.</p>
<h4 id="example-1">Example 1</h4>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">

User-agent: *
Disallow: /wp-admin/</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>

<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">

Disallow: /wp-admin/ # Don't allow access to the /wp-admin/ directory.</code></pre></figure>

<h3 id="crawl-delay">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#crawl-delay" tabindex="-1"></a>Crawl-delay in robots.txt</h3>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl-delay</code> directive is an unofficial directive used to prevent overloading servers with too many requests. If search engines are able to overload a server, adding <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl-delay</code> to your robots.txt file is only a temporary fix. The fact of the matter is, your website is running on a poor hosting environment and/or your website is incorrectly configured, and you should fix that as soon as possible.</p>

<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-2">Example robots.txt with specified crawl-delay for Bing</figcaption></figure>
<p>The way search engines handle the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl-delay</code> differs. Below we explain how major search engines handle it.</p>
<section aria-label="Frequently Asked Questions" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-background highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--faq-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Frequently Asked Questions</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<ul>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/faq/crawl-delay-10/">What does crawl-delay: 10 mean in robots.txt?</a></li>
</ul>
</div></section>
<p><strong>Crawl-delay and Google</strong></p>
<p>Google's crawler, Googlebot, does not support the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl-delay</code> directive, so don't bother with defining a Google crawl-delay.</p>
<p>However, Google does support defining a crawl rate (or "request rate" if you will) in Google Search Console.</p>
<section aria-label="Setting crawl rate in GSC" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--steps-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Setting crawl rate in GSC</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<ol>
<li>Log onto the old <a href="https://www.google.com/webmasters/tools/settings" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Google Search Console</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</li>
<li>Choose the website you want to define the crawl rate for.</li>
<li>There's only one setting you can tweak: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl rate</code>, with a slider where you can set the preferred crawl rate. By default the crawl rate is set to "Let Google optimize for my site (recommended)".</li>
</ol>
</div></section>
<p>This is what that looks like in Google Search Console:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-robots-txt-gsc-crawl-rate@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-robots-txt-gsc-crawl-rate@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-robots-txt-gsc-crawl-rate@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-robots-txt-gsc-crawl-rate@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-robots-txt-gsc-crawl-rate@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-robots-txt-gsc-crawl-rate@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-robots-txt-gsc-crawl-rate@1x.png"/><img alt="Define Crawl Rate in Google Search Console" class="image" height="292" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-robots-txt-gsc-crawl-rate@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-robots-txt-gsc-crawl-rate@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-robots-txt-gsc-crawl-rate@2x.png 2x" title="Define Crawl Rate in Google Search Console" width="600"/></picture></div>
<p><strong>Crawl-delay and Bing, Yahoo and Yandex</strong></p>
<p>Bing, Yahoo and Yandex all support the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl-delay</code> directive to throttle crawling of a website. Their interpretation of the crawl-delay is slightly different though, so be sure to check their documentation:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://blogs.bing.com/webmaster/2012/05/03/to-crawl-or-not-to-crawl-that-is-bingbots-question/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Bing and Yahoo</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
<li><a href="https://yandex.com/support/webmaster/controlling-robot/robots-txt.xml#crawl-delay" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Yandex</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
</ul>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl-delay</code> directive should be placed right after the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code> or <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Allow</code> directives.</p>
<h4 id="crawl-delay-example">Example:</h4>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: BingBot
Disallow: /private/
Crawl-delay: 10</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p><strong>Crawl-delay and Baidu</strong></p>
<p>Baidu does not support the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">crawl-delay</code> directive, however it's possible to register a Baidu Webmaster Tools account in which you can control the crawl frequency, similar to Google Search Console.</p>
<h2 class="toc-hidden" id="when-to-use-a-robots-txt">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#when-to-use-a-robots-txt" tabindex="-1"></a>When to use a robots.txt file?</h2>
<p>We recommend <strong>to always use</strong> a robots.txt file. There's absolutely no harm in having one, and it's a great place to hand search engines directives on how they can best crawl your website.</p>

<h2 id="best-practices">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#best-practices" tabindex="-1"></a>Robots.txt best practices</h2>
<p>The robots.txt best practices are categorized as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#location-and-filename">Location and filename</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#order-of-precedence">Order of precedence</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#only-one-group-per-robot">Only one group of directives per robot</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#be-as-specific-as-possible">Be as specific as possible</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#specifying-directives-for-all-bots-while-including-specific-directives-for-a-specific-bot">Directives for all robots while also including directives for a specific robot</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#robots-txt-file-for-each-subdomain">Robots.txt file for each (sub)domain.</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#conflicting-guidelines-robots-txt-vs-google-search-console">Conflicting guidelines: robots.txt vs. Google Search Console</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#check-robots-txt-after-launch">Monitor your robots.txt file</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#dont-use-noindex-in-your-robots-txt">Don't use noindex in your robots.txt</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/robotstxt/#utf-8-bom">Prevent UTF-8 BOM in robots.txt file</a></li>
</ul>
<h3 id="location-and-filename">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#location-and-filename" tabindex="-1"></a>Location and filename</h3>
<p>The robots.txt file should always be placed in the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">root</code> of a website (in the top-level directory of the host) and carry the filename <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">robots.txt</code>, for example: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/robots.txt</code>. Note that the URL for the robots.txt file is, like any other URL, case-sensitive.</p>
<p>If the robots.txt file cannot be found in the default location, search engines will assume there are no directives and crawl away on your website.</p>
<h3 id="order-of-precedence">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#order-of-precedence" tabindex="-1"></a>Order of precedence</h3>
<p>It's important to note that search engines handle robots.txt files differently. By default, the <strong>first matching directive always wins</strong>.</p>
<p>However, with Google and Bing <strong>specificity wins</strong>. For example: an <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Allow</code> directive wins over a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code> directive if its character length is longer.</p>
<h4 id="order-of-precedence-example">Example</h4>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Allow: /about/company/
Disallow: /about/</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>In the example above all search engines, including Google and Bing are not allowed to access the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/about/</code> directory, except for the sub-directory <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/about/company/</code>.</p>
<h4 id="order-of-precedence-example-2">Example</h4>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: /about/
Allow: /about/company/</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>In the example above, all search engines <strong>except for Google and Bing</strong>, aren't allowed access to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/about/</code> directory. That includes the directory <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/about/company/</code>.</p>
<p>Google and Bing <strong>are allowed access</strong>, because the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Allow</code> directive <strong>is longer</strong> than the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code> directive.</p>
<h3 id="only-one-group-per-robot">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#only-one-group-per-robot" tabindex="-1"></a>Only one group of directives per robot</h3>
<p>You can only define one group of directives per search engine. Having multiple groups of directives for one search engine confuses them.</p>
<h3 id="be-as-specific-as-possible">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#be-as-specific-as-possible" tabindex="-1"></a>Be as specific as possible</h3>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code> directive triggers on partial matches as well. Be as specific as possible when defining the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow</code> directive to prevent unintentionally disallowing access to files.</p>
<h4 id="be-specific-example">Example:</h4>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: /directory</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>The example above doesn't allow search engines access to:</p>
<ul>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/directory</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/directory/</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/directory-name-1</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/directory-name.html</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/directory-name.php</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/directory-name.pdf</code></li>
</ul>
<h3 id="specifying-directives-for-all-bots-while-including-specific-directives-for-a-specific-bot">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#specifying-directives-for-all-bots-while-including-specific-directives-for-a-specific-bot" tabindex="-1"></a>Directives for all robots while also including directives for a specific robot</h3>
<p>For a robot only one group of directives is valid. In case directives meant for all robots are followed with directives for a specific robot, only these specific directives will be taken into considering. For the specific robot to also follow the directives for all robots, you need to repeat these directives for the specific robot.</p>
<p>Let's look at an example which will make this clear:</p>
<h4 id="user-agent-example">Example</h4>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: /secret/
Disallow: /test/
Disallow: /not-launched-yet/

User-agent: googlebot
Disallow: /not-launched-yet/</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>In the example above all search engines <strong>except for Google</strong> are not allowed to access <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/secret/</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/test/</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/not-launched-yet/</code>. Google only isn't allowed access to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/not-launched-yet/</code>, but is allowed access to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/secret/</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/test/</code>.</p>
<p>If you don't want googlebot to access <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/secret/</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/not-launched-yet/</code> then you need to repeat these directives for <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">googlebot</code> specifically:</p>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: /secret/
Disallow: /test/
Disallow: /not-launched-yet/

User-agent: googlebot
Disallow: /secret/
Disallow: /not-launched-yet/</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>Please note that your robots.txt file is publicly available. Disallowing website sections in there can be used as an attack vector by people with malicious intent.</p>

<h3 id="robots-txt-file-for-each-subdomain">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#robots-txt-file-for-each-subdomain" tabindex="-1"></a>Robots.txt file for each (sub)domain</h3>
<p>Robots.txt directives only apply to the (sub)domain the file is hosted on.</p>
<h4 id="robots-txt-subdomain-examples">Examples</h4>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">http://example.com/robots.txt</code> is valid for <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">http://example.com</code>, but not for <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">http://www.example.com</code> or <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com</code>.</p>
<p>It's a best practice to only have one robots.txt file available on your (sub)domain.</p>
<p>If you have multiple robots.txt files available, be sure to either make sure they return a <a href="/academy/http-status-codes/#404">HTTP status 404</a>, or to <a href="/academy/redirects/faq/301-redirect/">301 redirect</a> them to the canonical robots.txt file.</p>
<h3 id="conflicting-guidelines-robots-txt-vs-google-search-console">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#conflicting-guidelines-robots-txt-vs-google-search-console" tabindex="-1"></a>Conflicting guidelines: robots.txt vs. Google Search Console</h3>
<p>In case your robots.txt file is conflicting with settings defined in Google Search Console, Google often chooses to use the settings defined in Google Search Console over the directives defined in the robots.txt file.</p>
<h3 id="check-robots-txt-after-launch">

<h3 id="dont-use-noindex-in-your-robots-txt">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#dont-use-noindex-in-your-robots-txt" tabindex="-1"></a>Don't use noindex in your robots.txt</h3>
<p>For years, Google was already <a href="https://twitter.com/JohnMu/status/638644112359604224" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">openly recommending against using the unofficial noindex directive</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>. As of September 1, 2019 however, Google stopped <a href="https://webmasters.googleblog.com/2019/07/a-note-on-unsupported-rules-in-robotstxt.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">supporting it entirely</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>
<p>The unofficial noindex directive never worked in Bing, as confirmed by FrÃ©dÃ©ric Dubut in <a href="https://twitter.com/CoperniX/status/1146085846845554688" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">this tweet</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>:</p>

<h3 id="utf-8-bom">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#utf-8-bom" tabindex="-1"></a>Prevent UTF-8 BOM in robots.txt file</h3>
<p>BOM stands for <em>byte order mark</em>, an invisible character at the beginning of a file used to indicate Unicode encoding of a text file.</p>
<p>While Google <a href="https://developers.google.com/search/reference/robots_txt#file-format" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">states</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> they ignore the optional Unicode byte order mark at the beginning of the robots.txt file, we recommend preventing the "UTF-8 BOM" because we've seen it cause issues with the interpretation of the robots.txt file by search engines.</p>
<p>Even though Google says they can deal with it, here are two reasons to prevent the UTF-8 BOM:</p>
<ol>
<li>You don't want there to be any ambiguity about your preferences around crawling to search engines.</li>
<li>There are other search engines out there, which may not be as forgiving as Google claims to be.</li>
</ol>

<h3 id="wordpress">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#wordpress" tabindex="-1"></a>Robots.txt file for WordPress</h3>
<p>The robots.txt file below is specifically optimized for WordPress, assuming:</p>
<ul>
<li>You don't want to have your admin section to be crawled.</li>
<li>You don't want to have your internal search result pages crawled.</li>
<li>You don't want to have your tag and author pages crawled.</li>
<li>You don't want your 404 page to be crawled.</li>
</ul>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: /wp-admin/ #block access to admin section
Disallow: /wp-login.php #block access to admin section
Disallow: /search/ #block access to internal search result pages
Disallow: *?s=* #block access to internal search result pages
Disallow: *?p=* #block access to pages for which permalinks fails
Disallow: *&amp;p=* #block access to pages for which permalinks fails
Disallow: *&amp;preview=* #block access to preview pages
Disallow: /tag/ #block access to tag pages
Disallow: /author/ #block access to author pages
Disallow: /404-error/ #block access to 404 page

Sitemap: https://www.example.com/sitemap_index.xml</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>Please note that this robots.txt file will work in most cases, but you should <strong>always</strong> adjust it and test it to make sure it applies to your exact situation.</p>
<h3 id="magento">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#magento" tabindex="-1"></a>Robots.txt file for Magento</h3>

<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: /catalogsearch/
Disallow: /search/
Disallow: /customer/account/login/
Disallow: /*?SID=
Disallow: /*?PHPSESSID=
Disallow: /*?price=
Disallow: /*&amp;price=
Disallow: /*?color=
Disallow: /*&amp;color=
Disallow: /*?material=
Disallow: /*&amp;material=
Disallow: /*?size=
Disallow: /*&amp;size=

Sitemap: https://www.example.com/sitemap_index.xml</code></pre></figure>

<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-are-the-limitations-of-robots-txt" tabindex="-1"></a>What are the limitations of the robots.txt file?</h2>

<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#robots-txt-file-contains-directives" tabindex="-1"></a>Robots.txt file contains directives</h3>
<p>Even though the robots.txt is well respected by search engines, it's still a directive and not a mandate.</p>

<h3 id="caching">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#caching" tabindex="-1"></a>Robots.txt file is cached up to 24 hours</h3>
<p>Google has indicated that a robots.txt file is generally cached for up to 24 hours. It's important to take this into consideration when you make changes in your robots.txt file.</p>
<p>It's unclear how other search engines deal with caching of robots.txt, but in general it's best to avoid caching your robots.txt file to avoid search engines taking longer than necessary to be able to pick up on changes.</p>
<h3 id="file-size">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#file-size" tabindex="-1"></a>Robots.txt file size</h3>
<p>For robots.txt files Google currently supports a file size limit of 500 kibibytes (512 kilobytes). Any content after this maximum file size <em>may be</em> ignored.</p>
<p>It's unclear whether other search engines have a maximum filesize for robots.txt files.</p>
<h2 class="toc-hidden" id="frequently-asked-questions">

<h3 id="faq-example">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#faq-example" tabindex="-1"></a>ðŸ¤– What does a robots.txt example look like?</h3>
<p>Here's an example of a robots.txt's content: User-agent: * Disallow: . This tell all crawlers they can access everything.</p>
<h3 id="faq-disallow-all">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#faq-disallow-all" tabindex="-1"></a>â›” What does Disallow all do in robots.txt?</h3>
<p>When you set a robots.txt to "Disallow all", you're essentially telling all crawlers to keep out. No crawlers, including Google, are allowed access to your site. This means they won't be able to crawl, index and rank your site. This will lead to a massive drop in organic traffic.</p>
<h3 id="faq-allow-all">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#faq-allow-all" tabindex="-1"></a>âœ… What does Allow all do in robots.txt?</h3>
<p>When you set a robots.txt to "Allow all", you tell every crawler they can access every URL on the site. There are simply no rules of enagement. Please note that this is the equivalent of having an empty robots.txt, or having no robots.txt at all.</p>
<h3 id="faq-importance-seo">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#faq-importance-seo" tabindex="-1"></a>ðŸ¤” How important is the robots.txt for SEO?</h3>
<p>In general, the robots.txt file is very important for SEO purposes. For larger websites the robots.txt is essential to give search engines very clear instructions on what content not to access.</p>

To achieve this, we use technical cookies to i.e. show you our Website, to make it function correctly, to create your ContentKing account, to sign you in and to manage your requests. These technical cookies are necessary for our website to function properly.</p>
<h3 id="functional-cookies">Functional cookies</h3>
<p>We also use functional cookies to remember your preferences and settings (as username, password, language etc.) and to help you to use the Website efficiently and effectively. These functional cookies are not strictly necessary for the functioning of our Website, but they add functionality for you and enhance your experience.</p>
<h3 id="analytical-cookies">Analytical cookies</h3>
<p>We use analytical cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. These analytic cookies generate statistical and other information about website use by means of cookies, which are stored on usersâ€™ Devices. The information generated relating to our Website is used to create reports about the use of the Website. Analytical cookies may be placed without your consent, unless such cookies have an impact on your privacy. In such cases, prior consent will be asked for.</p>
<h3 id="google-analytics">Google Analytics</h3>
<p>We use Google Analytics cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. With these analytical cookies a permanent cookie is saved on your device to register your use of the Website. Google Analytics analyses this data and provides us with the results. This way, we obtain insight into the traffic of the Website and the way in which the Website is used. Based on this information we are able to make specific adjustments to the Website.</p>
<p>The information that we obtain is transferred to Google and stored by Google on servers outside the European Economic Area. We have entered into a data processing agreement with Google in which agreements have been made regarding the processing of personal data.</p>
<p>If you would like more information about analytical cookies and their expiry date, please visit the Google Analytics <a href="https://developers.google.com/analytics/devguides/collection/gajs/cookie-usage">information page</a>. Google also offers an <a href="https://tools.google.com/dlpage/gaoptout?hl=en">opt-out option</a> for data collection within the scope of Google Analytics.</p>
<h3 id="marketing-cookies">Marketing cookies</h3>
<p>We use third party marketing cookies in order to follow your internet browsing behavior and collect data and information on your browsing behavior from various websites you visited. This information is used to make the content of the displayed advertisements as relevant as possible, and to limit repetition of the same advertisements.</p>
<h3 id="social-plug-ins">Social plug-ins</h3>
<p>By using the Website you can get access to social media websites, such as Facebook, Twitter and LinkedIn. These buttons come with a code which has been made by these networks themselves. Using these so called social plug-ins you can login to your social media profile and subsequently share information from the Website with others. With these social plug-ins third party marketing cookies are saved on your Devices. These cookies serve the purpose of optimizing your user experience.</p>
<p>Companies such as LinkedIn, Twitter and Facebook may share your personal data outside the European Economic Area. Please read our Privacy Policy <a href="/legal/privacy-policy/">here</a> and the respective social networkâ€™s privacy statement to see how these companies treat your (personal) data.</p>
<h3 id="pixels">Pixels</h3>
<p>We also place pixels of third parties. A pixel keeps track of your surfing behavior and how you use the Website. These data are aggregated and give us information about the target group that visits our website. Based on this information, we can show an advertisement to a specific or similar target group on the website of the third party that produced the pixel.</p>
<h2 id="disable-or-delete-cookies">Disable or delete cookies</h2>
<p>When you first visit the Website, we ask you to give consent for cookies that are not necessary or functional and that have an impact on your privacy. If you do not give consent, these cookies will not be placed. In that case, you may not be able to use all the functionalities on our Website.</p>
<p>You can configure your browser so that you do not receive any cookies the next time you use the website. You can read here how you can remove different types of cookies in different browsers. Please consult the help-function of your browser if your browser is not listed below.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://support.google.com/chrome/answer/95647?co=GENIE.Platform%3DDesktop&amp;hl=en-GB">Chrome</a></li>

<p>Note: Refusing or deleting cookies only affects the Device and the browser on which you perform this action. If you use different Devices and/or browsers you will need to repeat the above described actions on these Devices and/or browsers.</p>
