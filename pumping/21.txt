<h1 class="heading__title">Controlling Crawling and Indexing: the Ultimate Guide</h1>
<div class="heading__params">
<span aria-hidden="true" class="heading__label">
										Last updated:
									</span>
<span aria-label="The date of last update" class="heading__caption">
										April 14, 2022
									</span>
</div>
</div>
<div class="post-detail__text typo">
<section aria-label="Crawling and Indexing in short" class="highlighted-box highlighted-box--with-background typo-block"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">
<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/crawling-indexing--reversed.svg)"></i></span>Crawling and Indexing in short
	</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Take control of the crawling and indexing process of your website by communicating your preferences to search engines.</p>
<p>This helps them to understand what parts of your website to focus on, and what parts to ignore. There's a lot of methods to do this, so when to use which method?</p>
<p>In this article we'll discuss when to use each methods, and we'll highlight the pros and cons.</p>
</div>
</section>

<p>Search engines crawl billions of pages every day. But they index fewer pages than this, and they show even fewer pages in their results. You want your pages to be among them. So, how do you take control of this whole process and improve your rankings?</p>
<p>To answer that question, first we need look at how the crawling and indexing process works. Then we'll discuss all the methods you can put to work to control this process.</p>
<h2 id="how-does-crawling-work">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-does-crawling-work" tabindex="-1"></a>How does crawling work?</h2>
<p>Search engines' crawlers are tasked with finding and crawling as many URLs as possible. They do this to see if there's any new content out there. These URLs can be both new ones and URLs they already knew about. New URLs are found by crawling pages they already knew. After crawling, they pass on their results to the indexer. Pages that search engines are allowed to crawl are often called <em>crawlable</em>.</p>

<h2 id="how-does-indexing-work">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-does-indexing-work" tabindex="-1"></a>How does indexing work?</h2>
<p>The indexers receive the contents of URLs from the crawlers. Indexers then try to make sense of this content by analyzing it (including the links, if any). The indexer processes canonicalized URLs and determines the authority of each URL. The indexer also determines whether they should index a page. Pages that search engines are allowed to index are often called <em>indexable</em>.</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-1" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 1200px;"><img alt="After being crawled a page is forwarded to the indexers." class="image" height="338" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-crawlingindexing@2x.png" title="After being crawled a page is forwarded to the indexers." width="1200"/></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-1">After being crawled a page is forwarded to the indexers.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Indexers also render web pages and <a href="/academy/javascript-seo/">execute JavaScript</a>. If this results in any links being found, these are passed back to the crawler.</p>

<h2 id="how-to-control-crawling-and-indexing">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-to-control-crawling-and-indexing" tabindex="-1"></a>How to take control over crawling and indexing</h2>
<p>Take control of the crawling and indexing process by making your preferences clear to search engines. By doing so, you help them understand what sections of your website are most important to you.</p>
<p>In this chapter we'll cover all the methods and which to use when. We've also put together a table to illustrate what they can and cannot do.</p>
<p>First let's explain some concepts:</p>
<ul>
<li>Crawlable: are search engines able to crawl the URL?</li>
<li>Indexable: are search engines encouraged to index the URL?</li>
<li>Prevents duplicate content: does this method prevent <a href="/academy/duplicate-content/">duplicate content issues</a>?</li>
<li>Consolidates signals: are search engines encouraged to consolidate topical relevancy and <a href="/academy/authority/#page-authority">URL authority signals</a>, as defined by the URL's content and links?</li>
</ul>
<p>Furthermore, it's important to understand what <a href="/academy/crawl-budget/">crawl budget</a> is. Crawl budget is the amount of time search engines' crawlers spend on your website. You want them to spend it wisely, and you can give them instructions for that.</p>

<h2 id="robotstxt">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#robotstxt" tabindex="-1"></a>Robots.txt</h2>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><img alt="Controlling crawling and indexing with robots.txt." class="image" height="115" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-robottxt@2x.png" title="Controlling crawling and indexing with robots.txt." width="600"/></div>
<p>The <a href="/academy/robotstxt/">robots.txt file</a> is a central location that provides basic ground rules for crawlers. We call these ground rules <em>directives</em>. If you want to keep crawlers from crawling certain URLs, your robots.txt is the best way to do that.</p>
<p>If crawlers aren't allowed to crawl a URL and request its content, the indexer will never be able to analyse its content and links. This can prevent duplicate content, and it also means that the URL in question will never be able to rank. Also, search engines will not be able to consolidate topical relevance and authority signals when they don't know what's on the page. Those signals will therefore be lost.</p>

<p><strong>An example for using robots.txt</strong><br/>
A site's admin section is a good example of where you want to apply the robots.txt file to keep crawlers from accessing it. Let's say the admin section resides on: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/admin/</code>.</p>
<p>Block crawlers from accessing this section using the following directive in your robots.txt:</p>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Disallow: /admin</code></p>
<p>Can't change your robots.txt file? Then apply the <a href="/academy/control-crawl-indexing/#robots-tag">robots noindex directive</a> to the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/admin</code> section.</p>
<p><strong>Important notes</strong><br/>
Please note that URLs that are disallowed from being crawled by search engines can still appear in search results. This happens when the URLs are linked to from other pages, or were already known to search engines before they were made inaccessible through robots.txt. Search engines will then display a snippet like this:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 447px;"><img alt="Page is inaccessible through robots.txt" class="image" height="90" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/inaccessible-through-robots.png" title="Page is inaccessible through robots.txt" width="447"/></div>
<p>Robots.txt cannot resolve existing duplicate content issues. Search engines will not forget about a URL simply because they can't access it.</p>
<p>Adding a canonical URL or a meta robots noindex attribute to a URL that's been blocked through robots.txt will not get it deindexed. Search engines will never know about your request for deindexing, because your robots.txt file is keeping them from finding out.</p>
<p>The robots.txt file is an essential tool in optimizing crawl budget on your website. Using the robots.txt file, you can tell search engines not to crawl the parts of your website that are irrelevant for them.</p>
<p><strong>What the robots.txt file will do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Keep search engines from crawling certain parts of your website, thereby preserving crawl budget.</li>
<li>Keep search engines from indexing certain parts of your website – if there are no links to them.</li>
<li>Prevent new duplicate content issues.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>What the robots.txt file will not do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Consolidate relevancy and authority signals.</li>
<li>Remove content that's already indexed*.</li>
</ul>
<p>* While Google supports the <a href="/academy/robotstxt/#dont-use-noindex-in-your-robots-txt">noindex directive</a> and will <a href="/academy/google-remove-urls/">remove URLs from its index</a>, it's not recommended to use this method as it's an unofficial standard. It's only supported by Google and not 100% fool proof. Only use it when you can't use <a href="/academy/control-crawl-indexing/#robots-tag">robots directives</a> and <a href="/academy/control-crawl-indexing/#canonical-url">canonical URLs</a>.</p>

<h2 id="robots-tag">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#robots-tag" tabindex="-1"></a>Robots directives</h2>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><img alt="Controlling crawling and indexing with Meta Robots noindex." class="image" height="115" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-metarobots@2x.png" title="Controlling crawling and indexing with Meta Robots noindex." width="600"/></div>
<p>The robots directives instruct search engines on how to index pages, while keeping the page accessible for visitors. Often it's used to instruct search engines not to index certain pages. When it comes to indexing, it's a stronger signal than the canonical URL.</p>
<p>Implementing robots directives is generally done by including it in the source using the meta robots tag. For other documents such as PDFs or images, it's done through the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">X-Robots-Tag HTTP header</code>.</p>
<p><strong>An example for the use of the robots directives</strong><br/>
Say you have ten landing pages for Google AdWords traffic. You copied the content from other pages and then slightly adjusted it. You don't want these landing pages to be indexed, because that would cause duplicate content issues, so you include the robots directive with the noindex attribute.</p>
<p><strong>Important notes</strong><br/>
The robots directives helps you prevent duplicate content, but it doesn't attribute topical relevance and authority to another URL. That's just lost.</p>
<p>Besides instructing search engines not to index a page, the robots directives also discourages search engines from crawling the page. Some crawl budget is preserved because of this.</p>
<p>Contrary to its name, the robots directives nofollow attribute will not influence crawling of a page that has the nofollow attribute. However, when the robots directives nofollow attribute is set search engine crawlers won't use links on this page to crawl other pages and subsequently won't pass on authority to these other pages.</p>
<p><strong>What the robots directives will do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Keep search engines from indexing certain parts of your website.</li>
<li>Prevent duplicate content issues.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>What the robots directives will not do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Keep search engines from crawling certain parts of your website, preserving crawl budget.</li>
<li>Consolidate most of the relevancy and authority signals.</li>
</ul>

Check out the ultimate guide to <a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/">meta robots tag</a>.</p>

<h2 id="canonical-url">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#canonical-url" tabindex="-1"></a>Canonical URLs</h2>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><img alt="Controlling crawling and indexing with Canonical URLs." class="image" height="115" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-canonicalurl@2x.png" title="Controlling crawling and indexing with Canonical URLs." width="600"/></div>
<p>A canonical URL communicates the canonical version of a page to search engines, encouraging search engines to index the canonical version. The canonical URL can reference itself or other pages. If it's useful for visitors to be able to access multiple versions of a page and you want search engines to treat them as one version, the canonical URL is the way to go. When one page references another page using the canonical URL, most of its topical relevance and authority is attributed to the target URL.</p>
<p><strong>An example for the use of a canonical URL</strong><br/>
Say you have an eCommerce website with a product in three categories. The product is accessible via three different URLs. This is fine for visitors, but search engines should only focus on crawling and indexing one URL. Choose one of categories as the primary one, and canonicalize the other two categories to it.</p>
<p><strong>Important notes</strong><br/>
Make sure to 301 redirect URLs that don't serve a purpose for visitors anymore to the canonical version. This enables you to attribute all their topical relevance and authority to the canonical version. This also helps to get other websites to link to the canonical version.</p>
<p>A canonical URL is a guideline, rather than a directive. Search engines can choose to ignore it.</p>
<p>Applying a canonical URL will not preserve any crawl budget, as it doesn't prevent search engines from crawling pages. It prevents them from being returned for search queries as they are consolidated to the canonical version of the URL.</p>
<p><strong>What a canonical URL will do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Keep search engines from indexing certain parts of your website.</li>
<li>Prevent duplicate content issues.</li>
<li>Consolidate most of the relevancy and authority signals.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>What a canonical URL will not do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Keep search engines from crawling certain parts of your website, thereby preserving crawl budget.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Want to read more about canonical URLs?</strong><br/>
Check out the ultimate <a href="/academy/canonical/">canonical URL reference guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="hreflang-attribute">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#hreflang-attribute" tabindex="-1"></a>Hreflang attribute</h2>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><img alt="Controlling crawling and indexing with hreflang." class="image" height="115" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-hreflang@2x-2.png" title="Controlling crawling and indexing with hreflang." width="600"/></div>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">rel="alternate" hreflang="x"</code> link attribute, or <a href="/academy/hreflang/"><strong><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">hreflang</code> attribute</strong></a> for short, is used to communicate to search engines what language your content is in and what geographical region your content is meant for. If you're using the same content or similar content to target multiple regions,<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">hreflang</code> is the way to go. It enables you to rank your pages in your intended markets.</p>
<p>It helps prevent duplicate content, so having two pages with the exact same content for the United Kingdom and United States is fine when you've implemented<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">hreflang</code>. Duplicate content aside, the most important thing is to make sure your content rhymes with the audience. Make sure your audience feels at home, so having (somewhat) different text and visuals for the United Kingdom and United States is recommended.</p>
<p><strong>An example of using hreflang</strong><br/>
You're targeting several English speaking markets using subdomains for each market. Each subdomain contains English content, localized for its market:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">www.example.com</code> for the US market</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">ca.example.com</code> for the Canadian market</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">uk.example.com</code> for the UK market</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">au.example.com</code> for the Australian market</li>
</ul>
<p>Within each market you want to rank with the right page. Here's where hreflang comes in.</p>
<p><strong>What the hreflang attribute will do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Help search engines rank the right content in the right market.</li>
<li>Prevent duplicate content issues.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>What the hreflang attribute will not do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Keep search engines from crawling certain parts of your website, thereby preserving crawl budget.</li>
<li>Keep search engines from indexing certain parts of your website.</li>
<li>Consolidate relevancy and authority signals.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Want to read more about hreflang?</strong><br/>
Check out the ultimate <a href="/academy/hreflang/">hreflang reference guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="pagination-attributes">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#pagination-attributes" tabindex="-1"></a>Pagination attributes</h2>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><img alt="Controlling crawling and indexing with rel=prev and rel=next." class="image" height="115" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-prevnext@2x.png" title="Controlling crawling and indexing with rel=prev and rel=next." width="600"/></div>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">rel="prev"</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">rel="next"</code> link attributes, <strong>pagination attributes</strong> for short, are used to communicate the relationships among a series of pages to search engines. For series of similar pages, such as paginated blog archive pages or paginated product category pages, it's highly advisable to use the pagination attributes. Search engines will understand that the pages are very similar, which will eliminate duplicate content issues.</p>
<p>In most cases, search engines will not rank other pages than the first one in the paginated series.</p>
<p><strong>What the pagination attributes will do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Prevent duplicate content issues.</li>
<li>Consolidate relevancy and authority signals.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>What the pagination attributes will not do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Keep search engines from crawling certain parts of your website, thereby preserving crawl budget.</li>
<li>Keep search engines from indexing certain parts of your website.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Want to read more about pagination attributes?</strong><br/>
Check out the ultimate <a href="/academy/pagination/">pagination reference guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="mobile-attribute">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#mobile-attribute" tabindex="-1"></a>Mobile attribute</h2>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><img alt="Controlling crawling and indexing with rel=alternate mobile attribute." class="image" height="115" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-relalternatemedia@2x.png" title="Controlling crawling and indexing with rel=alternate mobile attribute." width="600"/></div>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">rel="alternate"</code> mobile attribute, or <strong>mobile attribute</strong> for short, communicates the relationship between a website's desktop and mobile versions to search engines. It helps search engines show the right website for the right device and prevents duplicate content issues in the process.</p>
<p><strong>What the mobile attribute will do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Prevent duplicate content issues.</li>
<li>Consolidate relevancy and authority signals.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>What the mobile attribute will not do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Keep search engines from crawling certain parts of your website, thereby preserving crawl budget.</li>
<li>Keep search engines from indexing certain parts of your website.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Want to read more about the mobile attribute?</strong><br/>
Check out the ultimate <a href="/academy/mobile-attribute/">mobile attribute reference guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="webmaster-tools">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#webmaster-tools" tabindex="-1"></a>Parameter handling in Webmaster Tools</h2>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><img alt="Controlling crawling and indexing with Webmaster Tools." class="image" height="115" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-webmaster@2x.png" title="Controlling crawling and indexing with Webmaster Tools." width="600"/></div>
<p>If you're unable to make changes (quickly) to your website, you can set up parameter handling in Google Search Console and Bing Webmaster Tools. Parameter handling defines how search engines should deal with URLs that contain a parameter. Using this, you can tell Google and Bing not to crawl and/or index certain URLs.</p>
<p>In order to set up parameter handling, you need URLs that are identifiable by a pattern. Parameter handling should only be used in certain situations, for example sorting, filtering, translating, and saving session data.</p>
<p><strong>Important note</strong><br/>
Keep in mind that configuring this for Google and Bing will not affect how other search engines crawl your website.</p>
<p><strong>What parameter handling will do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Keep search engines from crawling certain parts of your website, thereby preserving crawl budget.</li>
<li>Keep search engines from indexing certain parts of your website.</li>
<li>Prevent duplicate content issues.</li>
<li>Consolidate relevancy and authority signals.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>What parameter handling will not do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Let you configure crawling and indexing for individual URLs.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="http-authentication">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#http-authentication" tabindex="-1"></a>HTTP authentication</h2>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><img alt="Controlling crawling and indexing with HTTP authentication." class="image" height="115" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-httpauth@2x.png" title="Controlling crawling and indexing with HTTP authentication." width="600"/></div>
<p>HTTP authentication requires users or machines to log in to gain access to a (section of a ) website. Here's an example of how it looks:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 396px;"><img alt="Authentication required" class="image" height="232" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/authentication-required.png" title="Authentication required" width="396"/></div>
<p>Without a username and password, you (or a robot) won't get past the login screen, and you won't be able to access anything. HTTP authentication is great way to keep unwanted visitors – both humans and search engine crawlers – out of for instance a test environment. <a href="https://support.google.com/webmasters/answer/93708" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Google recommends</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> using HTTP authentication to prevent search engine crawlers from accessing test environments:</p>
<p><em>If you have confidential or private content that you don't want to appear in Google Search results, the simplest and most effective way to block private URLs from appearing is to store them in a password-protected directory on your site server. Googlebot and all other web crawlers are unable to access content in password-protected directories.</em></p>
<p><strong>What HTTP authentication will do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Keep search engines from crawling certain parts of your website, thereby preserving crawl budget.</li>
<li>Keep search engines from indexing certain parts of your website.</li>
<li>Prevent duplicate content issues.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>What HTTP authentication will not do:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Consolidate relevancy and authority signals.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="fetch-as-search-engines-putting-yourself-in-their-shoes">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#fetch-as-search-engines-putting-yourself-in-their-shoes" tabindex="-1"></a>Fetch as search engines: putting yourself in their shoes</h2>
<p>So how do search engine crawlers see your pages, and how do your pages render? Put yourself in their shoes by using their "Fetch and Render" tools.</p>
<p>Google's "Fetch as Googlebot" feature is most well known. It's located <a href="https://www.google.com/webmasters/tools/googlebot-fetch?hl=en" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">in Google Search Console</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> and allows you to fill in a URL on your site and then Google will show you what their crawlers see on this URL, and how they render the URL. You can do this for both desktop and mobile. See below what this looks like:</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-12" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 1247px;"><img alt="Fetching a URL" class="image" height="802" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/fetch-as-googlebot-fetch-result.png" title="Fetching a URL" width="1247"/></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-12">Fetching a URL</figcaption></figure>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-13" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 1245px;"><img alt="Rendering a URL" class="image" height="788" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2018/01/fetch-as-googlebot-render-result.png" title="Rendering a URL" width="1245"/></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-13">Rendering a URL</figcaption></figure>
<p>Rendering a URL</p>
<p>It's great for double checking whether URLs are responding as expected, but also to force push indexing the URL ("Request indexing"). Within seconds you can get a URL crawled and indexed. This doesn't mean its content is immediately processed and rankings are adjusted, but it does enable you to speed up the crawling and indexing process.</p>
<h3 id="other-situations-in-which-fetch-as-googlebot-comes-in-handy">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#other-situations-in-which-fetch-as-googlebot-comes-in-handy" tabindex="-1"></a>Other situations in which Fetch as Googlebot comes in handy</h3>
<p>Fetch as Googlebot is not just useful for speeding up the crawling and indexing process of an individual URL, it also allows you to:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<strong>Speed up discovery of entire new sections on your website</strong><br/>
Fetch the URL from which the new sections are linked, and choose to "Request index" with the option to "Crawl this URL and its direct links"</li>
<li>
<strong>Audit the mobile experience users have on your site:</strong><br/>
Fetch a URL as "Mobile: smartphone".</li>
<li>
<strong>Check whether 301-redirects are working correctly.</strong><br/>
Fill in a URL and check the header response.</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Notes:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Situation 3 can more easily be done, in bulk within ContentKing.</li>
<li>Google allows you to submit 500 URLs per month for Indexing.</li>
<li>Google allows you to only submit 10 URLs per month for Indexing with all URLs linked from that URL getting crawled too.</li>
<li>Bing has a similar tool, called "<a href="https://www.bing.com/webmaster/help/fetch-as-bingbot-fe18fa0d" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Fetch as Bingbot</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>".</li>

<h3 id="how-often-does-google-crawl-my-website">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-often-does-google-crawl-my-website" tabindex="-1"></a>1. 🗓️ How often does Google crawl my website?</h3>
<p>Google Search Console shares their crawl behavior with you. To check it out:</p>
<ol>
<li>Log on to Google Search Console and select a website.</li>
<li>Head to "Crawl" &gt; "Crawl Stats" and there you'll find out how often Google crawls your website.</li>
</ol>
<p>If you're fairly tech savvy, you can find out how often Google's crawls your website by analyzing your <a href="/academy/log-file-analysis/#what-is-log-file">website's log files</a>).</p>
<p>It's worth noting that Google determines how often they should crawl your website using the <a href="/academy/crawl-budget/">crawl budget</a> for your website.</p>
<h3 id="can-i-slow-down-crawlers-when-they-re-crawling-my-website">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#can-i-slow-down-crawlers-when-they-re-crawling-my-website" tabindex="-1"></a>2. 🐌 Can I slow down crawlers when they're crawling my website?</h3>
<p>Yes, you can do this using the <a href="/academy/robotstxt/#crawl-delay">crawl-delay</a> robots.txt directive. Google won't listen to it though. If you want Googlebot to crawl slower than you have to configure this in Google Search Console. Regardless of the method, it's not recommended to throttle Google and Bing's crawlers. Their crawlers are smart enough to know when your website is having a hard time, and they'll check back later in that case.</p>
<h3 id="how-to-prevent-search-engines-from-crawling-a-website-or-page">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-to-prevent-search-engines-from-crawling-a-website-or-page" tabindex="-1"></a>3. 🛑 How do I prevent search engines from crawling a website or page?</h3>
<p>There's a few ways to go about preventing search engines from crawling parts of your website, or just specific pages:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<strong>Robots.txt:</strong> can be used to prevent the crawling of an entire website, sections, and individual pages.</li>
<li>
<strong>Parameter handling:</strong> can be used to prevent the crawling of  URLs that contain a certain parameter.</li>
<li>
<strong>HTTP authentication:</strong> can be used to prevent the crawling of an entire website, sections, and individual pages.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="what-does-indexing-a-website-mean">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-does-indexing-a-website-mean" tabindex="-1"></a>4. 📇 What does indexing a website mean?</h3>
<p>It means actions are performed by a search engine to try to make sense of a website, in order to make it findable through their search engine.</p>
<h3 id="is-my-website-indexable-for-search-engines">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#is-my-website-indexable-for-search-engines" tabindex="-1"></a>5. 🔎 Is my website indexable for search engines?</h3>
<p>The best way to answer this is to <a href="/">create an account with ContentKing</a> to evaluate how indexable your website is for search engines. As you've been able to read above, there are many ways to influence how search engines index your website.</p>
<h3 id="how-often-does-google-index-my-website">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-often-does-google-index-my-website" tabindex="-1"></a>6. 📅 How often does Google index my website?</h3>
<p>As often as Google crawls your website. Its crawlers will pass on whatever they have found to the indexer, which takes care of indexing websites.</p>
<h3 id="how-long-before-google-has-indexed-my-new-website">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-long-before-google-has-indexed-my-new-website" tabindex="-1"></a>7. ⌚ How long will it take Google to index my new website?</h3>
<p>There's no single answer to this question, as it depends on the promotion of the new website. Promoting it speeds up the crawling and indexing process. If you do this well, a small website can be indexed with an hour. Alternatively, it can also take months to index an entirely new website.</p>
<p>Please note that having your website indexed by search engines doesn't mean your pages will start <em>ranking</em> high right off the bat. Achieving high rankings takes a lot more time.</p>
<h3 id="how-to-prevent-search-engines-from-indexing-a-website-or-page">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-to-prevent-search-engines-from-indexing-a-website-or-page" tabindex="-1"></a>8. ✋ How do I prevent search engines from indexing a website or page?</h3>
<p>Search engines can be prevented from indexing a website or page via these methods:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<strong>Meta Robots noindex tag:</strong> is a very strong signal to search engines not to index a page. It doesn't pass on relevance or authority signals to other pages.</li>
<li>
<strong>Canonical URL:</strong> is a moderately strong signal to search engines about what page to index, and to attribute relevance and authority signals too.</li>
<li>
<strong>HTTP authentication:</strong> will only prevent crawling and indexing of new pages from an SEO point of view. But it's a still general best practice to use HTTP authentication on test environments to keep unwanted search engines and users out of it.</li>
<li>
<strong>Robots.txt:</strong> can only be used to prevent the crawling and indexing of new pages.</li>
<li>
<strong>Parameter handling:</strong> can be used to prevent the crawling and indexing of URLs containing a certain parameter.</li>
</ul>

<p>Cookies are small text files which are stored on the browser or hard drive of your Device when you visit a webpage or use an application. Cookies may be needed to show the webpage or application on your Device and are also used to enhance the user experience. Cookies cannot damage your Devices or the files saved on it. There are different types of cookies. Some cookies come directly from our website (first party cookies) and others come from third parties which place cookies on our site (third party cookies).</p>
<p>Cookies can be stored for varying lengths of time on your browser or device. Session cookies are deleted from your computer or device when you close your web-browser. Persistent cookies will remain stored on your computer or Device until deleted or until they reach their expiry date.</p>
<h2 id="how-do-we-use-cookies">How do we use cookies?</h2>
<h3 id="essential-technical-cookies">Essential (Technical) cookies</h3>
<p>We try to provide an advanced and user-friendly website that adapts automatically to needs and wishes of our visitors and users. To achieve this, we use technical cookies to i.e. show you our Website, to make it function correctly, to create your ContentKing account, to sign you in and to manage your requests. These technical cookies are necessary for our website to function properly.</p>
<h3 id="functional-cookies">Functional cookies</h3>
<p>We also use functional cookies to remember your preferences and settings (as username, password, language etc.) and to help you to use the Website efficiently and effectively. These functional cookies are not strictly necessary for the functioning of our Website, but they add functionality for you and enhance your experience.</p>
<h3 id="analytical-cookies">Analytical cookies</h3>
<p>We use analytical cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. These analytic cookies generate statistical and other information about website use by means of cookies, which are stored on users’ Devices. The information generated relating to our Website is used to create reports about the use of the Website. Analytical cookies may be placed without your consent, unless such cookies have an impact on your privacy. In such cases, prior consent will be asked for.</p>
<h3 id="google-analytics">Google Analytics</h3>
<p>We use Google Analytics cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. With these analytical cookies a permanent cookie is saved on your device to register your use of the Website. Google Analytics analyses this data and provides us with the results. This way, we obtain insight into the traffic of the Website and the way in which the Website is used. Based on this information we are able to make specific adjustments to the Website.</p>
<p>The information that we obtain is transferred to Google and stored by Google on servers outside the European Economic Area. We have entered into a data processing agreement with Google in which agreements have been made regarding the processing of personal data.</p>
<p>If you would like more information about analytical cookies and their expiry date, please visit the Google Analytics <a href="https://developers.google.com/analytics/devguides/collection/gajs/cookie-usage">information page</a>. Google also offers an <a href="https://tools.google.com/dlpage/gaoptout?hl=en">opt-out option</a> for data collection within the scope of Google Analytics.</p>
<h3 id="marketing-cookies">Marketing cookies</h3>
<p>We use third party marketing cookies in order to follow your internet browsing behavior and collect data and information on your browsing behavior from various websites you visited. This information is used to make the content of the displayed advertisements as relevant as possible, and to limit repetition of the same advertisements.</p>
<h3 id="social-plug-ins">Social plug-ins</h3>
<p>By using the Website you can get access to social media websites, such as Facebook, Twitter and LinkedIn. These buttons come with a code which has been made by these networks themselves. Using these so called social plug-ins you can login to your social media profile and subsequently share information from the Website with others. With these social plug-ins third party marketing cookies are saved on your Devices. These cookies serve the purpose of optimizing your user experience.</p>
<p>Companies such as LinkedIn, Twitter and Facebook may share your personal data outside the European Economic Area. Please read our Privacy Policy <a href="/legal/privacy-policy/">here</a> and the respective social network’s privacy statement to see how these companies treat your (personal) data.</p>
<h3 id="pixels">Pixels</h3>
<p>We also place pixels of third parties. A pixel keeps track of your surfing behavior and how you use the Website. These data are aggregated and give us information about the target group that visits our website. Based on this information, we can show an advertisement to a specific or similar target group on the website of the third party that produced the pixel.</p>
<h2 id="disable-or-delete-cookies">Disable or delete cookies</h2>
<p>When you first visit the Website, we ask you to give consent for cookies that are not necessary or functional and that have an impact on your privacy. If you do not give consent, these cookies will not be placed. In that case, you may not be able to use all the functionalities on our Website.</p>
<p>You can configure your browser so that you do not receive any cookies the next time you use the website. You can read here how you can remove different types of cookies in different browsers. Please consult the help-function of your browser if your browser is not listed below.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://support.google.com/chrome/answer/95647?co=GENIE.Platform%3DDesktop&amp;hl=en-GB">Chrome</a></li>
<li><a href="https://support.mozilla.org/en-US/kb/clear-cookies-and-site-data-firefox">Firefox</a></li>
<li><a href="https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-edge/delete-cookies-in-microsoft-edge-63947406-40ac-c3b8-57b9-2a946a29ae09">Edge</a></li>
<li><a href="https://support.apple.com/guide/safari/manage-cookies-and-website-data-sfri11471/mac">Safari</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Note: Refusing or deleting cookies only affects the Device and the browser on which you perform this action. If you use different Devices and/or browsers you will need to repeat the above described actions on these Devices and/or browsers.</p>
