<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/magento-seo--reversed.svg)"></i></span>Magento 2 SEO Guide in short
	</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Magento is an incredibly powerful platform, but in order to make the most of it you need to configure it properly. This guide shows you how to do exactly that, zooming in on:</p>
<ul>
<li>Creating a SEO-friendly URL structure</li>
<li>Configuring page titles and meta descriptions correctly</li>
<li>Using headings correctly.</li>
<li>Taking search engines by the hand when they’re crawling and indexing your store.</li>
<li>Speed optimizing.</li>
</ul>

<p>Magento, founded in the United States in 2008, has become a leading eCommerce platform worldwide. That position was strengthened further with <a href="https://techcrunch.com/2018/05/21/adobe-to-acquire-magento-for-1-6-b/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">their acquisition by Adobe in 2018</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>
<p>It’s estimated that <a href="https://trends.builtwith.com/shop/Magento" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">around 270,000 stores</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> run on Magento world-wide. And according to Magento, they enabled their merchants to earn <a href="https://magento.com/case-studies" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">$155 billion in revenue in 2018</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>. It’s safe to say that Magento is a household brand in eCommerce land.</p>
<p>While Magento 2 is a very powerful platform, if you’re running on its default settings, you’re not going to get great SEO performance out of it. That’s why you need to put yourself in the position to win, and by applying all the best practices for Magento 2, you do just that.</p>
<p>In this article, we’ll explain step-by-step what you need to do in order to get your Magento store into top shape, all set for maximum SEO performance.</p>
<h2 id="what-does-it-take">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-does-it-take" tabindex="-1"></a>What does it take to make your Magento store rank well?</h2>
<p>In order to rank your Magento store well in the organic search listings, you need to make sure search engines can easily and efficiently find all your content and make sense of it, and that they have reasons to rank your content higher than others.</p>
<p>We can break all this down into the so-called “Three pillars of SEO”:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<strong>Technology:</strong> the technical foundation of a website, which helps search engines find and understand your site as quickly and efficiently as possible. This is about setting yourself up for success.</li>
<li>
<strong>Relevance:</strong> what search queries your content is relevant for. This is done by creating content that’s useful and satisfying for your visitors. Good technology is essential in order to communicate relevancy clearly.</li>
<li>
<strong>Authority and Trust:</strong> links to your website; these determine your authority and trustworthiness.</li>
</ol>
<p>This article describes the first pillar, <em>Technology</em>, in detail, as this is platform-specific, while <em>Relevance</em> and <em>Authority and Trust</em> work the same for every platform.</p>

<h3 class="toc" id="url-structure">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#url-structure" tabindex="-1"></a>Defining a SEO-friendly URL structure</h3>
<p>A good URL structure is one that works well for both visitors and search engines.</p>
<p>In a nutshell, your URLs need to be:</p>
<ul>
<li>Descriptive and easy to read</li>
<li>Brief</li>
<li>Consistent</li>
<li>Lowercase</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="website-redirects-and-url-rewrite-settings">Website redirects and URL rewrite settings</h4>
<p>You can manage a store’s redirects and rewrite settings by logging on to your Magento backend and navigating to:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Stores</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Configuration</code> (under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Settings</code>) &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">General</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Web</code>.</li>
<li>At the top of the page, select the right Store View you want to adjust the settings for. In our example this is <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Default Store View</code>.
<strong>Note:</strong> changing the settings on a higher level impacts other store views, so be careful with that. Read more on this <a href="https://docs.magento.com/m2/ce/user_guide/configuration/scope.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">here</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</li>
<li>Under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Url Options</code> you’ll find the field <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Add Store Code to Urls</code>. Keep its value set to it’s default: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">No</code> to make sure URLs are descriptive and easy to read.</li>
<li>The field <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Auto-redirect to Base URL</code> enables you to automatically redirect visitors and search engines when they request your store without the subdomain (e.g. when <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com</code> is requested, clients are forwarded to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com</code>), or vice versa. While this is a great feature, by default it’s configured to use a temporary <a href="/academy/redirects/#302-found-moved-temporarily">302 redirect</a> instead of a <a href="/academy/redirects/faq/301-redirect/">301 redirect</a>. Change this value to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Yes (301 Moved Permanently)</code> to make sure link authority from incorrectly placed links is passed on as much as possible to the right URL.</li>
<li>Under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Search Engine Optimization</code> you’ll find the field <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Use Web Server Rewrites</code> which is by default set to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Yes</code>. Keep it this way, as this determines that your store gets to use readable URLs.</li>
</ol>

<h4 id="remove-the-html-extension-from-product-urls-and-product-category-urls">Remove the .html extension from product URLs and product-category URLs</h4>
<p>By default, Magento adds the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">.html</code> extension to all URLs. It’s recommended to remove the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">.html</code> extension to prevent potential issues further down the road, when switching to a different eCommerce platform.</p>
<section aria-label="Pro tip" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--protip-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Pro tip</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Only remove the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">.html</code> extension if you're starting from scratch, as removing it for an active Magento store leads to a URL migration, which you want to avoid as much as possible.</p>
</div></section>
<p>The secret to defining a great URL structure is to think ahead. When you’re <a href="/academy/website-migrations/">migrating to a different platform</a>, you want to minimize the differences between both platforms.</p>
<p>To remove the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">.html</code> extension from the URLs, log on to your Magento backend and navigate to:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Stores</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Configuration</code> (under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Settings</code>) &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Catalog</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Catalog</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Search Engine Optimization</code>.</li>
<li>At the top of the page, select the right Store View you want to adjust the settings for. In our example this is <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Default Store View</code>.</li>
<li>In the screen that this loads, you'll find the fields <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Product URL Suffix</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Category URL Suffix</code>. Both fields have the value <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">.html</code>. Removing that value from both fields will lead to URLs such as <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/category</code> for category pages, and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/product</code> for product pages. If you prefer to have a trailing slash at the end of all URLs, you can fill in <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/</code> in both fields, leading to URLs like <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/category/</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/product/</code>.</li>
</ol>

<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-2">Magento 2 URL suffix settings.</figcaption></figure>
<h4 id="keep-urls-short-by-not-including-categories-in-urls">Keep URLs short by not including categories in URLs</h4>
<p>In that same screen, you can choose whether or not to include the product-category key in a product’s URL, for example: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/category/product/</code>.</p>
<p>By default, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Use Categories Path for Product URLs</code> is set to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">No</code>. Keep it that way to ensure that URLs stay short. This is especially important if you have categories with subcategories; URLs will become very long quickly. If you have products with highly similar names, it's recommended to include a unique identifier such as the SKU in a product's URL. Out of the box, you can't configure this in the Magento 2 backend, but you can use <a href="https://marketplace.magento.com/webpanda-catalog-url-rewrites.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">an extension</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> for this.</p>

<p>By default, this is turned on: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Create Permanent Redirect for URLs if URL Key Changed</code> is set to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Yes</code>. Keep it like this, it’ll save you a lot of headaches down the road.</p>

<h3 class="toc" id="meta-information">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#meta-information" tabindex="-1"></a>Defining meta information</h3>
<p>Your page title and meta description have a big impact on both your rankings and, when your page is shown in the SERP, your click-through-rate (CTR) as well. At the same time, you can’t manually define a title and meta description for each page, so you need to be smart about that. You need to use smart defaults, which can then be overwritten if need be.</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-5" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.png"/><img alt="Screenshot of a search engine result snippet from Google, with the title, URL and description highlighted." class="image" height="158" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-shopware-serp-snippet-en@2x.png 2x" title="A search engine result snippet from Google, with the title, URL and description highlighted." width="600"/></picture></div>

<h4 id="what-makes-for-a-good-title">What makes for a good title?</h4>
<p>A good title is easy to read, contains important keywords, has a call to action, is unique, and has a length between 285 and 575 pixels (30 and 60 characters respectively).</p>
<h4 id="what-makes-for-a-good-meta-description">What makes for a good meta description?</h4>
<p>What goes for the title goes for the meta description, except that the preferred length is different. Keep the meta description between 430 and 920 pixels long (70 and 155 characters respectively).</p>
<h4 id="define-the-homepage-meta-information">Define the homepage meta information</h4>
<p>To define the homepage meta information, log on to your Magento backend and navigate to:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Content</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Configuration</code> (under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Design</code>)</li>
<li>There, look for <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Default Store View</code> and click the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Edit</code> link after it to adjust the Default Store View homepage’s meta information.</li>
</ol>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-6" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-homepage-design-configuration@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-homepage-design-configuration@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-magento-seo-homepage-design-configuration@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-homepage-design-configuration@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-homepage-design-configuration@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-magento-seo-homepage-design-configuration@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-homepage-design-configuration@1x.png"/><img alt="Screenshot of the Design Configuration screen in Magento 2" class="image" height="160" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-homepage-design-configuration@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-homepage-design-configuration@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-magento-seo-homepage-design-configuration@2x.png 2x" title="Navigating to the Default Store View homepage’s meta information in Magento 2." width="600"/></picture></div>

<p>Scroll down to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">HTML Head</code> under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Other Settings</code> and expand the setting options:</p>

<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-7">Editing the Default Store View homepage’s meta information in Magento 2.</figcaption></figure>
<p>You can ignore the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Default Page Title</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Default Meta Description</code> fields, as they aren't used within Magento by default. Ignore the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Default Meta Keywords</code> field as well, as meta keywords haven’t been used by search engines for over a decade. They play no role in SEO.</p>
<p>The two remaining fields, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Page Title Prefix</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Page Title Suffix</code> do matter:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Page Title Prefix</code>: you want to keep this field empty, as whatever you add there is added at the start (the most important part) of the title. Having a generic prefix goes against everything we know about the title tag, so keep it empty.</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Page Title Suffix</code>: fill in <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">- $shopName</code>, for example: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">- ContentKing</code>. This will be added at the end of every page title, which helps you from a branding point of view.</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="define-meta-information-templates-for-products-and-categories">Define meta information templates for products and categories</h4>
<p>Defining meta information templates for products and categories, which take up the lion’s share of a store’s pages, is what will save you the most time here.</p>
<p>Regrettably, this is a feature Magento itself lacks. It wasn’t available in Magento 1, and it isn’t available in Magento 2 either. Therefore, we recommend using an extension such as “<a href="https://marketplace.magento.com/amasty-meta.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Meta Tags Templates by Amasty</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>” or “<a href="https://marketplace.magento.com/mageworx-module-seoxtemplatesmeta.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">SEO Meta Templates by MageWorx</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>”.</p>
<p>Here are some sample page title templates you can use:</p>
<ul>
<li>Category pages: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$categoryName | $shopName</code> – so for example: “T-shirts for men | Joe’s T-shirts”.</li>
<li>Subcategory pages: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$subcategoryName | $categoryName | $shopName</code> – so for example: “Breaking Bad T-shirts | T-shirts for men | Joe’s T-shirts”.</li>
<li>Product detail pages: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Buy $productName | $subcategoryName | $shopName</code> – so for example: “Buy Mr. White T-shirt | Breaking Bad t-shirts | Joe’s T-shirts”. Here we’re deliberately leaving out <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$categoryName</code>, as that would often make the title too long.</li>
</ul>
<p>Here are sample meta description templates you can use:</p>
<ul>
<li>Category pages: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Looking for $categoryName? Check out our massive selection of $amountOfProducts T-shirts at $shopName!</code>, so for example: “Looking for T-shirts? Check out our massive selection of 10,431 T-shirts at Joe’s T-shirts”.</li>
<li>Subcategory pages: use the same template as mentioned above for category pages.</li>
<li>Product detail pages: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Buy a $productName starting from $productPrize. Shipping time: $shippingTime. Only at $shopName!</code>, so for example: “Buy a Mr. White T-shirt for as little as $19.99. Shipping time: 24 hours. Only at Joe’s T-shirts!”.</li>
</ul>

<h3 class="toc" id="headings">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#headings" tabindex="-1"></a>Using headings correctly</h3>
<p>Whether headings are used correctly in your Magento store depends on two things:</p>
<ol>
<li>How headings are structured within your theme.</li>
<li>How you use headings in your content – which is entirely up to you.</li>
</ol>
<p>Here’s a quick refresher:</p>
<p>A good heading structure helps visitors quickly scan a page, and helps search engines easily understand its structure and topic.</p>
<p>Stick to these heading best practices:</p>
<ul>
<li>Don’t wrap logos or images within <a href="/academy/headings/faq/h1/">H1 heading tags</a>, as the H1 heading conveys the main topic of the page. Therefore, the H1 heading should include meaningful text.</li>
<li>Use only one H1 heading per page.</li>
<li>For readability, stick to a maximum heading length of 60 characters.</li>
<li>Don't use headings for global elements that are present on every page, such as for example search functionality.</li>
<li>Don't apply H2 headings to product names on product category overview pages.</li>
<li>Build up your heading structure with a logical hierarchy, as illustrated below.</li>
</ul>

<li>Be smart about your product-category, product, and page names, because these names will be used as the H1 headings by default.</li>
<li>Don’t use H1 headings in the body content, because that will lead to having multiple H1 headings (see above).</li>
</ul>

<h3 class="toc" id="crawling-indexing">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#crawling-indexing" tabindex="-1"></a>Crawling &amp; Indexing</h3>
<p>This section describes how you need to configure Magento to make it easy for search engines to crawl your store, and to understand what they need to index and what not.</p>
<h4 id="implement-canonical-urls-for-homepage-and-cms-pages">Implement canonical URLs for homepage and CMS pages</h4>
<p>By default, Magento doesn't add self-referencing canonical URLs to its homepage and CMS pages. This means, you're at risk of having duplicate content.</p>
<p>In order to fix this, you have several options:</p>
<ol>
<li>You adjust the template to always include the self-referencing canonical URL for the homepage and CMS pages.</li>
<li>You add a custom XML block to pages, where you manually define the self-referencing canonical URL for the homepage and CMS pages.</li>
<li>You use a plugin for this, like for example <a href="https://marketplace.magento.com/fme-canonicalurl.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">this one</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</li>
</ol>
<h4 id="turn-on-the-use-of-canonical-urls-for-products-and-categories">Turn on the use of canonical URLs for products and categories</h4>
<p><a href="/academy/canonical/">Canonical URLs</a> give search engines a strong hint as to what page they need to index. So in a case where you have one product with multiple variants, and you want to signal to search engines that they are to only index the product page, and not the variant pages, then you can do so with the canonical URL.</p>
<p>By default, the use of canonical URLs is turned off in Magento. With this setting turned off, a product is available through both its own URL and a URL that includes the category: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/product/</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/category/product/</code>. This leads to situations where these pages are competing with each other for a search engine’s attention.</p>
<p>To turn on the use of canonical URLs, log on to your Magento backend and navigate to:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Stores</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Configuration</code> (Under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Settings</code>) &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Catalog</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Catalog</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Search Engine Optimization</code>.</li>
<li>At the top of the page, select the right Store View you want to adjust the settings for. In our example, this is <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Default Store View</code>.</li>
<li>In the screen that this loads, you’ll find fields named <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Use Canonical Link Meta Tag For Categories</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Use Canonical Link Meta Tag For Products</code>. Change their values to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Yes</code>. This will result in both product and product-category pages having canonical URLs, which significantly helps to prevent duplicate content.</li>
</ol>

<h4 id="use-robots-directives-to-prevent-duplicate-content">Use robots directives to prevent duplicate content</h4>
<p><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/">Robots directives</a> communicate your preferences regarding crawling and indexing to search engines. Especially for larger sites – which eCommerce stores often are – robots directives are essential to SEO success.</p>
<h5 id="apply-the-noindex-robots-directive-to-internal-search-result-pages">Apply the noindex robots directive to internal search result pages</h5>
<p>By default, internal search result pages in Magento 2 are robot-indexable. That’s bad from an SEO point of view, as this can lead to an enormous number of low-quality pages being crawled and indexed when there are links pointing to these pages (be these internal, or external).</p>
<p>We recommend adding the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex, follow</code> robots directive to all internal search result pages.</p>
<h5 id="apply-the-noindex-robots-directive-to-urls-with-query-parameters">Apply the noindex robots directive to URLs with query parameters</h5>
<p>eCommerce platforms are notorious for generating tremendous numbers of URLs because of query parameters. In Magento 2, URLs with query parameters are indexable by default. And that’s not a good thing.</p>
<p>For example, if you’re on a product-category page and you’re filtering on prices between $10 and $20, you’d be sent to to a URL such as <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/category/?price=10-20</code>. You don’t want that URL to be indexed, because it’s just showing a subset of the category products.</p>
<p>Sorted pages are another example: say you want to see the products in a category from cheapest to most expensive. You’d be sent to a URL such as <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/category/?price=10-20&amp;product_list_order=price&amp;product_list_dir=asc</code>. This page just shows the same products in a different order. This adds no value from an SEO point of view, so you don’t want these pages to be indexed either. The same goes for choosing to see more or fewer products on a page. That will add a query parameter to the URL too, leading to a URL such as: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/cateogry/?product_list_limit=30</code>.</p>
<p>There are no native settings in Magento to manage the robots directives for pages such as these, so you either need to have this custom-developed and/or add a <a href="https://marketplace.magento.com/catalogsearch/result/?q=Layered%20Navigation%20Filters&amp;ext_platform=Magento%202" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Layered Navigation Extension</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>
<p>Besides adding a noindex robots directive, we recommend preventing search engine crawlers from accessing URLs with these query parameters in the first place as described in <a href="/academy/magento-seo/#robotstxt">the robots.txt section</a>.</p>

<h4 id="xml-sitemap">Use an XML sitemap</h4>
<p><a href="/academy/xml-sitemap/">XML sitemaps</a> are an efficient way to communicate to search engines what pages they need to crawl and index.</p>
<p>The best practices around XML sitemaps are as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>Keep the XML Sitemap up to date with your website’s content.</li>
<li>Only include indexable pages.</li>
<li>Don’t list more than 50,000 URLs in a single XML Sitemap.</li>
<li>Make sure the sitemap’s (uncompressed) file size doesn’t exceed 50MB.</li>
<li>Reference the XML sitemap in the robots.txt file.</li>
</ul>
<p>Magento 2 comes with XML sitemap functionality. To configure the XML sitemap, log on to your Magento backend and navigate to:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Stores</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Configuration</code> (under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Settings</code>) &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Catalog</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">XML Sitemap</code>.</li>
<li>At the top of the page, select the Store View you want to adjust the settings for. In our example this is <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Default Store View</code>.</li>
</ol>
<h5 id="including-categories-products-and-cms-pages-in-xml-sitemap">Including categories, products, and CMS pages in XML sitemap</h5>

<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-10">Editing the XML Sitemap settings for categories, products and CMS pages in Magento 2.</figcaption></figure>
<p>By default, the options for categories, products and CMS pages are configured correctly. Google doesn’t use the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Frequency</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Priority</code> hints, so there’s no need to change anything there. By default, images are included in the XML Sitemap as well.</p>
<h5 id="xml-sitemap-generation-settings">XML sitemap generation Settings</h5>
<p>At the top of the page, select <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Default Config</code> for <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Store View</code> to manage the generation settings for the XML sitemap. The page will reload, and when you scroll down a little, you’ll see that you can define if, when, and how often the XML sitemap needs to be regenerated under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Generation Settings</code>.</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-11" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-xml-sitemap-settings@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-xml-sitemap-settings@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-magento-seo-xml-sitemap-settings@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-xml-sitemap-settings@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-xml-sitemap-settings@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-magento-seo-xml-sitemap-settings@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-xml-sitemap-settings@1x.png"/><img alt="Screenshot of the XML sitemap generation settings in Magento 2" class="image" height="332" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-xml-sitemap-settings@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-magento-seo-xml-sitemap-settings@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-magento-seo-xml-sitemap-settings@2x.png 2x" title="Editing the XML Sitemap generation settings in Magento 2." width="600"/></picture></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-11">Editing the XML Sitemap generation settings in Magento 2.</figcaption></figure>
<p>By default, the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Enabled</code> field is set to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">No</code>. Change this to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Yes</code>, because you want your XML sitemap to be regenerated automatically.</p>
<p>Setting <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Start Time</code> to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">02:00:00</code> will ensure the XML sitemap is regenerated at 2 AM. This is just an example; change this value to the quietest time of day for your store, as regenerating the XML sitemap can temporarily make your Magento store slower.</p>
<p>Keep the value of the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Frequency</code> field set to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Daily</code> to make sure the XML sitemap is regenerated every day. This too is just an example: if the content on your store only changes once a week, it makes more sense to regenerate the XML sitemap once a week.</p>
<h5 id="xml-sitemap-limits-and-robotstxt">XML sitemap: limits and robots.txt</h5>
<p>Things get interesting down below; under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Sitemap File Limits</code>, you’ll find the fields <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Maximum No of URLs Per File</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Maximum File Size</code>.</p>

<h5 id="xml-sitemap-maximum-no-of-urls-per-file">XML sitemap: Maximum No of URLs Per File</h5>
<p>For most Magento stores, the default <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Maximum No of URLs Per File</code> limit is correct as it aligns with SEO best practices.</p>
<section aria-label="Pro tip" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--protip-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Pro tip</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>In case you're running into indexing issues, it can be useful to temporarily split up your XML sitemap into more files so you can better pinpoint the indexing issues. For example, if you have 200,000 products split into four XML sitemaps and you're encountering indexing issues, checking the Sitemap's coverage report in Google Search Console doesn't tell you that much. Dividing those 200,000 products up in say 20 XML sitemaps gives you better insights.</p>
<p>Alternatively, you could create separate XML sitemaps per product category to help pinpoint indexing issues within product categories.</p>
</div></section>
<h5 id="xml-sitemap-maximum-file-size">XML sitemap: Maximum File Size</h5>
<p>You can raise the maximum file size to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">52428800</code>, as this equals 50 MB.</p>
<h5 id="xml-sitemap-enable-submission-to-robotstxt">XML sitemap: Enable Submission to Robots.txt</h5>
<p>Under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Search Engine Submission Settings</code> you’ll find the field <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Enable Submission to Robots.txt</code>, which has a default value of <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">No</code>. Switch this to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Yes</code>, as you want your robots.txt file to reference your XML sitemap.</p>
<p>Once this is all set up, it’s time to create the XML sitemap and configure its (re)generation settings. To do this, go to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Marketing</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Site Map</code> (under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">SEO &amp; Search</code>) and click the orange <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Add Sitemap</code> button.</p>

<h4 id="robotstxt">Robots.txt</h4>
<p>The robots.txt file tells crawlers the rules of engagement when it comes to accessing URLs on your site. For eCommerce stores – and so this goes for Magento stores too – it’s essential to leverage the robots.txt file to send search engine crawlers in the right direction, and keep them from touching the wrong URLs in an effort to</p>
<ul>
<li>Keep them from reaching potential <a href="/academy/crawler-traps/">crawler traps</a>.</li>
<li>Prevent <a href="/academy/duplicate-content/">duplicate content</a>.</li>
<li>Preserve precious <a href="/academy/crawl-budget/">crawl budget</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>While search engines claim to be good at recognizing crawler traps, they first need to go down one in order to find out only later that it is in fact a crawler trap.</p>
<p>Your Magento 2 store has a variety of URL patterns that you don’t want search engines to touch.</p>
<p>Here are the pages we recommend barring off from search engines:</p>
<ul>
<li>Internal search result pages: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/catalogsearch/</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/search/</code>. While we recommend adding the robots noindex directive (either the meta tag or HTTP header) to these pages as a second line of defense, the first line of defense is the robots.txt file.</li>
<li>Login pages <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/customer/account/login/</code> – login pages.</li>
<li>URLs containing <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">SID</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">PHPSESSID</code> parameters.</li>
<li>Filtering criteria such as <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">price</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">color</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">material</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">size</code>.</li>
</ul>
<p>This boils down to the following robots.txt:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">User-agent: *
Disallow: /catalogsearch/
Disallow: /search/
Disallow: /customer/account/login/
Disallow: /*?SID=
Disallow: /*?PHPSESSID=
Disallow: /*?price=
Disallow: /*&amp;price=
Disallow: /*?color=
Disallow: /*&amp;color=
Disallow: /*?material=
Disallow: /*&amp;material=
Disallow: /*?size=
Disallow: /*&amp;size=</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>We recommend including both ways a criterion is included in URLs. For example, with <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">price</code> that’s both <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/*?price=</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/*&amp;price=</code>.</p>
<h5 id="editing-your-robotstxt-file-in-magento-2">Editing your robots.txt file in Magento 2</h5>
<p>In order to edit your robots.txt file in Magento 2, navigate to:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Content</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Configuration</code> (Under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Design</code>).</li>
<li>There, edit the settings for <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Main Website</code> instead of the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Default Store View</code>, because the latter won’t allow you to change the robots.txt file.</li>
</ol>

<h4 id="faceted-navigation">Faceted navigation</h4>
<p>When we’re discussing crawling, indexing, and filters, we also need to touch on faceted navigation.</p>
<p>Faceted navigation refers to how visitors can search through your products. Product filters, for instance, are part of faceted navigation. While we recommended earlier that you add noindex robots directives to your filtered product pages, and also exclude them through robots.txt, what if people are searching for a certain subset of your products, say all black "Breaking Bad t-shirts"?</p>
<p>In that case, you need to make that subset of your products available for search engines by creating a subcategory for it. Do this for all product subsets where it makes sense to do so.</p>
<p>This way you can cater to a larger audience, just by being smart about your product categories.</p>
<section aria-label="Pro tip" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--protip-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Pro tip</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>You shouldn't apply the robots noindex directive <strong>together</strong> with canonicalizing these pages to other pages, as this sends a confusing signal to search engines.</p>

<h4 id="use-pagination-attributes">Use pagination attributes</h4>
<p>By default, Magento isn’t using the prev/next pagination attributes link on paginated pages to explain the relationship among these pages. While Google has said they no longer need this attribute to make sense of a series of paginated pages, there are still other search engines out there. There’s nothing against using it, and we recommend adding it if it only requires a small investment in time.</p>
<p>For example, if you’re already using a SEO plugin such as <a href="https://marketplace.magento.com/amasty-module-seo-toolkit-meta.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">SEO toolkit</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> that contains this functionality, and it’s just a matter of switching it on: go for it!</p>
<p>Stick to these best practices when it comes to pagination:</p>
<ul>
<li>Apply self-referencing canonicals to paginated pages.</li>
<li>Don't apply noindex robots directives to paginated pages.</li>
<li>Don't disallow paginated pages through your robots.txt.</li>
</ul>

<h3 class="toc" id="https">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#https" tabindex="-1"></a>HTTPS</h3>
<p>Serving your visitors over a secure connection is a precondition for success – and it’s fully supported by Magento. Especially for eCommerce stores, using HTTPS is essential, because it all comes down to earning – and keeping – your visitors’ trust.</p>
<h4 id="enabling-https-in-magento-2">Enabling HTTPS in Magento 2</h4>
<p>Configure your image settings by going to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Stores</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Configuration</code> (under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Settings</code>) &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">General</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Web</code>.</p>
<p>There, expand <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Base URLs (Secure)</code> and change the values of <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Secure Base URL</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Secure Base Link URL</code> so that they start with <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https</code>.</p>
<p>Furthermore, change the value of the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Use Secure URLs on Storefront</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Use Secure URLs in Admin</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Enable HTTP Strict Transport Security (HSTS)</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Upgrade Insecure Requests</code> fields to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Yes</code>.</p>

<h2 class="toc-hidden" id="faqs-magento">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#faqs-magento" tabindex="-1"></a>Frequently asked questions about Magento 2</h2>
<h3 id="-how-do-i-define-a-seo-friendly-url-structure">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#-how-do-i-define-a-seo-friendly-url-structure" tabindex="-1"></a>🧮 How do I define a SEO friendly URL structure?</h3>
<p>In the Magento backend, go to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Stores</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Configuration</code> (under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Settings</code>) &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">General</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Web</code>. There you can define <a href="/academy/magento-seo/#url-structure">the settings for the URL structure</a>. Specifically, pay attention to the sections <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Url Options</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Search Engine Optimization</code>.</p>
<p>If you'd like to remove the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">.html</code> extension, head over to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Stores</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Configuration</code> (under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Settings</code>) &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Catalog</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Catalog</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Search Engine Optimization</code>.</p>
<h3 id="-how-do-i-make-the-most-of-magento-2-for-seo">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#-how-do-i-make-the-most-of-magento-2-for-seo" tabindex="-1"></a>🤔 How do I make the most of Magento 2 for SEO?</h3>
<p>To get the best SEO performance out of Magento 2, focus on getting the configuration right for:</p>
<ul>
<li>URL structure</li>
<li>Meta information</li>
<li>Headings</li>
<li>Faceted navigation</li>
<li>Crawling &amp; Indexing</li>
<li>Site speed</li>
<li>HTTPS</li>
</ul>
<p>Getting this technical foundation right first puts you in the position to start winning at SEO.</p>
<h3 id="-can-i-make-magento-2-load-fast">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#-can-i-make-magento-2-load-fast" tabindex="-1"></a>🤷 Can I make Magento 2 load fast?</h3>
<p>Yes, but you'll need to put in some effort. First of all, you need a solid, Magento optimized hosting platform that supports Redis and Varnish. Ideally, it's CDN compatable and running on Nginx. You'll need to activate and configure Magento's Caching and Full Page Caching features, and we also highly recommend using Magento's Baler module for advanced JavaScript bundling.</p>
<h3 id="-can-i-configure-the-robotstxt-in-magento-2">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#-can-i-configure-the-robotstxt-in-magento-2" tabindex="-1"></a>🤖 Can I configure the robots.txt in Magento 2?</h3>
<p>In the Magento 2 backend, go to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Content</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Configuration</code> (Under Design). There you can edit the settings for 'Main Website' instead of the Default Store View, because the latter won’t allow you to change the robots.txt file. Wondering what to include there? Check out this <a href="/academy/magento-seo/#robotstxt">robots.txt example</a>.</p>

<h2 id="what-are-cookies">What are cookies?</h2>
<p>Cookies are small text files which are stored on the browser or hard drive of your Device when you visit a webpage or use an application. Cookies may be needed to show the webpage or application on your Device and are also used to enhance the user experience. Cookies cannot damage your Devices or the files saved on it. There are different types of cookies. Some cookies come directly from our website (first party cookies) and others come from third parties which place cookies on our site (third party cookies).</p>
<p>Cookies can be stored for varying lengths of time on your browser or device. Session cookies are deleted from your computer or device when you close your web-browser. Persistent cookies will remain stored on your computer or Device until deleted or until they reach their expiry date.</p>
<h2 id="how-do-we-use-cookies">How do we use cookies?</h2>
<h3 id="essential-technical-cookies">Essential (Technical) cookies</h3>
<p>We try to provide an advanced and user-friendly website that adapts automatically to needs and wishes of our visitors and users. To achieve this, we use technical cookies to i.e. show you our Website, to make it function correctly, to create your ContentKing account, to sign you in and to manage your requests. These technical cookies are necessary for our website to function properly.</p>
<h3 id="functional-cookies">Functional cookies</h3>
<p>We also use functional cookies to remember your preferences and settings (as username, password, language etc.) and to help you to use the Website efficiently and effectively. These functional cookies are not strictly necessary for the functioning of our Website, but they add functionality for you and enhance your experience.</p>

<p>We use analytical cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. These analytic cookies generate statistical and other information about website use by means of cookies, which are stored on users’ Devices. The information generated relating to our Website is used to create reports about the use of the Website. Analytical cookies may be placed without your consent, unless such cookies have an impact on your privacy. In such cases, prior consent will be asked for.</p>
<h3 id="google-analytics">Google Analytics</h3>
<p>We use Google Analytics cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. With these analytical cookies a permanent cookie is saved on your device to register your use of the Website. Google Analytics analyses this data and provides us with the results. <p>Companies such as LinkedIn, Twitter and Facebook may share your personal data outside the European Economic Area. <h3 id="pixels">Pixels</h3>
<p>We also place pixels of third parties. A pixel keeps track of your surfing behavior and how you use the Website. These data are aggregated and give us information about the target group that visits our website. Based on this information, we can show an advertisement to a specific or similar target group on the website of the third party that produced the pixel.</p>
