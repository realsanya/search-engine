<h1 class="heading__title">Meta Robots Tag Guide</h1>
<div class="heading__params">
<span aria-hidden="true" class="heading__label">
										Last updated:
									</span>
<span aria-label="The date of last update" class="heading__caption">
										August  6, 2021
									</span>
</div>
</div>
<div class="post-detail__text typo">
<section aria-label="Meta Robots Tags in short" class="highlighted-box highlighted-box--with-background typo-block"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">
<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/meta-robots--reversed.svg)"></i></span>Meta Robots Tags in short
	</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Meta robots tags are an essential tool to improve search engine’s crawling and indexing behavior, and to control your snippets in the SERP.</p>
<p>In this article we'll explain how to do this, how interpretation and support differs per search engines and how the meta robots tag relates to the X-Robots-Tag and robots.txt file.</p>
</div>
</section>

<h2 id="what-is-a-meta-robots-tag">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-is-a-meta-robots-tag" tabindex="-1"></a>What is the meta robots tag?</h2>
<p>The meta robots tag gives site owners power over search engines’ crawling and indexing behavior and how their snippets are served in search engine result pages (SERPs).</p>
<p>The meta robots tag goes into the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;head&gt;</code> section of your HTML and is just one of the <a href="/academy/meta-tags/">meta tags</a> that live there.</p>
<p>Arguably the most well-known meta robots tag is the one telling search engines <strong>not to index</strong> a page:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex,follow" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>You can provide the same instructions by including them in the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">HTTP header</code> using the <a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#the-x-robots-tag-http-header">X-Robots-Tag</a>. The X-Robots-Tag is often used to prevent non-HTML content such as PDF and images from being indexed.</p>
<h3 id="terminology">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#terminology" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots directives</h3>
<p>We prefer to talk about <em>meta robots directives</em> instead of <em>meta robots tags</em>, because calling them “meta robots tags” is incorrect (see “anatomy of the meta element” below).</p>
<section aria-label="Careful with your robots lingo" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-background"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Careful with your robots lingo</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Meta robots directives are not to be confused with <a href="/academy/robotstxt/">robots.txt directives</a>. These are two different ways of communicating with search engines about different aspects of their crawling and indexing behavior. But they do <a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#meta-robots-tag-vs-robotstxt-vs-x-robots-header">influence one another</a>, as we’ll see further down the article.</p>
</div></section>
<h3 id="anatomy-of-the-meta-element">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#anatomy-of-the-meta-element" tabindex="-1"></a>Anatomy of the meta element</h3>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-meta-element@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-meta-element@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-meta-element@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-meta-element@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-meta-element@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-meta-element@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-meta-element@1x.png"/><img alt="A visual of the anatomy of the meta robots tag element" class="image" height="187" src="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-meta-element@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-meta-element@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-meta-element@2x.png 2x" title="A visual of the anatomy of the meta robots tag element" width="600"/></picture></div>
<p>Let’s use the meta robots directive example mentioned above to explain what’s what:</p>
<ul>
<li>The entire code snippet is called the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">meta element</code>.</li>
<li>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/&gt;</code> are the opening and closing tags.</li>
<li>There’s an attribute called <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">name</code> with the value <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">robots</code>. <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">robots</code> applies to all crawlers but can be replaced with a specific user-agent.</li>
<li>And then there’s an attribute called <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">content</code> with the value <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex,follow</code>. <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex,follow</code> can be replaced with other directives.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="why-you-should-care-about-the-meta-robots-tag">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#why-you-should-care-about-the-meta-robots-tag" tabindex="-1"></a>Why is it important?</h2>
<p>Firstly, meta robots directives give you much-needed control over search engines’ crawling and indexing behavior. Without any direction, search engines will try to crawl and index all the content they come across. That’s their default behavior.</p>
<p>Secondly, search engines will generate a snippet for your URLs when they rank them. They take your meta description as input, though they will often instead come up with their own snippet—based on your page’s content—if they think it’ll perform better.</p>

<p>Now, let’s look at a few applications of the meta robots directives in protecting your SEO performance:</p>
<ul>
<li>Prevent a <a href="/academy/duplicate-content/">duplicate content issue</a> by applying the meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive to PPC landing pages and on-site search result pages. Note that robots directives will not pass on any authority and relevancy like the <a href="/academy/canonical/">canonical URL</a> would.</li>
<li>Prevent search engines from indexing content that should never be indexed because you’re providing discounts or some other offer that you don’t want to be available to the entire world.</li>
<li>Remove sensitive content that has been indexed: if search engines have indexed content they should never have indexed in the first place, apply the meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive to <a href="/academy/google-remove-urls/#sensitive-content">remove the content</a> from their indices. You can use the same technique when <a href="/academy/crawler-traps/#how-do-you-fix-and-avoid-this-crawler-trap">fixing crawler traps</a>.</li>
<li>Selectively apply the meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive to <a href="/academy/discontinued-products/#keep-alive-as-long-as-search-demand-is-high">discontinued products</a> to keep providing users with a good user experience.</li>
</ul>
<section aria-label="Staging environments" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--protip-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Staging environments</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>We deliberately have not listed making sure staging environments don’t get indexed by search engines as a use case.</p>
<p>That's because using <a href="/academy/protect-staging-environment/#method-1-http-auth">HTTP Auth is a much better solution</a>, as it prevents access from <strong>both users and search engines</strong> and keeps the meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive from carrying over to the production site.</p>
</div></section>
<h2 id="meta-robots-syntax">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#meta-robots-syntax" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots syntax explained</h2>
<p>Before we dig in deeper, let’s cover some of the basics:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#not-case-sensitive">The syntax is not case sensitive</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#separate-directives-commas">Separating directives with commas is required for Google</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#spaces-after-commas">Spaces after commas are not required</a></li>
</ul>
<h3 id="not-case-sensitive">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#not-case-sensitive" tabindex="-1"></a>The syntax is not case sensitive</h3>
<p>Meta robots directives are not case sensitive, meaning the examples below are all valid:</p>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex,follow" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="ROBOTS" content="noindex,follow" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="NOINDEX,FOLLOW" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<h3 id="separate-directives-commas">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#separate-directives-commas" tabindex="-1"></a>Separating directives with commas for Google</h3>
<p>For Google, you need to separate directives with a comma. A space doesn’t cut it:</p>
<section aria-label="Bad example of code" class="code-box code-box--negative"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex follow" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<h3 id="spaces-after-commas">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#spaces-after-commas" tabindex="-1"></a>Spaces after commas not required</h3>
<p>You’re not required to use spaces after commas between directives. So, the examples below are both valid:</p>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex,follow" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<section aria-label="Good example of code" class="code-box code-box--positive"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex, follow" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>Now, let’s move on to the directives themselves!</p>
<h2 id="the-meta-robots-tag-directives">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#the-meta-robots-tag-directives" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots directives in detail</h2>
<p>In this section we’ll cover the most common meta directives you’ll come across in the wild and what exactly they mean. We'll focus primarily on directives support from Google, as they are the dominant search engine.</p>
<p>Here are the directives we'll cover:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#all">all</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#index">index</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#index-follow">index,follow</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#noindex">noindex</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#noindex-follow">noindex,follow</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#noindex-nofollow">noindex,nofollow</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#none">none</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#noarchive">noarchive</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#nosnippet">nosnippet</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#max-snippet">max-snippet</a></li>
</ul>
<h3 id="all">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#all" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots “all”</h3>
<p>By default, search engines will crawl and index any content they come across, unless specified otherwise. If you want to explicitly define that this is allowed, you can do so with the following directive:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="all" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<h3 id="index">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#index" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots “index”</h3>
<p>While not necessary because it’s default behavior, if you want to make it explicit to search engines that they are allowed to index a page, you can do so with the meta robots directive below.</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="index" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<h3 id="index-follow">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#index-follow" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots “index,follow”</h3>
<p>Oftentimes the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">index</code> directive is combined with the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">follow</code> directive, leading to:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="index,follow" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>These directives essentially mean the same thing as the one above that only states <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">index</code>, since <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">follow</code> is default search engine behavior as well.</p>
<h3 id="noindex">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#noindex" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots “noindex”</h3>
<p>The meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive tells search engines not to index a page. Here’s what the meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive looks like:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>The example above tells search engines they shouldn’t index the page, but they should feel free to follow all its links, because it’s not explicitly stated they shouldn’t.</p>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive carries a lot of weight, so when search engines find it, they are quick to remove content from their index. The other side of the coin is that it’s tough to get this content re-indexed when for example you’ve accidentally applied the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive.</p>

<h3 id="noindex-follow">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#noindex-follow" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots “noindex,follow”</h3>
<p>You’ll frequently find meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> being combined with the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">follow</code> directive. It tells search engines not to index the page—but that it’s fine to follow the links:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex,follow" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>At the risk of sounding like a broken record, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex" /&gt;</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex,follow" /&gt;</code> mean the same thing, since <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">follow</code> is default search engine crawler behavior.</p>
<h3 id="noindex-nofollow">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#noindex-nofollow" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots “noindex,nofollow”</h3>
<p>You can also combine the meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive with a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">nofollow</code> meta directive (not to be confused with the <a href="/academy/nofollow/">nofollow link attribute</a>):</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex,nofollow" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex,nofollow</code> combination tells search engines not to index the page and not to follow the links on the page, meaning no <a href="/academy/authority/#link-authority">link authority</a> should be passed on either.</p>
<section aria-label="”noindex” becomes “noindex,nofollow” over time" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--protip-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">”noindex” becomes “noindex,nofollow” over time</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Search engines significantly decrease re-crawling of a noindexed page, essentially leading to a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex, nofollow</code> situation, because links on a page that doesn’t get crawled are not followed.</p>
</div></section>
<h3 id="none">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#none" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots “none”</h3>
<p>The meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">none</code> directive is actually a shortcut for <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex,nofollow</code>, which we covered just above. Here’s what the meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">none</code> directive looks like:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="none" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>It’s not used very often, and folks often think it means the exact opposite: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">index,follow</code>.</p>
<p>So be careful with this one!</p>
<h3 id="noarchive">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#noarchive" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots “noarchive”</h3>
<p>The meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noarchive</code> directive prevents search engines from presenting a cached version of a page in the SERP. If you don’t specify the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noarchive</code> directive, search engines may just go ahead and serve a cached version of the page. So again, this is an opt-out directive.</p>
<p>Here’s what the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noarchive</code> directive looks like:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="noarchive" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>It’s frequently combined with other directives though. For example, you’ll commonly see it used together with the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">nofollow</code> directives:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex,nofollow,noarchive" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>This means search engines shouldn’t index the page, shouldn’t follow any of its links and shouldn’t cache the page either.</p>
<h3 id="nosnippet">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#nosnippet" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots “nosnippet”</h3>
<p>The meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">nosnippet</code> directive tells search engines not to show a text snippet (usually drawn from the <a href="/academy/meta-description/">meta description</a>) or video preview for the page.</p>
<p>Here’s what the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">nosnippet</code> directive looks like:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="nosnippet" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>If we were to apply the meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">nosnippet</code> directive to our redirects article, the snippet would then look like this:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-nosnippet-example@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-nosnippet-example@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-nosnippet-example@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-nosnippet-example@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Screenshot of a SERP snippet where the nosnippet directive has been used" class="image" height="95" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-nosnippet-example@1x.png" title="Screenshot of a SERP snippet where the nosnippet directive has been used" width="600"/></picture></div>
<p>Search engines may still show an image thumbnail if they think this results in a better user experience. For Google, this applies to regular Web Search, Google Images, and Google Discover. The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">nosnippet</code> directive also functions as a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noarchive</code> directive.</p>
<p>If the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">nosnippet</code> directive is not included, Google will generate a text snippet and video preview on its own.</p>
<section aria-label="Preventing certain content from being used for a snippet" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--protip-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Preventing certain content from being used for a snippet</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>On Google specifically, you can prevent some of your page’s content from showing up in a snippet by using the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">data-nosnippet</code> HTML attribute. While it’s not a meta robots directive, it is closely related to them, so we need to touch on this here.</p>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">data-nosnippet</code> HTML attribute can be used on <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">span</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">div</code>, and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">section</code> elements. Here’s an example:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;p&gt;This may be shown in a snippet, while 
&lt;span data-nosnippet&gt;this will not be shown in a snippet&lt;/span&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>Learn more about the <a href="https://developers.google.com/search/docs/advanced/robots/robots_meta_tag#data-nosnippet-attr" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">data-nosnippet attribute here</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>
</div></section>
<h3 id="max-snippet">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#max-snippet" tabindex="-1"></a>Meta robots “max-snippet”</h3>
<p>The meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">max-snippet</code> directive tells search engines to limit the page’s snippet (generally drawn from the page’s meta description) to a specified number of characters.</p>
<p>Here's an example where the snippet will have a maximum length of 50 characters:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="max-snippet:50" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<h4 id="meta-robots-max-snippet0">Meta robots “max-snippet:0”</h4>
<p>When you specify <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">max-snippet:0</code>, you’re telling search engines not to show a snippet—essentially the same as the meta robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">nosnippet</code> directive we just described above:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="max-snippet:0" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<h4 id="meta-robots-max-snippet-1">Meta robots “max-snippet:-1”</h4>
<p>When you specify <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">max-snippet:-1</code>, you’re explicitly telling search engines they can determine the snippet’s length themselves, which is their default behavior:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="max-snippet:-1" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<section aria-label="Impact of the European Copyright Directive" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--protip-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Impact of the European Copyright Directive</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Since October 2019, sites classified as “European press publications” are shown in Google without a snippet by default. You can opt-in to having your snippets shown by using the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">max-snippet</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">max-image-preview</code> directives, or you can choose to remove your site from the list of European press publications <a href="https://search.google.com/search-console/settings/eucd" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">via Google Search Console</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>. Learn more about this <a href="https://support.google.com/webmasters/answer/9405176?hl=en" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">here</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>
<p>If you’re using the Yoast SEO plugin on WordPress, you’ll find that it automatically opts in when you include the following snippet:
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta name="robots" content="index, follow, max-snippet:-1, max-image-preview:large, max-video-preview:-1" /&gt;</code></p>
</div></section>
<h3 id="less-important-meta-robots-directives">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#less-important-meta-robots-directives" tabindex="-1"></a>Less-important meta robots directives</h3>
<p>Now we’ve arrived at the less important meta robots directives, which we’ll only touch on briefly.</p>
<p>What goes for the other meta robots directives goes for these too: if they aren’t defined, search engines will do as they please.</p>
<p>Here’s what the directives signal to search engines:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">unavailable_after</code>: "remove a page from your index after a specific date". The date should be specified in a widely adopted format, such as for example <a href="https://www.iso.org/iso-8601-date-and-time-format.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">ISO 8601</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>. The directive is ignored if no valid date/time is specified. By default there is no expiration date for content. It’s basically a timed <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive, so be careful when using it.</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noimageindex</code>: "don’t index the images on this page".</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">max-image-preview</code>: "define a maximum size for the image preview for a page, with possible values: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">none</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">standard</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">large</code>".</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">max-video-preview</code>: "define a maximum for the preview length of videos on the page".</li>
<li>
<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">notranslate</code>: "don't offer a translated version of the page in your search results".</li>
</ul>
<section aria-label="Useful resources" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-background highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--resources-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Useful resources</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<ul>
<li><a href="https://developers.google.com/search/docs/advanced/robots/robots_meta_tag" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Google’s Meta Robots documentation</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
</ul>
</div></section>
<h2 id="combining-meta-robots-tag-directives">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#combining-meta-robots-tag-directives" tabindex="-1"></a>How can you combine meta robots directives?</h2>
<p>In addition to being able to combine directives, you can also provide directives to different crawlers. Each crawler will use the sum of the directives provided to them, that is: they stack.</p>
<p>To illustrate how, let’s look at an example:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="nofollow" /&gt;
&lt;meta name="googlebot" content="noindex" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>These directives are interpreted as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>Google: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex,nofollow</code>
</li>
<li>Other search engines: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">nofollow</code>
</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="conflicting-meta-robots-directives">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#conflicting-meta-robots-directives" tabindex="-1"></a>How do search engines interpret conflicting directives?</h2>
<p>As you can imagine, when you start stacking directives, it’s easy to mess up. If a scenario presents itself where there are conflicting directives, Google will <strong>default to the most restrictive one</strong>.</p>
<p>Take for example the following directives:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="index" /&gt;
&lt;meta name="googlebot" content="noindex" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p><strong>Verdict:</strong> Google will err on the side of caution and <strong>not index</strong> the page.</p>
<p>But, the way conflicting directives are interpreted can differ among search engines. Let’s take another example:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;meta name="robots" content="index" /&gt;
&lt;meta name="robots" content="noindex" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>Google will not index this page, but Yandex will <strong>do the exact opposite</strong> and index it.</p>
<p>So keep this in mind, and make sure that your robots directives work right for the search engines that are important to you.</p>
<h2 id="the-x-robots-tag-http-header">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#the-x-robots-tag-http-header" tabindex="-1"></a>X-Robots-Tag—the HTTP header equivalent</h2>
<p>Non-HTML files such as images and PDF files don’t have an HTML source that you can include a meta robots directive in. If you want to signal your crawling and indexing preferences to search engines for these files, your best bet is to use the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">X-Robots-Tag</code> HTTP header.</p>
<p>Let’s briefly touch on HTTP headers.</p>
<p>When a visitor or search engine requests a page from a web server, and the page exists, the web server typically responds with three things:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<strong>HTTP Status Code</strong>: the three-digit response to the client’s request (e.g. <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">200 OK</code>).</li>
<li>
<strong>HTTP Headers</strong>: headers containing for example the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">content-type</code> that was returned and instructions on how long the client should cache the response.</li>
<li>
<strong>HTTP Body</strong>: the body (e.g. <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">HTML</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">CSS</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">JavaScript</code> etc.), which is used to render and display the page in a browser.</li>
</ol>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">X-Robots-Tag</code> can be included in the HTTP Headers. Here’s a screenshot of a page’s HTTP response headers taken from Chrome Web Inspector, for a page that contains a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">X-Robots-Tag: noindex</code>:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-screenshot-x-robots-tag@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-screenshot-x-robots-tag@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-screenshot-x-robots-tag@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-screenshot-x-robots-tag@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Screenshot of Google Web Inspector HTTP Response that includes X-Robots-Tag noindex" class="image" height="536" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-meta-robots-tag-screenshot-x-robots-tag@1x.png" title="Screenshot of Google Web Inspector HTTP Response that includes X-Robots-Tag noindex" width="600"/></picture></div>
<p>So how does this work in practice?</p>
<h3 id="configuring-x-robots-tag-on-apache">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#configuring-x-robots-tag-on-apache" tabindex="-1"></a>Configuring X-Robots-Tag on Apache</h3>
<p>For example, if you're using the Apache web server, and you’d like to add a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex,nofollow X-Robots-Tag</code> to the HTTP response for all of your PDF files, add the following snippet to your <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">.htaccess</code> file or <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">httpd.conf</code> file:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;Files ~ "\.pdf$"&gt;
Header set X-Robots-Tag "noindex,nofollow"
&lt;/Files&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>Or perhaps you want to make images of file types <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">PNG</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">JPG</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">JPEG</code>, and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">GIF</code> non-indexable:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;Files ~ "\.(png|jpe?g|gif)$"&gt;
Header set X-Robots-Tag "noindex"
&lt;/Files&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>

<h3 id="configuring-x-robots-tag-on-nginx">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#configuring-x-robots-tag-on-nginx" tabindex="-1"></a>Configuring X-Robots-Tag on nginx</h3>
<p>Meanwhile on the nginx web server, you need to edit a site’s <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">.conf</code> file.</p>
<p>To remove all PDF files from search engines’ indices across an entire site, use this:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">location ~* \.pdf$ {
  add_header X-Robots-Tag "noindex, nofollow";
}</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>And to noindex the images, use this:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">location ~* \.(png|jpe?g|gif)$ {
  add_header X-Robots-Tag "noindex";
}</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>Note that tweaking your web server configuration can negatively impact your entire website’s SEO performance. Unless you're comfortable with making changes to your web server's configuration, it’s best to leave these changes to your server administrator.</p>

<h2 id="best-practices">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#best-practices" tabindex="-1"></a>SEO best practices for robots directives</h2>
<p>Stick to these best practices around robots directives:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<strong>Avoid conflicting robots directives</strong>: avoid using both meta robots and X-Robots-Tag directives to signal your crawling and indexing preferences for your pages, as it’s easy to mess up and send conflicting instructions. It’s fine to use meta robots directives on pages and X-Robots-Tag for your images and PDFs though—just make sure you're not using both methods of delivering robots directive instructions on the same file.</li>
<li>
<strong>Don’t disallow content with important robots directives</strong>: if you disallow content using your robots.txt, search engines won’t be able to pick up that content’s preferred robots directives. Say for example you apply the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive to a page, and go on to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">disallow</code> access to that same page. Search engines won’t be able to see the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code>, and they may still keep the page in their index for a long time.</li>
<li>
<strong>Don’t combine noindex directive with canonical URL</strong>: a page that has both a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive and a canonical to another page is confusing for search engines. In rare cases, this results in the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> being carried over to the canonical target. <a href="/academy/canonical/faq/noindex/#robots-noindex-and-canonical-to-other-page">Learn more</a>.</li>
<li>
<strong>Don’t apply noindex directive to paginated pages</strong>: because search engines (Google especially) understand paginated pages well, they treat them differently and won’t see them as duplicate content. And keep in mind that in practice, over time a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive becomes a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex,nofollow</code>, closing the door on a discovery path for the content that’s linked through paginated pages. <a href="/academy/pagination/#dont-noindex-paginated-pages">Learn more</a>.</li>
<li>
<strong>No hreflang to pages with noindex</strong>: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">hreflang</code> signals to search engines which content variants are available to different audiences, sending a signal that these need to be indexed. Therefore, avoid referencing pages that have a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive.</li>
<li>
<strong>Don’t include pages with noindex in XML sitemap</strong>: pages that shouldn’t be indexed, shouldn’t be included in your XML sitemap either, since the XML sitemap is used to signal to search engines which pages they should crawl and index.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="what-are-cookies">What are cookies?</h2>
<p>Cookies are small text files which are stored on the browser or hard drive of your Device when you visit a webpage or use an application. Cookies may be needed to show the webpage or application on your Device and are also used to enhance the user experience. Cookies cannot damage your Devices or the files saved on it. There are different types of cookies. Some cookies come directly from our website (first party cookies) and others come from third parties which place cookies on our site (third party cookies).</p>
<p>Cookies can be stored for varying lengths of time on your browser or device. Session cookies are deleted from your computer or device when you close your web-browser. Persistent cookies will remain stored on your computer or Device until deleted or until they reach their expiry date.</p>
<h2 id="how-do-we-use-cookies">How do we use cookies?</h2>
<h3 id="essential-technical-cookies">Essential (Technical) cookies</h3>
<p>We try to provide an advanced and user-friendly website that adapts automatically to needs and wishes of our visitors and users. To achieve this, we use technical cookies to i.e. show you our Website, to make it function correctly, to create your ContentKing account, to sign you in and to manage your requests. These technical cookies are necessary for our website to function properly.</p>
<h3 id="functional-cookies">Functional cookies</h3>
<p>We also use functional cookies to remember your preferences and settings (as username, password, language etc.) and to help you to use the Website efficiently and effectively. These functional cookies are not strictly necessary for the functioning of our Website, but they add functionality for you and enhance your experience.</p>
<h3 id="analytical-cookies">Analytical cookies</h3>
<p>We use analytical cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. These analytic cookies generate statistical and other information about website use by means of cookies, which are stored on users’ Devices. The information generated relating to our Website is used to create reports about the use of the Website. Analytical cookies may be placed without your consent, unless such cookies have an impact on your privacy. In such cases, prior consent will be asked for.</p>
<h3 id="google-analytics">Google Analytics</h3>
<p>We use Google Analytics cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. With these analytical cookies a permanent cookie is saved on your device to register your use of the Website. Google Analytics analyses this data and provides us with the results. This way, we obtain insight into the traffic of the Website and the way in which the Website is used. Based on this information we are able to make specific adjustments to the Website.</p>
<p>The information that we obtain is transferred to Google and stored by Google on servers outside the European Economic Area. We have entered into a data processing agreement with Google in which agreements have been made regarding the processing of personal data.</p>
<p>If you would like more information about analytical cookies and their expiry date, please visit the Google Analytics <a href="https://developers.google.com/analytics/devguides/collection/gajs/cookie-usage">information page</a>. Google also offers an <a href="https://tools.google.com/dlpage/gaoptout?hl=en">opt-out option</a> for data collection within the scope of Google Analytics.</p>
<h3 id="marketing-cookies">Marketing cookies</h3>
<p>We use third party marketing cookies in order to follow your internet browsing behavior and collect data and information on your browsing behavior from various websites you visited. This information is used to make the content of the displayed advertisements as relevant as possible, and to limit repetition of the same advertisements.</p>
<h3 id="social-plug-ins">Social plug-ins</h3>
<p>By using the Website you can get access to social media websites, such as Facebook, Twitter and LinkedIn. These buttons come with a code which has been made by these networks themselves. Using these so called social plug-ins you can login to your social media profile and subsequently share information from the Website with others. With these social plug-ins third party marketing cookies are saved on your Devices. These cookies serve the purpose of optimizing your user experience.</p>
<p>Companies such as LinkedIn, Twitter and Facebook may share your personal data outside the European Economic Area. Please read our Privacy Policy <a href="/legal/privacy-policy/">here</a> and the respective social network’s privacy statement to see how these companies treat your (personal) data.</p>
<h3 id="pixels">Pixels</h3>
<p>We also place pixels of third parties. A pixel keeps track of your surfing behavior and how you use the Website. These data are aggregated and give us information about the target group that visits our website. Based on this information, we can show an advertisement to a specific or similar target group on the website of the third party that produced the pixel.</p>
<h2 id="disable-or-delete-cookies">Disable or delete cookies</h2>
<p>When you first visit the Website, we ask you to give consent for cookies that are not necessary or functional and that have an impact on your privacy. If you do not give consent, these cookies will not be placed. In that case, you may not be able to use all the functionalities on our Website.</p>
<p>You can configure your browser so that you do not receive any cookies the next time you use the website. You can read here how you can remove different types of cookies in different browsers. Please consult the help-function of your browser if your browser is not listed below.</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://support.google.com/chrome/answer/95647?co=GENIE.Platform%3DDesktop&amp;hl=en-GB">Chrome</a></li>
<li><a href="https://support.mozilla.org/en-US/kb/clear-cookies-and-site-data-firefox">Firefox</a></li>
<li><a href="https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-edge/delete-cookies-in-microsoft-edge-63947406-40ac-c3b8-57b9-2a946a29ae09">Edge</a></li>
<li><a href="https://support.apple.com/guide/safari/manage-cookies-and-website-data-sfri11471/mac">Safari</a></li>
</ul>
<p>Note: Refusing or deleting cookies only affects the Device and the browser on which you perform this action. If you use different Devices and/or browsers you will need to repeat the above described actions on these Devices and/or browsers.</p>
