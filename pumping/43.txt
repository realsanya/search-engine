<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/ux-seo--reversed.svg)"></i></span>The Role of UX in SEO in short

<p>Here’s what a great user experience looks like:</p>
<ul>
<li>Give users a satisfying answer to their question.</li>
<li>Do this blazingly fast…</li>
<li>…through a site that’s easy to use on any device.</li>

<ol>
<li>Hit search intent</li>
<li>Win the actual click</li>
<li>Answer the visitor’s query…</li>
<li>While making sure pages load fast, the website is usable on any device, and the usability is good.</li>
</ol>
<p>The key to your users’ and search engines’ hearts is delivering a great user experience.</p>

<h2 id="how-does-ux-affect-seo">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-does-ux-affect-seo" tabindex="-1"></a>How does UX affect SEO?</h2>
<p>Over the last few years, user experience (UX for short) has become a major ranking factor for Google. Therefore, more and more SEO specialists have gained an interest in UX.</p>
<p>We’re introducing two commonly misunderstood terms here, so let’s first explain what we mean by them:</p>
<p>1) When we say “<em>ranking factor</em>”, we mean an element that influences your rankings.</p>
<p>2) When talking about “<em>user experience</em>” within the context of the web, we define as a great user experience one that:</p>
<ul>
<li>Gives users a satisfying answer to their question.</li>
<li>Does this blazingly fast…</li>
<li>…through a site that’s easy to use on any device.</li>
</ul>
<p>So: UX is important to SEO. But how do you go about delivering a great user experience that doesn’t hurt the rest of your SEO performance?</p>
<p>That’s exactly what we’ll cover in this article!</p>

<h2 id="satisfying-answer">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#satisfying-answer" tabindex="-1"></a>Giving users a satisfying answer to their question</h2>
<p>So users have a question, and they want a satisfying answer.</p>
<p>A satisfying answer means one that massively helps their quest for answers—or maybe even ends it, because you’ve answered their question.</p>
<p>To figure out how to craft the answer your users are looking for, you need to understand their “<em>search intent</em>”.</p>
<p>Search intent is the “why” behind a query—why is this person searching? What problem are they looking to solve? Figuring out the search intent behind a query is the first step to coming up with a satisfying answer. Failing this step means you’ll fail at it all: delivering a great user experience, driving organic traffic, and making sales.</p>
<p>After you’ve figured out the search intent behind the query, the next step is to create the answer—in the form of content. That content can be packaged in different ways; think beyond just text. Other content types such as images, videos, presentations, podcasts, and helpful tools can play an important role too.</p>

<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/michelle-wilding-baker@1x.avif, /media/people/michelle-wilding-baker@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/michelle-wilding-baker@1x.webp, /media/people/michelle-wilding-baker@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Michelle Wilding-Baker" class="image" height="200" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/michelle-wilding-baker@1x.jpg" srcset="/media/people/michelle-wilding-baker@1x.jpg, /media/people/michelle-wilding-baker@2x.jpg 2x" title="Michelle Wilding-Baker" width="200"/></picture></span></span></div>
<div class="quote-box__content typo">
<blockquote class="quote-box__blockquote">
<p>Gone are the days of optimising copy and code just for crawlers. Search engines care deeply about providing users with quality experiences, so SEOs must put their audience needs first to succeed in and beyond the SERP.</p>
<p>Cater to every aspect by providing:</p>
<ul>
<li>An easy to navigate, speedy site optimised for all devices.</li>
<li>Relevant content that meets the intent of the searcher at the correct stage of their journey - trigger events, awareness/research phases, comparison and conversion.</li>
</ul>
<p>By all means keep the crawlers happy with flawless Technical SEO, but don’t forget about the actual eyeballs on screens. Turn their click-through into a ‘long click’ - if they are satisfied with your content and experience, they will most likely stay on page (or your site) for longer.</p>

<h3 id="understanding-search-intent">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#understanding-search-intent" tabindex="-1"></a>Understanding search intent</h3>
<p>We’ve drawn up a framework here to classify search intent. We’ve distinguished the different search intents and categorized them according to the query types you may already be familiar with:</p>
<ol>
<li>Informational</li>
<li>Navigational</li>
<li>Commercial</li>
<li>Transactional</li>
</ol>
<p>Note that a framework like this is never finished, and is not meant to be exhausting. It's meant to help you understand search intent and the different ways visitors search.</p>
<h3 id="informational-queries">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#informational-queries" tabindex="-1"></a>Informational queries</h3>
<p>Visitors make informational queries when they want to learn and research.</p>
<p>These types of queries are typical for visitors in the “<em>awareness</em>” phase: the first phase of the buying journey.</p>
<h4 id="research-intent">1. Research intent</h4>
<p>When visitors have a research intent, they’re looking to learn something. They want to soak up knowledge. This can mean they’re seeking a simple how-to video explaining, for example, how to tie a tie, but it can also mean they want a deep dive on a historical event.</p>
<p>This search intent types often triggers videos or longread articles.</p>
<p>Example query: “<em>how to make pancakes</em>”</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-research-en@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-research-en@1x.avif, /media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-research-en@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-research-en@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><source srcset="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-research-en@1x.webp, /media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-research-en@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-research-en@1x.png"/><img alt="A screenshot of the search results for the query “how to make pancakes”" class="image" height="510" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-research-en@1x.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-research-en@1x.png, /media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-research-en@2x.png 2x" title="Search results for the query “how to make pancakes” showing a featured snippet and videos of making pancakes." width="600"/></picture></div>
<p>The top ranking article <a href="https://www.allrecipes.com/recipe/21014/good-old-fashioned-pancakes/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Good Old Fashioned Pancakes</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> has a featured snippet, so it’s getting massive exposure.</p>
<p>What makes this content so great?</p>
<p>It includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>A detailed recipe that’s been reviewed over 11,000 times.</li>
<li>A video explaining how the pancakes are made.</li>

<h4 id="quick-answer-intent">2. Quick-answer intent</h4>
<p>Visitors want a quick answer. These are often 0-click searches, meaning that the visitor’s query is answered in the SERP and does not result in an actual click to a website.</p>
<p>Example query: “<em>weather new york</em>”</p>

<h4 id="news-intent">3. News intent</h4>
<p>Visitors are looking for results about a recent event.</p>
<p>Example query: “<em>uber Q2 2019 earnings</em>”</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><img alt="A screenshot of the search results for the query “uber Q2 2019 earnings”." class="image" height="580" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-news-en.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-news-en.png, /media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-news-en@2x.png 2x" title="Search results for the query “uber Q2 2019 earnings” showing recent news articles about the Q2 2019 earnings report." width="600"/></div>

<h4 id="visual-intent">4. Visual intent</h4>
<p>Visitors are looking for inspiration or examples.</p>
<p>Example query: “<em>decoration boy room</em>”</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><img alt="A screenshot of the search results for the query “decoration boy room”." class="image" height="540" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-visual-en.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-visual-en.png, /media/content/contentking-ux-seo-search-intent-visual-en@2x.png 2x" title="Search results for the query “decoration boy room” showing Google shopping results and images of boys rooms." width="600"/></div>

<h3 id="navigational-queries">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#navigational-queries" tabindex="-1"></a>Navigational queries</h3>
<p>Here visitors <em>know</em> what brand they’re looking for; they’re just using a query to navigate.</p>
<h4 id="branded-intent">5. Branded intent</h4>
<p>Visitors are aware of a brand and are specifically seeking something related to that brand.</p>
<p>These types of queries are typical for visitors in the “interest” buying-journey phase.</p>
<p>Example query: “<em>nikola tesla wikipedia</em>”</p>

<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#commercial-queries" tabindex="-1"></a>Commercial queries</h3>
<p>Visitors who are using commercial queries already know what they want. They’re just not yet sure where they’ll buy it. These queries are typical for users in the “consideration” buying-journey phase.</p>
<h4 id="commercial-intent">7. Commercial intent</h4>
<p>Visitors are comparing products or services. Often these queries include words like “review” or “best.”</p>

<h3 id="the-impact-of-word-count-on-satisfying-answers">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#the-impact-of-word-count-on-satisfying-answers" tabindex="-1"></a>The impact of word count on satisfying answers</h3>
<p>A satisfying answer can be really short! You don’t need to write a 3,000 word article to rank for the query “<em>denzel washington age</em>”.</p>
<p>Don’t try to answer an easy question with a 3,000 word article. That’s how you miss search intent.</p>
<p>To summarize, here’s what to do to give a user a satisfying answer:</p>
<ol>
<li>Understand the user’s search intent.</li>
<li>Create content that really answers their question, using the content type(s) that are likely to help the most.</li>
<li>Make the article as long or short as you need to.</li>
</ol>

<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#winning-clicks" tabindex="-1"></a>Winning the user’s clicks</h3>
<p>You know what queries you want to rank for. You’ve created the best content out there, it’s starting to rank… but is it winning the user’s clicks? Are your snippets engaging, and do they stand out?</p>
<p>Most people forget about this stage, but it’s essential to delivering great user experience: ranking for a query means nothing if people don’t click on your snippet.</p>
<p>So how do you go about getting those clicks?</p>
<p>Let’s illustrate this using the example query “<em>best cbd oil</em>” and the snippet below:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><img alt="A screenshot of a SERP snippet for the query “best cbd oil”." class="image" height="223" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-winning-click-cbd-oil-example-en.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-winning-click-cbd-oil-example-en.png, /media/content/contentking-ux-seo-winning-click-cbd-oil-example-en@2x.png 2x" title="A SERP snippet for the query “best cbd oil”." width="600"/></div>
<ul>
<li>
<a href="/academy/title-tag/">Title:</a> the title should have been shorter, but it does contain the most important keywords at the start, and it also includes what the CBD oil addresses: anxiety, pain relief, and stress relief.</li>
<li>
<a href="/academy/urls/">URL</a>: while the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/news/</code> part of the URL could have been left out, it’s still short, so it’s OK.</li>
<li>Stars: the stars help the snippet stand out among the competition, and they add credibility.</li>
<li>
<a href="/academy/meta-description/">Description</a>: the article has recently been updated, and—while it could have been a bit shorter—it describes exactly what we’re looking for: they’ve reviewed hundreds of CBD oils and created a simple list for us.</li>
</ul>
<p>This snippet is winning clicks.</p>

<h3 id="winning-trust">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#winning-trust" tabindex="-1"></a>Winning the user’s trust</h3>
<p>You’ve gotten the clicks—users have landed on your page. But have you earned their trust?</p>
<p>In today’s day and age, we need to touch on trust and credibility as well.</p>
<p>Especially for “Your Money or Your Life” (YMYL) queries, these two are really important. For example, would you submit your income records over an insecure connection or take medical advice from a complete stranger with no medical background?</p>
<p>No, and rightfully so: these are sensitive topics.</p>
<p>To gain your users’ trust and be credible, you need to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Serve your pages over a secure connection (HTTPS).</li>
<li>Have reviews and testimonials.</li>
<li>Back up your claims with authoritative sources, and have experts fact-check your articles.</li>
</ul>
<p>For instance, if you’re writing about something random like having bumps on your gums, and you’re not a medical professional, you can still write a great article and have a medical professional fact-check it. And then show your visitors that this has been done. This shows you’re taking things seriously and aiming to provide the best source of information.</p>

<h2 id="answering-fast">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#answering-fast" tabindex="-1"></a>Answering their question fast</h2>
<p>Nobody likes to wait, and your visitors aren’t any different.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.thinkwithgoogle.com/marketing-resources/data-measurement/mobile-page-speed-new-industry-benchmarks/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">According to Google</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>, 53% of mobile site visits leave any page that takes longer than three seconds to load. Make sure to give your visitors their answers quickly.</p>
<p>Google finds fast loading pages so important, it's been a ranking factor for years. With the introduction of <a href="/academy/core-web-vitals/">Core Web Vitals</a>, they're taking it <em>way</em> further. It has really become a corner stone of their SEO best practices. To learn more about why page speed matters, we recommend checking out these <a href="/academy/page-speed-resources/">recent case studies</a>.</p>
<p>Explaining in detail how to ensure your pages will load fast is beyond the scope of this section; that deserves an article of its own. But even still, below you’ll find useful pointers to get started.</p>
<p>What really matters to your users is how soon your pages are <strong>usable</strong>. In technical terms, we call that “<a href="/academy/core-web-vitals/#tti">Time to Interactive (TTI)</a>”.</p>
<p>Apply these best practices to ensure your pages load blazingly fast:</p>
<ul>
<li>Use a Content Delivery Network (CDN).</li>
<li>Use caching and file compression. Minify your JS and CSS.</li>
<li>Avoid <a href="https://developers.google.com/web/tools/lighthouse/audits/blocking-resources" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">render-blocking resources</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</li>
<li>
<a href="https://developers.google.com/web/fundamentals/performance/lazy-loading-guidance/images-and-video/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Lazy-load</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> your images and serve responsive images.</li>
<li>Use <a href="/academy/image-seo/#image-formats">image formats</a> smartly (notably WebP and SVG).</li>
</ul>
<p>To help you with this, here are a few tools for analyzing and speeding up your pages:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://webspeedtest.cloudinary.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Cloudinary website speed test</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
<li><a href="https://developers.google.com/web/tools/lighthouse/#devtools" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Lighthouse DevTools</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
<li><a href="https://gtmetrix.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">GTMetrix</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
</ul>
<p>Don’t forget: perform real user tests as well! A tool can only do so much; at the end of the day the only thing that matters is that your users have a great user experience.</p>

<h2 id="providing-a-website-thats-easy-to-use-on-any-device">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#providing-a-website-thats-easy-to-use-on-any-device" tabindex="-1"></a>Providing a website that’s easy to use on any device</h2>
<h3 id="great-usability">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#great-usability" tabindex="-1"></a>Great usability</h3>
<p>Users want websites that are easy to use. How easy a website is to use is called its <em>usability</em>.</p>
<p>Simplified, this breaks down into having;</p>
<ul>
<li>a clear navigation structure across your website and within pages;</li>
<li>a clear heading structure;</li>
<li>content structured using the inverted pyramid;</li>
<li>content formatted for great readability;</li>
<li>content upgrades to increase that content’s usefulness.</li>
</ul>
<h4 id="clear-navigation">Clear navigation structure</h4>
<p>Let’s look at a few examples to see what we mean by a clear navigation structure:</p>
<p>The outdoor store REI has done some great work to create a navigation that’s really clear. Their main navigation is organized by their users’ goals: camping &amp; hiking, climbing, cycling, and so on. And within those goals it breaks down into what you need to achieve that goal. That might be a backpack, a tent, and a sleeping bag.</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><img alt="A screenshot of REI’s main navigation." class="image" height="381" loading="lazy" src="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-rei-site-structure-en.png" srcset="/media/content/contentking-ux-seo-rei-site-structure-en.png, /media/content/contentking-ux-seo-rei-site-structure-en@2x.png 2x" title="A screenshot of REI’s main navigation." width="600"/></div>
<p>You can also use in-page navigation for long articles. Doing so enables your users to jump to the most relevant section immediately, skipping sections they’re not interested in.</p>
<p>Here’s an example—the in-page navigation for <a href="/academy/robotstxt/">our in-depth guide on robots.txt files</a>:</p>

<p>Headings are used to provide hierarchy and clarity to a web page. Using them helps visitors to quickly scan a page and helps search engines to easily understand its structure and topic.</p>

<h4 id="inverted-pyramid">Content structured using the inverted pyramid</h4>
<p>The way you structure your content has a huge impact on a page’s usability. Imagine burying the answer to your visitor’s query at the bottom of a 3,000 word article! That makes the page pretty useless, which makes for poor usability and poor user experience.</p>
<p>Therefore, apply the concept of the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_pyramid_(journalism)" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">inverted pyramid</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>—which journalists have been using for a long time. Applying this concept means that you’re placing the most important information at the start of your content.</p>
<h4 id="content-formatting">Content formatted for great readability</h4>
<p>Make it easy for your visitors to read and digest your content by using white space and lists, while preventing walls of text.</p>
<p>Among other things, we love how Glen on <a href="https://detailed.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">detailed.com</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> uses white space; see the screenshot below. <h3 id="ads-popups">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#ads-popups" tabindex="-1"></a>Go easy on the ads and pop-ups</h3>
<p>Many businesses rely on selling ads as a business model… but in most cases ads are just a distraction for your visitors. Go easy on them.</p>
<p>In Google’s Quality Rater Guidelines section 6.7, “disruptive ads” are mentioned several times. For good reason, because they hurt a visitor’s experience.</p>
<p>Here’s an example of a page where its content is basically buried underneath ads:</p>

<p>The same goes for pop-ups to gather newsletter signups, or request whitepapers. Often websites are programmed to show a pop-up after a visitor is on a website for, say 30 seconds. But what if the user has just opened a couple of websites in different tabs without having looked at them?</p>

<div class="quote-box__content typo">
<blockquote class="quote-box__blockquote">
<p>How to mess up your user experience by going overboard with ads and pop-ups</p>
<p>Have you ever…</p>
<ul>
<li>Clicked on an ad on Facebook for a product you're interested in, and immediately received a pop up telling you about the deal of the day?</li>
<li>Searched Google for answers to a particular problem you're facing, clicked on an article that seemed promising and immediately got a pop up inviting you to subscribe to their email list, before getting to read the content you intended on reading?</li>
</ul>
<p>These are both examples of poor user experiences, of disregarding what people really need, and using pop ups incorrectly.</p>
<p>Here are some examples of high-converting pop ups we've used at GetUplift for our clients, that improve user experience:</p>
<ul>
<li>A pop up offering more relevant information for people who have scrolled more than 40% on an article page.</li>
<li>A pop up inviting people to subscribe and get a coupon code when they reach a product page (not immediately on the homepage).</li>
<li>A pop up inviting people to answer a survey on their 3rd visit to the site and after visiting at least 3 pages.</li>
<li>A pop up inviting people to schedule a demo, after visiting the pricing page and not converting.</li>
</ul>
<p>Everyone loves to hate pop ups but the truth is, that pop ups can work extremely well and even <a href="https://getuplift.co/cro-ux-design-for-experience-and-revenue/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">improve the user experience</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>, when done <em>correctly</em>.</p>
<p>Countless pop ups, with no context, value or benefit can cause more abandonment, let-down and lower conversions. However, pop-ups focused on the value of the user can help people take the next step, find the information they need, get answers they're searching for and even convert. Remember: it's all about <a href="https://getuplift.co/landing-page-optimization-complete-guide/#state-of-awareness-analysis" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">the context and intent of the people behind the screen</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>

<h3 id="mobile-friendliness">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#mobile-friendliness" tabindex="-1"></a>Mobile friendliness</h3>
<p><a href="/academy/ecommerce-seo-checklist/#mobile-friendliness">Mobile friendliness</a> is an essential part of delivering a great user experience. It needs to be easy for users to use your website, and they need to be able to use it on any device.</p>
<p>You need to have a responsive website, one that ensures that its text size is readable on any device, its content isn’t wider than the screen, and there’s enough room between clickable elements.</p>
<p><a href="https://search.google.com/test/mobile-friendly" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Google’s mobile-friendliness tool</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> helps you analyze your website’s mobile friendliness.</p>
<h2 class="toc-hidden" id="wrapping-up">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#wrapping-up" tabindex="-1"></a>Wrapping up the role of UX in SEO</h2>
<p>UX plays a massive role in SEO. If you want to excel at SEO, you need to make sure your UX is on point.</p>
<p>Here’s what a great user experience looks like:</p>
<ol>
<li>Give users a satisfying answer to their question.</li>
<li>Do this blazingly fast…</li>
<li>…through a site that’s easy to use on any device.</li>
</ol>
<p>You can achieve that by:</p>
<ol>
<li>Hitting search intent</li>
<li>Winning the actual click</li>
<li>Answering the visitor’s query…</li>
<li>While making sure pages load fast, the website is usable on any device, and the usability is good.</li>
</ol>
<p>The key to your users’ and search engines’ hearts is delivering a great user experience.</p>

<h2 id="what-are-cookies">What are cookies?</h2>
<p>Cookies are small text files which are stored on the browser or hard drive of your Device when you visit a webpage or use an application. Cookies may be needed to show the webpage or application on your Device and are also used to enhance the user experience. Cookies cannot damage your Devices or the files saved on it. There are different types of cookies. Some cookies come directly from our website (first party cookies) and others come from third parties which place cookies on our site (third party cookies).</p>
<p>Cookies can be stored for varying lengths of time on your browser or device. Session cookies are deleted from your computer or device when you close your web-browser. Persistent cookies will remain stored on your computer or Device until deleted or until they reach their expiry date.</p>
<h2 id="how-do-we-use-cookies">How do we use cookies?</h2>
<h3 id="essential-technical-cookies">Essential (Technical) cookies</h3>
<p>We try to provide an advanced and user-friendly website that adapts automatically to needs and wishes of our visitors and users. To achieve this, we use technical cookies to i.e. show you our Website, to make it function correctly, to create your ContentKing account, to sign you in and to manage your requests. These technical cookies are necessary for our website to function properly.</p>
<h3 id="functional-cookies">Functional cookies</h3>
<p>We also use functional cookies to remember your preferences and settings (as username, password, language etc.) and to help you to use the Website efficiently and effectively. These functional cookies are not strictly necessary for the functioning of our Website, but they add functionality for you and enhance your experience.</p>
<h3 id="analytical-cookies">Analytical cookies</h3>
<p>We use analytical cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. These analytic cookies generate statistical and other information about website use by means of cookies, which are stored on users’ Devices. The information generated relating to our Website is used to create reports about the use of the Website. Analytical cookies may be placed without your consent, unless such cookies have an impact on your privacy. In such cases, prior consent will be asked for.</p>
<h3 id="google-analytics">Google Analytics</h3>
<p>We use Google Analytics cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. With these analytical cookies a permanent cookie is saved on your device to register your use of the Website. Google Analytics analyses this data and provides us with the results. This way, we obtain insight into the traffic of the Website and the way in which the Website is used. Based on this information we are able to make specific adjustments to the Website.</p>
<p>The information that we obtain is transferred to Google and stored by Google on servers outside the European Economic Area. We have entered into a data processing agreement with Google in which agreements have been made regarding the processing of personal data.</p>
<p>If you would like more information about analytical cookies and their expiry date, please visit the Google Analytics <a href="https://developers.google.com/analytics/devguides/collection/gajs/cookie-usage">information page</a>. Google also offers an <a href="https://tools.google.com/dlpage/gaoptout?hl=en">opt-out option</a> for data collection within the scope of Google Analytics.</p>
<h3 id="marketing-cookies">Marketing cookies</h3>
<p>We use third party marketing cookies in order to follow your internet browsing behavior and collect data and information on your browsing behavior from various websites you visited. This information is used to make the content of the displayed advertisements as relevant as possible, and to limit repetition of the same advertisements.</p>
<h3 id="social-plug-ins">Social plug-ins</h3>
<p>By using the Website you can get access to social media websites, such as Facebook, Twitter and LinkedIn. These buttons come with a code which has been made by these networks themselves. Using these so called social plug-ins you can login to your social media profile and subsequently share information from the Website with others. With these social plug-ins third party marketing cookies are saved on your Devices. These cookies serve the purpose of optimizing your user experience.</p>
<p>Companies such as LinkedIn, Twitter and Facebook may share your personal data outside the European Economic Area. Please read our Privacy Policy <a href="/legal/privacy-policy/">here</a> and the respective social network’s privacy statement to see how these companies treat your (personal) data.</p>
<h3 id="pixels">Pixels</h3>
<p>We also place pixels of third parties. A pixel keeps track of your surfing behavior and how you use the Website. These data are aggregated and give us information about the target group that visits our website. Based on this information, we can show an advertisement to a specific or similar target group on the website of the third party that produced the pixel.</p>

If you do not give consent, these cookies will not be placed. In that case, you may not be able to use all the functionalities on our Website.</p>
<p>You can configure your browser so that you do not receive any cookies the next time you use the website. You can read here how you can remove different types of cookies in different browsers. Please consult the help-function of your browser if your browser is not listed below.</p>
