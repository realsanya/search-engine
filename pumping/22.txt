<h1 class="heading__title">Duplicate Content and SEO: The Ultimate Guide</h1>
<div class="heading__params">
<span aria-hidden="true" class="heading__label">

<section aria-label="Duplicate Content in short" class="highlighted-box highlighted-box--with-background typo-block"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">
<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/duplicate-content--reversed.svg)"></i></span>Duplicate Content in short
	</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Duplicate content refers to very similar, or the exact same, content being on multiple pages. Keep this in mind:</p>
<ul>
<li>Duplicate content adds little to no value for your visitors and confuses search engines.</li>
<li>Avoid having duplicate content, as it <strong>may harm your SEO performance</strong>.</li>
<li>Duplicate content can be caused by technical mishaps and manually copied content.</li>
<li>There are effective ways to prevent both cases of duplicate content from becoming an issue, which we'll discuss in this article.</li>
</ul>
</div>

<h2 id="what-is-duplicate-content">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-is-duplicate-content" tabindex="-1"></a>What is duplicate content?</h2>
<p>Taken narrowly, duplicate content refers to very similar, or the exact same, content being on multiple pages within your own website or on other websites.</p>
<p>Taken broadly, duplicate content is content that adds little to no value for your visitors. Therefore, pages with little to no body content are also considered to be duplicate content.</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-1" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-robotconfused@1x.png"/><img alt="Search engine robots get confused by duplicate content." class="image" height="263" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-robotconfused@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-robotconfused@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-robotconfused@2x.png 2x" title="Search engine robots get confused by duplicate content." width="600"/></picture></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-1">Search engine robots get confused by duplicate content.</figcaption></figure>
<h2 id="why-is-duplicate-content-bad-for-seo">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#why-is-duplicate-content-bad-for-seo" tabindex="-1"></a>Why is duplicate content bad for SEO?</h2>
<p>Duplicate content is bad for two reasons:</p>
<ol>
<li>When there are several versions of content available, it's hard for search engines to determine which version to index, and subsequently show in their search results. This <strong>lowers performance for all versions of the content</strong>, since they're competing against each other.</li>
<li>Search engines will have trouble consolidating link metrics (<a href="/academy/authority/">authority</a>, relevancy and trust) for the content, especially when other websites link to more than one version of that content.</li>
</ol>

<h2 id="can-i-get-a-duplicate-content-penalty">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#can-i-get-a-duplicate-content-penalty" tabindex="-1"></a>Can I get a duplicate content penalty?</h2>
<p>Having duplicate content <strong>can hurt your SEO performance</strong>, but it won't get you a penalty from Google <strong>as long as you didn't intentionally copy someone else's website</strong>. If you're an honest website owner with some technical website challenges, and you're not trying to trick Google, you don't have to worry about getting a penalty from Google.</p>
<p>If you've copied large amounts of other people's content, then you're walking a fine line. This is <a href="https://support.google.com/webmasters/answer/66359?hl=en" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">what Google says about it</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>:</p>
<p>"Duplicate content on a site is not grounds for action on that site unless it appears that the intent of the duplicate content is to be deceptive and manipulate search engine results. If your site suffers from duplicate content issues, and you don't follow the advice listed above, we do a good job of choosing a version of the content to show in our search results."</p>
<div aria-label="Embedded quote from our customer" class="quote-box typo-block">
<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/paul-shapiro@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/paul-shapiro@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Paul Shapiro" class="image" height="100" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/paul-shapiro@1x.jpg" title="Paul Shapiro" width="100"/></picture></span></span></div>

<p>People often have mispersceptions about duplicate content. If I had a quarter everytime I heard an SEO say that duplicate content would earn you a Panda penalty, I'd have at least $50. That's a joke. Small industry.</p>
<p>Anyway, if you have one or two, less significant pages with duplicate content, it's really nothing to worry about. The real issues come along when your own website is generating multitudes of duplicate content due to poor web development and technical SEO issues. These may lead to crawling complications and traffic issues. Duplicate content may also be concerning if another domain is scraping your content and those pages are outranking your own, which is rarely the case, but it does happen!</p>
<p>Lastly, probably the biggest conern with duplicate content is in regards to the dillution of backlinks that happen as a result of it. If I have two versions of the same page, and users don't know which one is the 'main' one, then it may receive backlinks and the other may not. This way, instead of one page with all the backlinks, it is split between two or more pages. No bueno.</p>

<h2 id="what-is-the-most-common-fix-for-duplicate-content">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-is-the-most-common-fix-for-duplicate-content" tabindex="-1"></a>What is the most common fix for duplicate content?</h2>
<p>In many cases, the best way to fix duplicate content is implementing 301 redirects from the non-preferred versions of URLs to the preferred versions.</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-2" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 1200px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/ContentKing-Redirect-EN-Redirect-301@1x.png"/><img alt="301 redirect: permanently moved content" class="image" height="354" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/ContentKing-Redirect-EN-Redirect-301@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/ContentKing-Redirect-EN-Redirect-301@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2018/06/ContentKing-Redirect-EN-Redirect-301@2x.png 2x" title="301 redirect: permanently moved content" width="1200"/></picture></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-2">301 redirect: permanently moved content</figcaption></figure>
<p>When URLs need to remain accessible to visitors, you can't use redirect but you can either use a <a href="/academy/canonical/">canonical URL</a> or a robots <a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#noindex">noindex</a> redirective. The canonical URL allows you to consolidates some signals, while the robots noindex directive doesn't.</p>
<p>Choose your weapon to battle duplicate content carefully through as they all have their pros and cons. There's no "one size fits all" approach to duplicate content.</p>
<p>Go through the section below to learn about the different causes of duplicate content, and see which method to tackle it fits best.</p>
<h2 id="common-causes-of-duplicate-content">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#common-causes-of-duplicate-content" tabindex="-1"></a>Common causes of duplicate content</h2>
<p>Duplicate content is often due to an incorrectly set up web server or website. These occurrences are technical in nature and will likely never result in a Google penalty. They can seriously harm your rankings though, so it's important to make it a priority to fix them.</p>
<p>But besides technical causes, there are also human-driven causes: content that's purposely being copied and published elsewhere. As we've said, these can bring penalties if they have a malicious intent.</p>
<h3 class="toc" id="duplicate-content-due-to-technical-reasons">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#duplicate-content-due-to-technical-reasons" tabindex="-1"></a>Duplicate content due to technical reasons</h3>
<h4 id="non-www-vs-www-and-http-vs-https">Non-www vs www and HTTP vs HTTPs</h4>
<p>Say you're using the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">www</code> subdomain and HTTPs. Then your preferred way of serving your content is via <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com</code>. This is your canonical domain.</p>
<p>If your web server is badly configured, your content may also be accessible through:</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-3" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-urlvariants@1x.png"/><img alt="Duplicate content due to different canonical domains." class="image" height="173" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-urlvariants@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-urlvariants@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-urlvariants@2x.png 2x" title="Duplicate content due to different canonical domains." width="600"/></picture></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-3">Duplicate content due to different canonical domains.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Choose a preferred way of serving your content, and implement 301 redirects for non-preferred ways that lead to the preferred version: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com</code>.</p>
<h4 id="url-structure-casing-and-trailing-slashes">URL structure: casing and trailing slashes</h4>
<p>For Google, URLs are case-sensitive. Meaning that <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/url-a/</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/url-A/</code> are seen as different URLs. When you're creating links, it's easy to make a typo, causing both versions of the URL to get indexed. Please note that URLs aren't case-sensitive for Bing.</p>
<p>A forward slash (<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/</code>) at the end of an URL is called a <em>trailing slash</em>. Often URLs are accessible through both variants here: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/url-a</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://example.com/url-a/</code>.</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-4" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-casesensitiveurl@1x.png"/><img alt="Duplicate content due to inconsistencies in URL casing and trailing slash usage." class="image" height="223" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-casesensitiveurl@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-casesensitiveurl@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-casesensitiveurl@2x.png 2x" title="Duplicate content due to inconsistencies in URL casing and trailing slash usage." width="600"/></picture></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-4">Duplicate content due to inconsistencies in URL casing and trailing slash usage.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Choose a preferred structure for your URLs, and for non-preferred URL versions, implement a 301 redirect to the preferred URL version.</p>

<h4 id="index-pages-indexhtml-indexphp">Index pages (index.html, index.php)</h4>
<p>Without your knowledge, your homepage may be accessible via multiple URLs because your web server is misconfigured. Besides https://www.example.com, your homepage may also be accessible through:</p>
<ul>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/index.html</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/index.asp</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/index.aspx</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/index.php</code></li>
</ul>
<p>Choose a preferred way to serve your homepage, and implement 301 redirects from non-preferred versions to the preferred version.</p>
<p>In case your website is using any of these URLs to serve content, make sure to canonicalize these pages because redirecting them would break the pages.</p>
<h4 id="parameters-for-filtering">Parameters for filtering</h4>
<p>Websites often use parameters in URLs so they can offer filtering functionality. Take this URL for example:</p>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/toys/cars?colour=black</code></p>
<p>This page would show all the black toy cars.</p>
<p>While this is fine for visitors, it may cause major issues for search engines. Filter options often generate a virtually infinite amount of combinations when there is more than one filter option available. All the more so because the parameters can be rearranged as well.</p>
<p>These two URLs would show the exact same content:</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-5" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-differenturls@1x.png"/><img alt="Duplicate content due to different order of URL parameters." class="image" height="164" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-differenturls@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-differenturls@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-differenturls@2x.png 2x" title="Duplicate content due to different order of URL parameters." width="600"/></picture></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-5">Duplicate content due to different order of URL parameters.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Implement canonical URLs—one for each main, unfiltered page—to prevent duplicate content and consolidate the filter-delivered page's authority. Please note that this doesn't prevent <a href="/academy/crawl-budget/">crawl budget</a> issues. Alternatively, you could use parameter handling functionality in Google Search Console and Bing Webmaster Tools to instruct their crawlers how to deal with parameters.</p>

<ul>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/category-a/topic/</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/category-b/topic/</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/category-c/topic/</code></li>

<p>If possible, disable the feature to give images dedicated pages. If that's not possible, the next best thing is to add a meta robots noindex attribute to the page.</p>

<h4 id="comment-pages">Comment pages</h4>
<p>If you have comments enabled on your website, you may be automatically paginating them after a certain amount. The paginated comment pages will show the original content; only the comments at the bottom will be different.</p>
<p>For example, the article URL that shows comments 1-20 could be <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/category/topic/</code>, with <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/category/topic/comments-2/</code> for comments 21-40, and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/category/topic/comments-3/</code> for comments 41-60.</p>
<p>Use the pagination link relationships to signal that these are a series of paginated pages.</p>
<h4 id="localization-and-hreflang">Localization and hreflang</h4>
<p>When it comes to localization, duplicate content issues can arise when you're using the exact same content to target people in different regions who speak the same language.</p>
<p>For example: when you have a dedicated website for the Canadian market and also one for the US-market—both in English—chances are there's a lot of duplication in the content.</p>
<p>Google is good at detecting this, and usually folds these results together. The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">hreflang</code> attribute helps prevent duplicate content. So if you're using the same content for different audiences, be sure to <a href="/academy/hreflang/">implement hreflang</a> as part of a <a href="/academy/international-seo/">solid international SEO strategy</a>.</p>

<h4 id="indexable-search-result-pages">Indexable search result pages</h4>
<p>Many websites offer search functionality, allowing visitors to search through the website's content. The pages on which the search results are displayed are all very similar, and in most cases don't provide any value to search engines. That's why you don't want them to be indexable for search engines.</p>
<p>Prevent search engines from indexing the search result pages by utilizing the meta robots noindex attribute. And also in general, it's a best practice not to link to your search result pages.</p>
<p>In case of a large amount of search result pages that are getting crawled by search engines it's recommended to stop search engines from accessing them in the first place using the <a href="/academy/robotstxt/">robots.txt file</a>.</p>
<h4 id="indexable-stagingtesting-environment">Indexable staging/testing environment</h4>
<p>It's likewise a best practice to use staging environments for rolling out and testing new features on websites. But these are often incorrectly left accessible and indexable for search engines.</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-7" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-productionstaging@1x.png"/><img alt="Duplicate content due to multiple environments being publicly available." class="image" height="229" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-productionstaging@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-productionstaging@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-productionstaging@2x.png 2x" title="Duplicate content due to multiple environments being publicly available." width="600"/></picture></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-7">Duplicate content due to multiple environments being publicly available.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Use <a href="/academy/protect-staging-environment/#method-1-http-auth">HTTP authentication to prevent access to staging/testing environments</a>. An additional benefit of doing so is that you're preventing the wrong people from accessing them too.</p>
<p>Should your staging/testing environment get indexed at some point, follow the steps outlined in <a href="/academy/google-remove-urls/#staging-environment">this URL removal guide</a> to quickly remove them.</p>
<h4 id="avoid-publishing-work-in-progress-content">Avoid publishing work-in-progress content</h4>
<p>When you create a new page that contains little content, save it without publishing it yet—often it will provide little to no value.</p>
<p>Save unfinished pages as drafts. If you do need to publish pages with limited content, prevent search engines from <a href="/academy/control-crawl-indexing/#how-does-indexing-work">indexing</a> them: use the meta robots noindex attribute.</p>
<h4 id="parameters-used-for-tracking">Parameters used for tracking</h4>
<p>Parameters are commonly used for tracking purposes too. For instance when sharing URLs on Twitter, the source is added to the URL. This is another source of duplicate content. Take for example this URL that was tweeted using Buffer:</p>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.contentkingapp.com/academy/ecommerce-link-building/?utm_content=buffer825f4&amp;utm_medium=social&amp;utm_source=twitter.com&amp;utm_campaign=buffer</code></p>
<p>It's a best practice to implement self-referencing canonical URLs on pages. If you've already done that, this solves the issue. All URLs with these tracking parameters are canonicalized by default to the version without the parameters.</p>
<h4 id="session-ids">Session IDs</h4>
<p>Sessions may store visitor information for web analytics. If each URL a visitor requests gets a session ID appended, this creates a lot of duplicate content, because the content at these URLs is exactly the same.</p>
<p>For example, when you click through to a localized version of our website, we add a Google Analytics session variable like <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.contentking.nl/?_ga=2.41368868.703611965.1506241071-1067501800.1494424269</code>. It shows the homepage with the exact same content, just on a different URL.</p>
<p>Once again—it's a best practice to implement self-referencing canonical URLs on pages. If you've already done that, this solves the issue. All URLs with these tracking parameters are canonicalized by default to the version without the parameters.</p>
<h4 id="print-friendly-version">Print-friendly version</h4>
<p>When pages have a print-friendly version at a separate URL, there are essentially two version of the same content. Imagine this: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/some-page/</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/print/some-page/</code>.</p>
<p>Implement a canonical URL leading from the print friendly version to the normal version of the page.</p>
<h3 class="toc" id="duplicate-content-caused-by-copied-content">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#duplicate-content-caused-by-copied-content" tabindex="-1"></a>Duplicate content caused by copied content</h3>
<h4 id="landing-pages-for-paid-search">Landing pages for paid search</h4>
<p>Paid search requires dedicated landing pages that target specific keywords. The landing pages are often copies of original pages, which are then adjusted to target these specific keywords. Since these pages are very similar, they produce duplicate content if they are <a href="/academy/google-index/">indexed by search engines</a>.</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-8" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-titlechange@1x.png"/><img alt="Duplicate content due to minor differences between landing pages." class="image" height="275" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-titlechange@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-titlechange@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2017/10/contentking-en-academy-titlechange@2x.png 2x" title="Duplicate content due to minor differences between landing pages." width="600"/></picture></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-8">Duplicate content due to minor differences between landing pages.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Prevent search engines from indexing the landing pages by implementing the meta robots noindex attribute. In general, it's a best practice to neither link to your landing pages nor include them in your XML sitemap.</p>
<h4 id="other-parties-copying-your-content">Other parties copying your content</h4>
<p>Duplicate content can also originate from others copying your content and publishing it elsewhere. This is in particular a problem if your website has a low <a href="/academy/authority/#domain-authority">domain authority</a>, and the one copying your content has a higher domain authority. Websites with a higher domain authority often get crawled more frequent, resulting in the copied content being crawled first on the website of the one that copied the content. They may now be perceived as the original author and rank above you.</p>
<p>Make sure that other websites credit you by both implementing a canonical URL leading to your page and linking to your page. If they're not willing to do so, you can send a <a href="https://support.google.com/legal/troubleshooter/1114905?hl=en" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">DMCA request to Google</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> and/or take legal action.</p>
<h4 id="copying-content-from-other-websites">Copying content from other websites</h4>
<p>Copying content from other websites is a form of duplicate content too. Google <a href="https://support.google.com/webmasters/answer/66359?hl=en" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">has documented</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> how to best handle this from an SEO point of view: linking to the original source, combined with either a <a href="/academy/canonical/">canonical URL</a> or <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">a meta robots noindex</code> tag. Keep in mind that not all website owners are happy with you syndicating their content, so it's recommended to ask for permission to use their content.</p>

<p>Google Search Console's <a href="/academy/index-coverage/">Index Coverage report</a> comes in handy too when finding duplicate content within your site. Be on the lookout for the following issies:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<a href="/academy/index-coverage/faq/duplicate-without-canonical/">Duplicate without user-selected canonical</a>: Google's found duplicate URls that aren't canonicalized to a preferred version.</li>
<li>
<a href="/academy/index-coverage/#duplicate-google-choose-different-canonical">Duplicate, Google chose different canonical than user</a>: Google chose to ignore your canonical on URLs they found on their own, and instead assigns Google-selected canonicals.</li>
<li>
<a href="/academy/index-coverage/#duplicate-submitted-url-not-canonical">Duplicate, submitted URL not selected as canonical</a>: Google chose to ignore the canonicals you defined for URLs you submitted through an XML sitemap.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="toc" id="finding-duplicate-content-outside-your-own-website">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#finding-duplicate-content-outside-your-own-website" tabindex="-1"></a>Finding duplicate content outside your own website</h3>
<p>If you've got a small website, you can try searching in Google for phrases between quotes. For instance, if I want to see if there are any other versions of this article, I may search for "Using ContentKing, you can easily find duplicate content by checking whether your pages have a unique page title, meta description, and H1 heading."</p>
<p>Alternatively, for larger website you can use a service such as <a href="https://www.copyscape.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Copyscape</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>. Copyscape crawls the web looking for multiple occurrences of the same or nearly the same content.</p>
<h2 id="faq">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#faq" tabindex="-1"></a>Frequently asked questions about duplicate content</h2>
<ol>
<li><a href="/academy/duplicate-content/#can-i-get-a-penalty-for-having-duplicate-content">Can I get a penalty for having duplicate content?</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/duplicate-content/#will-fixing-duplicate-content-issues-increase-my-rankings">Will fixing duplicate content issues increase my rankings?</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/duplicate-content/#how-much-duplicate-content-is-acceptable">How much duplicate content is acceptable?</a></li>
</ol>
<h3 id="can-i-get-a-penalty-for-having-duplicate-content">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#can-i-get-a-penalty-for-having-duplicate-content" tabindex="-1"></a>⚠️ Can I get a penalty for having duplicate content?</h3>
<p>If you didn't intentionally copy someone's website, then it's very unlikely for you to get a duplicate content penalty. If you have did copy large amounts of other people's content, then you're walking a fine line. This is what <a href="https://support.google.com/webmasters/answer/66359?hl=en" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Google says about it</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Duplicate content on a site is not grounds for action on that site unless it appears that the intent of the duplicate content is to be deceptive and manipulate search engine results. If your site suffers from duplicate content issues, and you don't follow the advice listed above, we do a good job of choosing a version of the content to show in our search results.</p>
</blockquote>
<h3 id="will-fixing-duplicate-content-issues-increase-my-rankings">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#will-fixing-duplicate-content-issues-increase-my-rankings" tabindex="-1"></a>🔧 Will fixing duplicate content issues increase my rankings?</h3>
<p>Yes, because by fixing the duplicate content issues you're telling search engines what pages they should really be crawling, indexing, and ranking.</p>
<p>You'll also be preventing search engines from spending their <a href="/academy/crawl-budget/">crawl budget</a> for your website on irrelevant duplicate pages. They can focus on the unique content on your website that you want to rank for.</p>
<h3 id="how-much-duplicate-content-is-acceptable">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-much-duplicate-content-is-acceptable" tabindex="-1"></a>🧮 How much duplicate content is acceptable?</h3>
<p>There's no one good answer to this question. However:</p>
<p>If you want to rank with a page, it needs to be valuable to your visitors and have unique content.</p>

<h2 id="what-are-cookies">What are cookies?</h2>
<p>Cookies are small text files which are stored on the browser or hard drive of your Device when you visit a webpage or use an application.
