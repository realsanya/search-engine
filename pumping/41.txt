<h1 class="heading__title">Image SEO: the Ultimate Guide</h1>
<div class="heading__params">

<section aria-label="Image SEO in short" class="highlighted-box highlighted-box--with-background typo-block"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">
<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/image-seo--reversed.svg)"></i></span>Image SEO in short
	</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Image SEO can be a game-changer for your business.</p>
<p>In order to be successful at image SEO, pay attention to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Your images' discoverability, crawlability, and indexability</li>
<li>The authority of the pages your images are on</li>
<li>Those pages' topical relevance</li>
<li>Image captions</li>
<li>Image attributes (file name, format, size, and alt attributes)</li>
<li>How fast your images load</li>
<li>Providing context about the images – stated using Schema markup</li>
</ul>
</div>

<h2 id="what-is-it">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-is-it" tabindex="-1"></a>What is Image SEO?</h2>
<p>Image SEO comprises the activities used for getting images ranked high in search engines' organic listings.</p>
<p>Just like with pages, search engines try to make sense of images. To make your images rank high, you should do whatever you can to help them with the "making sense" part. In this article, we'll explain how you do this.</p>
<h2 id="why-care">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#why-care" tabindex="-1"></a>Why should you care about Image SEO?</h2>
<p>There are two reasons to care about Image SEO:</p>
<ol>
<li>Images can be a huge driver of quality organic traffic. Especially <a href="https://www.searchenginejournal.com/google-image-search-2/238049/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">now that Google has removed the "View image" option</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>, users are much more likely to go to your page.</li>
<li>When you do Image SEO, you automatically optimize for screen readers, thereby helping visually impaired people use and understand your website better too.</li>
</ol>
<p>We bet now you're asking yourself: "Will image SEO be a huge traffic driver for me as well?"</p>
<p>Whether that's the case depends on your business. For us, it actually only accounts for a fraction of our traffic. Usually, people looking to resolve their SEO questions aren't looking for images. Search engines know this, so they mostly return regular pages in their results.</p>
<p>As a rule of thumb: if images frequently come up as results for queries you're trying to rank for, Image SEO is important to you. So if you're running an eCommerce site, a celebrity news site, or for instance a stock photo website, Image SEO is hugely important.</p>

<p>Image search traffic doesn't only come from images.google.com, but also from universal search - and this is important. For some user intents, Google will show videos and images prominently in the SERPs. That's a great workaround if you can't rank at the top organically.</p>

<h2 id="discoverability-crawlability-indexability">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#discoverability-crawlability-indexability" tabindex="-1"></a>Discoverability, crawlability, and indexability</h2>
<p>You might think we don't even have to mention this, but people get it wrong all the time. Make sure search engines can discover, crawl, and index your images easily.</p>
<h3 id="help-search-engines-discover-your-images-with-image-xml-sitemaps">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#help-search-engines-discover-your-images-with-image-xml-sitemaps" tabindex="-1"></a>Help search engines discover your images with image XML sitemaps</h3>
<p>Including images in your <a href="/academy/xml-sitemap/">XML sitemaps</a> makes it easy for search engines to discover them, and to prioritize the crawling and indexing of your images.</p>
<p>You can either create dedicated image XML sitemaps or include your images in your existing XML sitemaps. Both methods are fine, although we recommend creating dedicated image XML sitemaps if your site has thousands of images, as having separate XML sitemaps gives you a better overview when you're analyzing which images are indexed.</p>
<p>Some guidelines around image XML sitemaps:</p>
<ul>
<li>Each <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;url&gt;</code> tag can contain up to 1,000 <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">image:image</code> tags. In other words, you can define 1,000 images per page</li>
<li>To define an image caption, use <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">image:caption</code>
</li>
<li>To define an image's geographic location, use <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">image:geo_location</code>
</li>
<li>To define an image license, use <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">image:license</code>
</li>
</ul>

<h3 id="make-sure-your-images-are-crawlable-and-indexable">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#make-sure-your-images-are-crawlable-and-indexable" tabindex="-1"></a>Make sure your images are crawlable and indexable</h3>
<p>What can't be crawled, can't be indexed. So make sure an incorrectly set up <a href="/academy/robotstxt/">robots.txt file</a> isn't blocking search engines' access to your images. Don't prevent Googlebot-Image and Googlebot from accessing your images in your robots.txt!</p>
<p>If you want your images to be indexed by search engines, make sure they don't have a <a href="/academy/meta-robots-tag/#the-x-robots-tag-http-header">X-Robots-Tag HTTP Header</a> noindex directive and are not canonicalized using the HTTP header.</p>
<p>If you're using the robots <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noindex</code> directive on a page which contains the images, the images won't be indexed either (unless they're also on other pages that are indexable).</p>
<p>Also make sure your pages don't contain the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">noimageindex</code> robots directive, which specifically communicates to search engines that you don't want them to index the images on your page.</p>
<p>When you're serving pages over <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">HTTPS</code>, prevent serving images over <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">HTTP</code> as browsers may show a warning in the address bar that your site isn't completely secure. And for search engines, you're just adding another hurdle in finding the right image to rank.</p>

<h2 id="image-popularity">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#image-popularity" tabindex="-1"></a>Image popularity</h2>
<p>The amount of times the image is used, an image's popularity, is taken into account by search engines too. If an image is often used, it must be popular and therefore rank highly. Embedding images that other websites are already using, and that search engines already know about, gives you a head start to rank your image.</p>
<p>Take for example Denzel Washington's Wikipedia headshot. When you do a reverse-image search in Google you'll find it's <a href="https://www.google.com/search?tbs=sbi:AMhZZisp-a_1eUAL5Geo3AUdr_1nNQf0Ma6LtXy3gyxuoplGphpXxFqkFEOmxhGgko2Y-Zjr5aG8hxj2OLPfgJUPp0hMuJ62Y1VqkWssZ7fE3NuYd-y-fhvma9UGyOCgEUEv-Omah0ZsGpfm2RYyAOH7iNJGRzvF8nlMdYNth21wo58yAYgnGSSowuLZbPsJp07v_1k-OdxmIrPrWNt-59c-Z8NHRutDalkTxdcAW0kc7Mt6Nv0QPamIRLjUO4OU2M4r4Zn7ApbHz0_1ZRmQ9GWERPUlx3VkCVXgDLI2HRZmmmnmsgoBgXfu5JRmfbcAYsmbSf4NlyGnhuQtK40NTyOaGvM53erLNUnpPQ" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">used on lots of other websites</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>. You don't even have the include the exact same image. It can have different dimensions, different file sizes and even different formats. Google understands it's the same image.</p>
<div aria-label="Embedded quote from our customer" class="quote-box typo-block">
<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/martin-missfeldt@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/martin-missfeldt@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Martin Missfeldt" class="image" height="100" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/martin-missfeldt@1x.jpg" title="Martin Missfeldt" width="100"/></picture></span></span></div>

<p>Many SEOs know the usual image ranking factors: alt-text, captions, text around the image, heading in front of the image and so on. But what many don't consider is that this only takes into account which queries (!) the image can rank for. It doesn't say anything about the <em>popularity</em> of the image, which is important too. So, how does Google measure the popularity of images? It's quite simple: how often the image is used.</p>
<p>It doesn't matter if you use the same image physically (by hot linking) or if it is a copy of an image. A copy can even have a different compression, different image dimensions or a different image file size. Google's image algorithm recognizes copies, and related them accordingly. In principle, Google says: "these popular images are used often - so we'll give them priority in the results".</p>

<h2 id="related-images">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#related-images" tabindex="-1"></a>Images that are related</h2>
<p>Including related images about the same topic, with similar content in the images, also helps you rank your images better. Doing so helps become more relevant, and authoritative.</p>
<p>In case of the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">denzel washington</code> query, you should gather different photos of him and put them together on one page.</p>
<p>Here's another example: let's say you're trying to rank for the query <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">checklist illustration.</code> Including different illustrations containing checklists helps all these images rank better. Please note that these illustrations should be showing roughly the same thing, in this case a list of checkboxes.</p>
<p>This is one of the reasons Pinterest has been so successful at driving organic traffic through images - they're hosting millions of images, and their users neatly categorize them too. This is a text book example of how including related images helps you rank.</p>

<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">

</div></section>
<p>An image tag has a file name, a format, a location where the image can be found, and attributes that provide context. This is another place where search engines will pick up clues on the queries for which your images are relevant. But note that you can overdo this. Don't stuff image file names and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">alt</code> attributes full of keywords.</p>
<p>Here's a breakdown of an image's properties:</p>
<ul>
<li>Image file name</li>
<li>Image format</li>
<li>Image URL path</li>
<li>Image <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">srcset</code> attribute for serving responsive images</li>
<li>Image <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">alt</code> attribute</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="image-file-name">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#image-file-name" tabindex="-1"></a>Image file name: how to name images for SEO</h3>
<p>Make sure the file name is descriptive and fits relevant queries.</p>
<ul>
<li>Good: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">denzel-washington-graduating-high-school</code>
</li>
<li>Bad: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">photo</code>
</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="image-formats">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#image-formats" tabindex="-1"></a>Image formats</h3>
<p>Choosing the right format for the image helps keep image load times down and image quality good.</p>
<ul>
<li>Good: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">.jpeg</code> for a photo</li>
<li>Bad: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">.gif</code> for your screenshot that's supposed to show details</li>
</ul>
<p>Here are the most popular image formats, and what they're best used for.</p>

<td>Photos</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>PNG</td>
<td>Logos (as it supports a transparent background) and images that you want to remain sharp, such as screenshots.</td>

<td>GIF</td>
<td>Animations</td>

<h3 id="image-url-path">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#image-url-path" tabindex="-1"></a>Image URL path</h3>
<p>You want your images to have a short and logical URL path. Avoid using a chronological folder structure such as for instance the one used by WordPress. Your images are probably timeless, so don't make people think they're outdated.</p>
<ul>
<li>Good: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/images/denzel-washington-graduating-high-school.jpeg</code>
</li>
<li>Bad: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">/wp-content/uploads/2016/09/images/denzel-washington-graduating-high-school.jpeg</code>
</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="image-srcset">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#image-srcset" tabindex="-1"></a>Image srcset attribute for serving responsive images</h3>
<p>Considering Google's mobile-first focus, the role of page speed as a ranking factor, and the fact that Google primarily crawls with a mobile user-agent, carefully serving the right image sizes to the right users is essential.</p>
<p>Thankfully, you can define what versions of an image will be used for different devices and viewports. For instance, if you're browsing a website on your phone, you don't need an image with a width of <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">1024 px</code>. You can make do with a version that's only <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">400 px</code> wide, saving you a lot of bandwidth.</p>
<p>You can define what image to use for what viewport width using the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">srcset</code> attribute. Here's an example:</p>
<section aria-label="A fragment of code" class="code-box"><div class="code-box__code">
<figure class="highlight"><pre><code class="language-text" data-lang="text">&lt;img src="/images/denzel-washington-graduating-high-school.jpeg" srcset="/images/denzel-washington-graduating-high-school-400.jpg 375w, /images/denzel-washington-graduating-high-school-800.jpg 768w" alt="Denzel Washington graduating high school" /&gt;</code></pre></figure>
</div></section>
<p>In this example, we'll serve the image with a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">400 px</code> width to screens with a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">375 px</code> width viewport and below, the image with a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">800 px</code> width to screens with a viewport width above <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">375 px</code> and up to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">768 px</code>, and the default picture to every device with a viewport width higher than <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">768 px</code>.</p>
<p>If you want your images to look extra sharp on retina screens, you can serve images with double the pixel density of a normal image. If your normal image has dimensions of 600 x 300 and is called <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">denzel-washington-graduating-high-school.jpeg</code>, then your retina image should have dimensions of <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">1200 x 600</code> and be called <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">denzel-washington-graduating-high-school@2x.jpeg</code>.</p>

<h3 id="image-alt-attribute">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#image-alt-attribute" tabindex="-1"></a>Image alt attribute</h3>

<h2 id="image-load-time">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#image-load-time" tabindex="-1"></a>Image load time</h2>
<p>Page speed is one of the ranking factors for search engines (from May 2021 on folded into <a href="/academy/core-web-vitals/">Core Web Vitals</a>). That makes it important for images to load fast too. On top of that, images are using the same <a href="/academy/crawl-budget/">crawl budget</a> your pages and other assets are.</p>
<p>To make sure your images load fast, make sure to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Choose the right image format (as covered in <a href="/academy/image-seo/#image-formats">the section Image formats</a>)</li>
<li>Serve the right image size (as covered in <a href="/academy/image-seo/#image-srcset">the section Image srcset attribute</a>)</li>
<li>Compress your images</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="image-compression">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#image-compression" tabindex="-1"></a>Compressing images</h3>
<p>Compress your images to decrease their file sizes. There are several overall options for compressing images:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<strong>Lossy:</strong> file size is decreased significantly, but the images lose quality. This option makes for the fastest loading images.</li>
<li>
<strong>Lossless:</strong> file size is decreased moderately, and the images don't lose quality. This option doesn't help you that much to make images load fast.</li>
<li>
<strong>Glossy:</strong> this is the golden mean between "lossy" and "lossless", balancing decreased file size and loss of quality.</li>
</ul>

<p>In this section we'll cover page speed optimization best practices that go beyond images, and yet are worth noting considering their impact from an SEO and user standpoint.</p>
<h4 id="serve-images-through-a-cdn">Serve images through a CDN</h4>
<p>Using a CDN such as Cloudflare to serve your content, including images, has many advantages. Since a CDN enables users to request your content from the server that's closest to them, it greatly decreases load times.</p>
<h4 id="use-browser-caching">Use browser caching</h4>
<p>Use browser caching to ensure that images that are used on multiple pages are cached. Examples are images that are used site-wide, such as for example logos in the header, sidebar, and footer.</p>
<h4 id="lazy-loading">Use lazy loading for images</h4>
<p>While using lazy loading for images doesn't speed up their loading, it is important from a page speed optimization point of view, and since we're talking about images, it makes sense to mention it here too.</p>
<p>Defer loading your images until they're needed, and also until after all the elements that are critical to rendering the page are loaded. It's more important for users that pages are quickly usable, than that they look perfect. Why would you immediately load images that are all the way down on a page, when users start reading at the top?</p>
<h5 id="important-notes-on-lazy-loading">Important notes on lazy loading</h5>
<p>It's important to note here that it's recommended not to use lazy loading for images that are essential to the page. Search engines won't immediately crawl and index images that are included using lazy loading because for search engines to "see" these images, they first need to render the page. We recommend including all essential images directly in the HTML, and using lazy loading for all non-essential images.</p>
<section aria-label="Useful resources" class="highlighted-box typo-block highlighted-box--with-background highlighted-box--with-icon highlighted-box--resources-style"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">Useful resources</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<ul>
<li><a href="/academy/page-speed-resources/">Why Page Speed Matters: 10 Case Studies</a></li>
</ul>
</div></section>
<p>There are a lot of developments on the subject of lazy loading, as <a href="https://web.dev/native-lazy-loading" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Google announced</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> on August 6th, 2019 that they're adding native support for lazy loading starting with Chrome 76. We've yet to wait for other major browsers to jump on this band wagon, so keep an eye on this <a href="https://caniuse.com/#feat=loading-lazy-attr" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Can I Use</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> page. This sounds promising though, as this means — when there's enough support  from other browsers — there's no longer a need to implement lazy loading through JavaScript. This means page load times will go down and the whole <a href="/academy/control-crawl-indexing/">crawling and indexing process</a> of your images by search engines will be accelerated too.</p>
<h2 id="schema">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#schema" tabindex="-1"></a>Use Schema to communicate context</h2>
<p>Mark up any image that is representative for your content using Schema.org markup. This helps search engines understand its context, plus it also makes it more likely that you'll win featured snippets with that image. Having the image there makes the featured snippet stand out more, and so you'll also get more clicks.</p>

<li>Provide the images at high resolution, with the following aspect ratios: 16x9, 4x3, and 1x1.</li>

<h2 id="image-optimization-tools">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#image-optimization-tools" tabindex="-1"></a>Tools to help you analyze image optimization potential</h2>
<p>There are several tools out there to help you analyze your potential when it comes to image optimization. They'll analyze the images you're currently using and gauge the optimization potential. In most cases, you can significantly decrease load time – by several seconds.</p>
<p>List of tools:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://webspeedtest.cloudinary.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Website Speed Test</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
<li><a href="https://developers.google.com/web/tools/lighthouse/#devtools" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Google's Lighthouse in Chrome DevTools</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
<li><a href="https://gtmetrix.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">GTMetrics</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></li>
<li>
<a href="https://tinypng.com/analyzer" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">TinyPNG Analyzer</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> - This tool not only shows you the current size of your images, it also compresses them in realtime so you can directly see how much you can save.</li>
</ul>
<p>Please note that these are automated tools, and that you should take the outcome with a grain of salt. Don't take the outcome as the most important points of action. As with everything in SEO, always remain critical and place things in perspective.</p>

Especially for eCommerce. Take for instance Google Shopping — where one of the fiercest content battles is fought all day, every day. Having great product pages make all the difference there. You can't just use any image for that, there are <a href="https://blog.wakeupdata.com/how-to-implement-product-images-to-get-the-best-conversions" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">strict guidelines and best practices</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> to stick to.</p>

<p>When you use our website <a href="https://www.contentkingapp.com/">www.contentkingapp.com</a> (“Website”), we may place cookies. <h2 id="what-are-cookies">What are cookies?</h2>
<p>Cookies are small text files which are stored on the browser or hard drive of your Device when you visit a webpage or use an application. Cookies may be needed to show the webpage or application on your Device and are also used to enhance the user experience. Cookies cannot damage your Devices or the files saved on it. There are different types of cookies. Some cookies come directly from our website (first party cookies) and others come from third parties which place cookies on our site (third party cookies).</p>
<p>Cookies can be stored for varying lengths of time on your browser or device. Session cookies are deleted from your computer or device when you close your web-browser. Persistent cookies will remain stored on your computer or Device until deleted or until they reach their expiry date.</p>
<h2 id="how-do-we-use-cookies">How do we use cookies?</h2>
<h3 id="essential-technical-cookies">Essential (Technical) cookies</h3>
<p>We try to provide an advanced and user-friendly website that adapts automatically to needs and wishes of our visitors and users. To achieve this, we use technical cookies to i.e. show you our Website, to make it function correctly, to create your ContentKing account, to sign you in and to manage your requests. These technical cookies are necessary for our website to function properly.</p>
<h3 id="functional-cookies">Functional cookies</h3>
<p>We also use functional cookies to remember your preferences and settings (as username, password, language etc.) and to help you to use the Website efficiently and effectively. These functional cookies are not strictly necessary for the functioning of our Website, but they add functionality for you and enhance your experience.</p>
<h3 id="analytical-cookies">Analytical cookies</h3>
<p>We use analytical cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. These analytic cookies generate statistical and other information about website use by means of cookies, which are stored on users’ Devices. The information generated relating to our Website is used to create reports about the use of the Website. Analytical cookies may be placed without your consent, unless such cookies have an impact on your privacy. In such cases, prior consent will be asked for.</p>
<h3 id="google-analytics">Google Analytics</h3>
<p>We use Google Analytics cookies in order to collect statistics about the use and visits of the Website. With these analytical cookies a permanent cookie is saved on your device to register your use of the Website. Google Analytics analyses this data and provides us with the results. This way, we obtain insight into the traffic of the Website and the way in which the Website is used. Based on this information we are able to make specific adjustments to the Website.</p>
<p>The information that we obtain is transferred to Google and stored by Google on servers outside the European Economic Area. We have entered into a data processing agreement with Google in which agreements have been made regarding the processing of personal data.</p>
<p>If you would like more information about analytical cookies and their expiry date, please visit the Google Analytics <a href="https://developers.google.com/analytics/devguides/collection/gajs/cookie-usage">information page</a>. Google also offers an <a href="https://tools.google.com/dlpage/gaoptout?hl=en">opt-out option</a> for data collection within the scope of Google Analytics.</p>
<h3 id="marketing-cookies">Marketing cookies</h3>
<p>We use third party marketing cookies in order to follow your internet browsing behavior and collect data and information on your browsing behavior from various websites you visited. This information is used to make the content of the displayed advertisements as relevant as possible, and to limit repetition of the same advertisements.</p>
<h3 id="social-plug-ins">Social plug-ins</h3>
<p>By using the Website you can get access to social media websites, such as Facebook, Twitter and LinkedIn. These buttons come with a code which has been made by these networks themselves. Using these so called social plug-ins you can login to your social media profile and subsequently share information from the Website with others. With these social plug-ins third party marketing cookies are saved on your Devices. These cookies serve the purpose of optimizing your user experience.</p>
<p>Companies such as LinkedIn, Twitter and Facebook may share your personal data outside the European Economic Area. Please read our Privacy Policy <a href="/legal/privacy-policy/">here</a> and the respective social network’s privacy statement to see how these companies treat your (personal) data.</p>
<h3 id="pixels">Pixels</h3>
<p>We also place pixels of third parties. A pixel keeps track of your surfing behavior and how you use the Website. These data are aggregated and give us information about the target group that visits our website. Based on this information, we can show an advertisement to a specific or similar target group on the website of the third party that produced the pixel.</p>
<h2 id="disable-or-delete-cookies">Disable or delete cookies</h2>
<p>When you first visit the Website, we ask you to give consent for cookies that are not necessary or functional and that have an impact on your privacy. If you do not give consent, these cookies will not be placed. In that case, you may not be able to use all the functionalities on our Website.</p>
<p>You can configure your browser so that you do not receive any cookies the next time you use the website. You can read here how you can remove different types of cookies in different browsers. Please consult the help-function of your browser if your browser is not listed below.</p>
