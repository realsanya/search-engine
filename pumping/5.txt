<h1 class="heading__title">Crawl budget explained</h1>
<div class="heading__params">
<span aria-hidden="true" class="heading__label">
										Last updated:

<span aria-label="The date of last update" class="heading__caption">

<section aria-label="Crawl budget in short" class="highlighted-box highlighted-box--with-background typo-block"><div aria-hidden="true" class="highlighted-box__title">
<span class="highlighted-box__title-icon"><i class="post-type-icon post-type-icon--reversed-style" style="background-image: url(/assets/images/icons/post-types/crawl-budget--reversed.svg)"></i></span>Crawl budget in short
	</div>
<div class="highlighted-box__content">
<p>Crawl budget is the number of pages search engines will crawl on a website within a certain timeframe.</p>
<p>Search engines calculate crawl budget based on crawl limit (how often they can crawl without causing issues) and crawl demand (how often they'd like to crawl a site).</p>
<p>If you’re wasting crawl budget, search engines won’t be able to crawl your website efficiently, which would end up hurting your SEO performance.</p>
</div>
</section>

<h2 id="definition-crawl-budget">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#definition-crawl-budget" tabindex="-1"></a>What is crawl budget?</h2>
<p>Crawl budget is the number of pages search engines will crawl on a website within a certain timeframe.</p>
<h3 class="toc" id="search-engines-crawl">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#search-engines-crawl" tabindex="-1"></a>Why do search engines assign crawl budget to websites?</h3>
<p>Because they don't have unlimited resources, and they divide their attention across millions of websites. So they need a way to prioritize their crawling effort. Assigning crawl budget to each website helps them do this.</p>
<h3 class="toc" id="engines-assign-pages">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#engines-assign-pages" tabindex="-1"></a>How do they assign crawl budget to websites?</h3>
<p>That's based on two factors, crawl limit and crawl demand:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<strong>Crawl limit / host load:</strong> how much crawling can a website handle, and what are its owner's preferences?</li>
<li>
<strong>Crawl demand / crawl scheduling:</strong> which URLs are worth (re)crawling the most, based on its popularity and how often it's being updated.</li>
</ol>
<p>Crawl budget is a common term within SEO. Crawl budget is sometimes also referred to as <strong>crawl space</strong> or <strong>crawl time</strong>.</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/ContentKing-Crawl-Budget-EN-Stopwatch@1x.png"/><img alt="Crawl budget" class="image" height="246" src="/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/ContentKing-Crawl-Budget-EN-Stopwatch@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/ContentKing-Crawl-Budget-EN-Stopwatch@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2018/07/ContentKing-Crawl-Budget-EN-Stopwatch@2x.png 2x" title="Crawl budget" width="600"/></picture></div>
<h3 class="toc" id="not-just-pages">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#not-just-pages" tabindex="-1"></a>Is crawl budget just about pages?</h3>
<p>It's not actually, for the sake of ease we're talking about pages, but in reality it's about any document that search engines crawl. Some examples of other documents: JavaScript and CSS files, mobile page variants, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">hreflang</code> variants and PDF files.</p>
<h3 class="toc" id="crawl-limi">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#crawl-limi" tabindex="-1"></a>How does crawl limit / host load work in practice?</h3>
<p>Crawl limit, or host load if you will, is an important part of crawl budget. Search engines crawlers are designed to prevent overloading a web server with requests so they're careful about this.How search engines determine the crawl limit of a website? There are a variety of factors influencing the crawl limit. To name a few:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<strong>Signs of platform in bad shape:</strong> how often requested URLs timeout or return server errors.</li>
<li>
<strong>The amount of websites running on the host:</strong> if your website is running on a shared hosting platform with hundreds of other websites, and you've got a fairly large website the crawl limit for your website is very limited as crawl limit is determined on a host level. You have to share the host's crawl limit with all of the other sites running on it. In this case you'd be way better of on a dedicated server, which will most likely also massively decrease load times for your visitors.</li>
</ul>
<p>Another thing to consider is having separate mobile and desktop sites running on the same host. They have a shared crawl limit too. So keep this in mind.</p>

<h3 class="toc" id="crawl-demand">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#crawl-demand" tabindex="-1"></a>How does crawl demand / crawl scheduling work in practice?</h3>
<p>Crawl demand, or crawl scheduling, is about determining the worth of re-crawling URLs. Again, many factors influence crawl demand among which:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<strong>Popularity:</strong> how many inbound internal and inbound external links a URL has, but also the amount of queries it's ranking for.</li>
<li>
<strong>Freshness:</strong> how often the URL's being updated.</li>
<li>
<strong>Type of page:</strong> is the type of page likely to change. Take for example a product category page, and a terms and conditions page - which one do you think changes most often and deserves to be crawled more frequently?</li>
</ul>
<div aria-label="Embedded quote from our customer" class="quote-box typo-block">
<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/dawn-anderson@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/dawn-anderson@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Dawn Anderson" class="image" height="100" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/dawn-anderson@1x.png" title="Dawn Anderson" width="100"/></picture></span></span></div>
<div class="quote-box__content typo">
<blockquote class="quote-box__blockquote">
<p>Forcing Google's crawlers to come back to your site when there is nothing more important to find (i.e. meaningful change) is not a good strategy and they're pretty smart at working out whether the frequency of these pages changing actually adds value. The best advice I could give is to concentrate on making the pages more important (adding more useful information, making the pages content rich (they will naturally trigger more queries by default as long as the focus of a topic is maintained). By naturally triggering more queries as part of 'recall' (impressions) you make your pages more important and lo and behold: you'll likely get crawled more frequent.</p>

<h3 id="dont-forget-crawl-capacity-of-the-system-itself">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#dont-forget-crawl-capacity-of-the-system-itself" tabindex="-1"></a>Don't forget: crawl capacity of the system itself</h3>
<p>While search engine crawling systems have massive crawl capacity, at the end of the day it's limited. So in a scenario where 80% of Google's data centers go offline at the same time, their crawl capacity decreases massively and in turn all websites' crawl budget.</p>
<p><strong>Massive thanks to <a href="https://twitter.com/dawnieando" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Dawn Anderson</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> for providing us with details on crawl limit, crawl demand and crawl capacity!</strong></p>
<h2 id="why-should-you-care-about-crawl-budget">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#why-should-you-care-about-crawl-budget" tabindex="-1"></a>Why should you care about crawl budget?</h2>
<p>You want search engines to find and understand as many as possible of your indexable pages, and you want them to do that as quickly as possible. When you add new pages and update existing ones, you want search engines to pick these up as soon as possible. The sooner they've indexed the pages, the sooner you can benefit from them.</p>
<p>If you're wasting crawl budget, search engines won't be able to crawl your website efficiently. They'll spend time on parts of your site that don't matter, which can result in important parts of your website being left undiscovered. If they don't know about pages, they won't crawl and index them, and you won't be able to bring visitors in through search engines to them.</p>
<p>You can see where this is leading to: wasting crawl budget hurts your SEO performance.</p>
<p>Please note that crawl budget is generally only something to worry about if you've got a large website, let's say 10,000 pages and up.</p>

<h2 id="what-is-my-crawl-budget">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-is-my-crawl-budget" tabindex="-1"></a>What is the crawl budget for my website?</h2>
<p>Out of all the search engines, Google is the most transparent about their crawl budget for your website.</p>
<h3 id="crawl-budget-in-google-search-console">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#crawl-budget-in-google-search-console" tabindex="-1"></a>Crawl budget in Google Search Console</h3>
<p>If you have your website verified in Google Search Console, you can get some insight into your website's crawl budget for Google.</p>
<p>Follow these steps:</p>
<ol>
<li>Log in to Google Search Console and choose a website.</li>
<li>Go to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl Stats</code>. There you can see the number of pages that Google crawls per day.</li>
</ol>
<p>During the summer of 2016, our crawl budget looked like this:</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-2" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 1159px;"><img alt="Google Search Console Crawl Stats" class="image" height="390" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/crawl-budget-google-search-console.png" title="Google Search Console Crawl Stats" width="1159"/></div>

<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-3" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 1145px;"><img alt="Google Search Console Crawl Stats" class="image" height="415" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/google-search-console-crawl-stats.png" title="Google Search Console Crawl Stats" width="1145"/></div>

<h2 id="how-optimize-crawl-budget">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-optimize-crawl-budget" tabindex="-1"></a>How do you optimize your crawl budget?</h2>
<p>Optimizing your crawl budget comes down to making sure no crawl budget is wasted. Essentially, fixing the reasons for wasted crawl budget. We monitor thousands of websites; if you were to check each one of them for crawl budget issues, you'd quickly see a pattern: most websites are suffering from the same kind of issues.</p>
<p>Common reasons for wasted crawl budget that we encounter:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<strong>Accessible URLs with parameters:</strong> an example of a URL with a parameter is <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">https://www.example.com/toys/cars?color=black</code>. In this case, the parameter is used to store a visitor's selection in a product filter.</li>
<li>
<strong>Duplicate content:</strong> we call pages that are highly similar, or exactly the same, "duplicate content." Examples are: copied pages, internal search result pages, and tag pages.</li>
<li>
<strong>Low-quality content:</strong> pages with very little content, or pages that don't add any value.</li>
<li>
<strong>Broken and redirecting links:</strong> broken links are links referencing pages that don't exist anymore, and redirected links are links to URLs that are redirecting to other URLs.</li>
<li>
<strong>Including incorrect URLs in XML sitemaps:</strong> non-indexable pages and non-pages such as 3xx, 4xx and <a href="/academy/http-status-codes/faq/server-error-5xx/">5xx</a> URLs shouldn't be included in your XML sitemap.</li>
<li>
<strong>Pages with high load time / time-outs:</strong> pages that take a long time to load, or don't load at all, have a negative impact on your crawl budget, because it's a sign to search engines that your website can't handle the request, and so they may adjust your crawl limit.</li>
<li>
<strong>High numbers of non-indexable pages:</strong> the website contains a lot of pages that aren't indexable.</li>
<li>
<strong>Bad internal link structure:</strong> if your internal link structure isn't set up correctly, search engines may not pay enough attention to some of your pages.</li>
</ol>

<h3 id="accessible-urls-with-parameters">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#accessible-urls-with-parameters" tabindex="-1"></a>Accessible URLs with parameters</h3>
<p>In most cases, URLs with parameters shouldn't be accessible for search engines, because they can generate a virtually infinite quantity of URLs.We've written extensively about this type of issue in our article about <a href="/academy/crawler-traps/">crawler traps</a>.</p>
<p>URLs with parameters are commonly used when implementing <a href="/academy/ecommerce-seo-checklist/#product-filters">product filters on eCommerce sites</a>. It's fine to use them,; just make sure they aren't accessible to search engines.</p>
<p>How can you make them inaccessible to search engine?</p>
<ol>
<li>Use your robots.txt file to instruct search engines not to access such URLs. If this isn't an option for some reason, use the URL parameter handling settings in Google Search Console and Bing Webmaster Tools to instruct Google and Bing regarding which pages not to crawl.</li>
<li>Add the <a href="/academy/nofollow/">nofollow attribute value</a> to links on filter links. Please note that as of March 2020, Google may choose to ignore the nofollow. Therefore step 1 is even more important.</li>
</ol>
<h3 id="duplicate-content">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#duplicate-content" tabindex="-1"></a>Duplicate content</h3>
<p>You don't want search engine to spend their time on <a href="/academy/duplicate-content/">duplicate content</a> pages, so it's important to prevent, or at the very least minimize, the duplicate content in your site.</p>
<p>How do you do this? By…</p>
<ol>
<li>Setting up website redirects for all domain variants (<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">HTTP</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">HTTPS</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">non-WWW</code>, and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">WWW</code>).</li>
<li>Making internal search result pages inaccessible to search engines using your robots.txt. Here's <a href="/academy/robotstxt/#how-can-i-prevent-search-engines-from-indexing-search-result-pages-on-my-wordpress-website">an example robots.txt for a WordPress website</a>.</li>
<li>Disabling dedicated pages for images (for example: the infamous image attachment pages in WordPress).</li>
<li>Being careful around your use of taxonomies such as categories and tags.</li>
</ol>
<p>Check out some more <a href="/academy/duplicate-content/#duplicate-content-due-to-technical-reasons">technical reasons for duplicate content and how to fix them</a>.</p>
<h3 id="low-quality-content">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#low-quality-content" tabindex="-1"></a>Low-quality content</h3>
<p>Pages with very little content aren't interesting to search engines. Keep them to a minimum, or avoid them completely if possible. One example of low-quality content is a FAQ section with links to show the questions and answers, where each question and answer is served over a separate URL.</p>
<h3 id="broken-redirected-links">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#broken-redirected-links" tabindex="-1"></a>Broken and redirecting links</h3>
<p>Broken links and long <a href="/academy/redirects/faq/redirect-chains/">chains of redirects</a> are dead ends for search engines. Similar to browsers, Google seems to follow a maximum of five chained redirects in one crawl (they may resume crawling it later). It's unclear how well other search engines deal with subsequent redirects, but we strongly advise that you avoid chained redirects entirely and keep the usage of redirects to a minimum.</p>
<p>It's clear that by fixing broken links and redirecting links, you can quickly recover wasted crawl budget. Besides recovering crawl budget, you're also significantly improving a visitor's user experience. Redirects, and chains of redirects in particular, cause longer page load time and thereby hurt the user experience.</p>
<p>To make finding broken and redirecting links easy, we've dedicated special Issues to this within ContentKing.</p>
<p>Go to <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Issues</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Links</code> to find out if you are wasting crawl budgets because of faulty links. Update each link so that it link to an indexable page, or remove the link if it's no longer needed.</p>

<h3 id="incorrect-urls-xml-sitemap">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#incorrect-urls-xml-sitemap" tabindex="-1"></a>Incorrect URLs in XML sitemaps</h3>
<p>All URLs included in <a href="/academy/xml-sitemap/">XML sitemaps</a> should be for indexable pages. Especially with large websites, search engines heavily rely on XML sitemaps to find all your pages. If your XML sitemaps are cluttered with pages that, for instance, don't exist anymore or are redirecting, you're wasting crawl budget. Regularly check your XML sitemap for non-indexable URLs that don't belong in there. Check for the opposite as well: look for pages that are incorrectly excluded from the XML sitemap. The XML sitemap is a great way to help search engines spend crawl budget wisely.</p>

<h3 id="pages-high-load-timeout">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#pages-high-load-timeout" tabindex="-1"></a>Pages with high load times / timeouts</h3>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-7" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/ContentKing-Crawl-Budget-EN-High-load-time@1x.png"/><img alt="Crawl budget: pages with high load times or timeouts" class="image" height="200" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/ContentKing-Crawl-Budget-EN-High-load-time@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/ContentKing-Crawl-Budget-EN-High-load-time@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2018/07/ContentKing-Crawl-Budget-EN-High-load-time@2x.png 2x" title="Crawl budget: pages with high load times or timeouts" width="600"/></picture></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-7">Pages with high load times / timeouts hurt the crawl process</figcaption></figure>
<p>When pages have high load times or they time out, search engines can visit fewer pages within their allotted crawl budget for your website. Besides that downside, high page load times and timeouts significantly hurt your visitor's user experience, resulting in a lower conversion rate.</p>
<p>Page load times above two seconds are an issue. Ideally, your page will loadd in under one second. Regularly check your page load times with tools such as <a href="https://tools.pingdom.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Pingdom</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>, <a href="http://www.webpagetest.org/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">WebPagetest</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> or <a href="https://gtmetrix.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">GTmetrix</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>.</p>
<p>Google reports on page load time in both Google Analytics (under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Behavior</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Site Speed</code>) and Google Search Console under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl Stats</code>.</p>
<p>Google Search Console and Bing Webmaster Tools both report on page timeouts. In Google Search Console, this can be found under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl Errors</code>, and in Bing Webmaster Tools, it's under <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Reports &amp; Data</code> &gt; <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Crawl Information</code>.</p>
<p>Check regularly to see if your pages are loading fast enough, and take action immediately if they aren't. Fast-loading pages are vital to your online success.</p>

<h3 id="high-amount-non-indexable-pages">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#high-amount-non-indexable-pages" tabindex="-1"></a>High numbers of non-indexable pages</h3>
<p>If your website contains a high number of non-indexable pages that are accessible to search engines, you're basically keeping search engines busy sifting through irrelevant pages.</p>
<p>We consider the following types to be non-indexable pages:</p>
<ul>
<li>Redirects (3xx)</li>
<li>Pages that can't be found (4xx)</li>
<li>Pages with server errors (5xx)</li>
<li>Pages that are not indexable (pages that contain the robots noindex directive or canonical URL)</li>
</ul>
<p>In order to find out if you have a high number of non-indexable pages, look up the total number of pages that crawlers have found within your website and how they break down. You can easily do this using ContentKing:</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-8" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 431px;"><img alt="URL break-down in ContentKing" class="image" height="264" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/url-type-break-down.png" title="URL break-down in ContentKing" width="431"/></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-8">URL break-down in ContentKing</figcaption></figure>
<p>In this example, there are 63,137 URLs found, of which only 20,528 are pages.</p>
<figure aria-labelledby="image-caption-9" class="captioned-image"><div class="media-container" style="max-width: 424px;"><img alt="Page indexability break-down in ContentKing" class="image" height="255" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/page-indexability-break-down.png" title="Page indexability break-down in ContentKing" width="424"/></div>
<figcaption class="captioned-image__caption" id="image-caption-9">Page indexability break-down in ContentKing</figcaption></figure>
<p>And out of these pages, only 4,663 are indexable for search engines. Only 7.4% of the URLs found by ContentKing can be indexed by search engines. That's not a good ratio, and this website definitely needs to work on that by cleaning up all references to them that are unnecessary, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>The XML sitemap (see previous section)</li>
<li>Links</li>
<li>Canonical URLs</li>
<li>Hreflang references</li>
<li>Pagination references (link rel prev/next)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="internal-link-structure">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#internal-link-structure" tabindex="-1"></a>Bad internal link structure</h3>
<p>How pages within your website link to one another plays a big role in crawl budget optimization. We call this the <em>internal link structure</em> of your website. Backlinks aside, pages that have few internal links get much less attention from search engines than pages that are linked to by a lot of pages.</p>
<p>Avoid a very hierarchical link structure, with pages in the middle having few links. In many cases these pages will not be frequently crawled. It's even worse for pages at the bottom of the hierarchy: because of their limited amount of links, they may very well be neglected by search engines.</p>
<p>Make sure that your most important pages have plenty of internal links. Pages that have recently been crawled typically rank better in search engines. Keep this in mind, and adjust your internal link structure for this.</p>
<p>For example, if you have a blog article dating from 2011 that drives a lot of organic traffic, make sure to keep linking to it from other content. Because you've produced many other blog articles over the years, that article from 2011 is automatically being pushed down in your website's internal link structure.</p>

<h2 id="how-to-increase-crawl-budget">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-to-increase-crawl-budget" tabindex="-1"></a>How do you increase your website's crawl budget?</h2>
<p>During <a href="https://www.stonetemple.com/matt-cutts-interviewed-by-eric-enge-2/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">an interview</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> between Eric Enge and Google's former head of the webspam team Matt Cutts, the relation between <a href="/academy/authority/">authority</a> and crawl budget was brought up:</p>

Since PageRank is a misunderstood and confusing term, let's call it <a href="/academy/authority/#page-authority">page authority</a>. The take-away here is that Matt Cutts basically says: <strong>there's a pretty strong relation between page authority and crawl budget</strong>.</p>
<p>So, in order to increase your website's crawl budget, you need to increase the authority of your website. A big part of this is done by earning more links from external websites. More information about this can be found in <a href="/academy/ecommerce-link-building/">our link building guide</a>.</p>

<h2 id="faq">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#faq" tabindex="-1"></a>Frequently asked questions about crawl budget</h2>
<ol>
<li><a href="/academy/crawl-budget/#what-is-crawl-budget">🧾 What is crawl budget?</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/crawl-budget/#how-to-increase-your-crawl-budget">🤔 How do I increase my crawl budget?</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/crawl-budget/#what-limits-crawl-budget">🎛️ What can limit my crawl budget?</a></li>
<li><a href="/academy/crawl-budget/#should-i-use-canonical-meta-tag">🤖 Should I be using canonical URL and meta robots at all?</a></li>
</ol>
<h3 id="what-is-crawl-budget">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-is-crawl-budget" tabindex="-1"></a>1. 🧾 What is crawl budget?</h3>
<p>Crawl budget is the number of pages search engines will crawl on a website within a certain timeframe.</p>
<h3 id="how-to-increase-your-crawl-budget">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-to-increase-your-crawl-budget" tabindex="-1"></a>2. 🤔 How do I increase my crawl budget?</h3>
<p>Google has indicated there’s a strong relation between page authority and crawl budget. The more authority a page has, the more crawl budget it has. Simply put, to increase your crawl budget, build your page’s authority.</p>
<h3 id="what-limits-crawl-budget">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-limits-crawl-budget" tabindex="-1"></a>3. 🤷 What can limit my crawl budget?</h3>
<p>Crawl limit, also known as crawl host load, is based on many factors, such as the website’s condition and hosting abilities. Search engine crawlers are set to prevent overloading a web server. If your website returns server errors, or if the requested URLs time out often, the crawl budget will be more limited. Similarly, if your website runs on a shared hosting platform, the crawl limit will be higher as you have to share your crawl budget with other websites running on the hosting.</p>
<h3 id="should-i-use-canonical-meta-tag">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#should-i-use-canonical-meta-tag" tabindex="-1"></a>4. 🤖 Should I be using canonical URL and meta robots at all?</h3>
<p>Yes, and it's important to understand the differences between indexing issues and crawl issues.</p>
<p>The <a href="/academy/canonical/">canonical URL</a> and meta robots tags send a clear signal to search engines what page they should show in their index, but it does not prevent them from crawling those other pages.</p>
<p>You can use the robots.txt file and the nofollow link relation for dealing with crawl issues.</p>
