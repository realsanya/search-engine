<h2 id="what-are">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-are" tabindex="-1"></a>What are Twitter Cards?</h2>
<p>Twitter Card markup—Twitter Cards for short—is structured markup that you can add to your HTML documents to explain your content's context and gain control over the snippets shown when your URLs are shared on Twitter.</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 1200px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/ContentKing-Twitter-Cards-EN-Twitter-Card-2@1x.png"/><img alt=" " class="image" height="638" src="/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/ContentKing-Twitter-Cards-EN-Twitter-Card-2@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/ContentKing-Twitter-Cards-EN-Twitter-Card-2@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2018/07/ContentKing-Twitter-Cards-EN-Twitter-Card-2@2x.png 2x" title=" " width="1200"/></picture></div>
<p>Here's an example of CNN using Twitter Cards to take control of how their blog post looks on Twitter:</p>
<div class="media-container" style="max-width: 600px;"><picture><source media="(max-width: 600px)" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/ContentKing-Twitter-Cards-example-tweet@1x.png"/><img alt="Twitter Card example tweet" class="image" height="594" loading="lazy" src="/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/ContentKing-Twitter-Cards-example-tweet@1x.png" srcset="/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/ContentKing-Twitter-Cards-example-tweet@1x.png, /wp-content/uploads/2018/09/ContentKing-Twitter-Cards-example-tweet@2x.png 2x" title="Twitter Card example tweet" width="600"/></picture></div>
<h2 id="why-care">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#why-care" tabindex="-1"></a>Why you should care about Twitter Cards</h2>
<p>Pages optimized with Twitter Cards get more clicks, likes, and retweets.</p>
<p>Why?</p>
<p>Because having an appealing image catches attention. And having a great title and description entices people to click on your snippet.</p>
<p>Doesn't Twitter generate these automatically?</p>
<p>They do—to some extent. But 99% of the time, Twitter picks a less-than-ideal image and shows a truncated title and description.</p>
<p>Similar to Open Graph markup for platforms like Facebook and LinkedIn, Twitter Cards let you gain control over how your URLs' snippets are shown on Twitter. In SEO, we're obsessed with fine-tuning our titles and meta descriptions to get the highest possible click-through rate, but often people forget when it comes to social media. That's a pity, as the battle for attention on social media is just as fierce (if not fiercer).</p>

<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/geoffrey-colon@1x.avif, /media/people/geoffrey-colon@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/geoffrey-colon@1x.webp, /media/people/geoffrey-colon@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Geoffrey Colon" class="image" height="100" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/geoffrey-colon@1x.jpg" srcset="/media/people/geoffrey-colon@1x.jpg, /media/people/geoffrey-colon@2x.jpg 2x" title="Geoffrey Colon" width="100"/></picture></span></span></div>

In addition to your 140-character message, you can include images, videos, audio, and download links. This makes your tweet much more dynamic and highly clickable.</p>
<p>Users never have to leave their Twitter newsfeed to:</p>
<ul>
<li>View an image</li>
<li>Watch a video</li>
<li>Visit a landing page</li>
<li>Download an app</li>
</ul>
<p>This provides a frictionless user experience while allowing you to track metrics on those actions.</p>

<h3 id="do-twitter-cards-increase-my-rankings">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#do-twitter-cards-increase-my-rankings" tabindex="-1"></a>Do Twitter Cards increase my rankings?</h3>
<p>No, Twitter Cards do not directly increase your rankings. We do think, though, that Twitter Cards help search engines determine the context of your content, similarly to <a href="/academy/open-graph/">Open Graph</a> and  <a href="/academy/schema/">Schema.org</a>. And besides, marking up your content with Twitter Cards improves your visibility on Twitter, leading to more shares and links. So indirectly, your SEO performance does benefit from Twitter Cards.</p>
<div aria-label="Embedded quote from our customer" class="quote-box typo-block">
<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--is-flipped avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/arnout-hellemans@1x.avif, /media/people/arnout-hellemans@2x.avif 2x" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/arnout-hellemans@1x.webp, /media/people/arnout-hellemans@2x.webp 2x" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Arnout Hellemans" class="image" height="100" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/arnout-hellemans@1x.jpg" srcset="/media/people/arnout-hellemans@1x.jpg, /media/people/arnout-hellemans@2x.jpg 2x" title="Arnout Hellemans" width="100"/></picture></span></span></div>
<div class="quote-box__content typo">

<p>Any implementation to get your content properly displayed on platforms where it may go viral is one you should make use of. If you don't do this, you might not be able to stand out in the steady stream of content that is being shared. This is especially important for organic shares. Twitter Cards enable you convey a custom message to the Twitter audience, so you need to grab that opportunity. Apart from that, Google is still connected with the Twitter firehose. As such, they'll pick up these Twitter Cards way easier.</p>

<h2 id="what-platforms">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#what-platforms" tabindex="-1"></a>What platforms support Twitter Cards</h2>
<p>The obvious one is Twitter, as they developed the Twitter Cards standard, but Twitter Cards are also supported by Slack.</p>
<p>After running some tests we found that Facebook, LinkedIn, and Google Plus do not base their snippets on Twitter Card markup if no Open Graph markup is present. Instead, they base their snippets on the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">title</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">meta description</code> and a randomly picked image.</p>
<p>So if you want to optimize your visibility on Facebook, LinkedIn, and Google Plus, be sure to  <a href="/academy/open-graph/">implement Open Graph markup</a>.</p>
<p>Most social media tools support displaying Twitter Cards and Open Graph previews too, so tweak them until they look right!</p>
<h2 id="how-to-implement">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#how-to-implement" tabindex="-1"></a>How to implement Twitter Cards</h2>
<p>As we've mentioned above, you implement Twitter Cards by adding Twitter Card markup to your HTML documents—in the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;head&gt;</code> section of your pages. You can do this manually, but if your website's driven by a CMS, there are likely plugins or other features available for this job. And if not, go talk to your developers. It's usually not hard to build functionality for this.</p>
<h2 id="card-types">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#card-types" tabindex="-1"></a>The different types of Twitter Cards</h2>
<p>There are four Twitter Card types you can use:</p>
<ol>
<li>Summary Card</li>
<li>Summary Card with Large Image</li>
<li>App Card</li>
<li>Player Card</li>
</ol>
<p>Below we'll discuss the first two in detail, and what properties to define in a Twitter Card.</p>
<div aria-label="Embedded quote from our customer" class="quote-box typo-block">
<div aria-hidden="true" class="quote-box__avatar"><span class="avatar avatar--small-size"><span class="avatar__img"><picture><source srcset="/media/people/bonnie-porter@1x.avif" type="image/avif"/><source srcset="/media/people/bonnie-porter@1x.webp" type="image/webp"/><img alt="Bonnie Porter" class="image" height="100" loading="lazy" src="/media/people/bonnie-porter@1x.jpg" title="Bonnie Porter" width="100"/></picture></span></span></div>
<div class="quote-box__content typo">
<blockquote class="quote-box__blockquote">
<p>Using different kinds of Twitter Cards for different kinds of content is very helpful! Summary Cards are great for blog posts, Large image cards are great for brands with rich imagery, Player cards are essential for music, games, and multimedia, and App cards are a great way to showcase your app. You can't go wrong with a great image, so try out large image cards to see how they work for you.</p>
<p>Pinning Twitter cards to the top of your timeline can be super helpful for marketers. We've found that pinning our lead generation card to the top of our Twitter stream is particularly successful.</p>
</blockquote>
<div aria-label="The author of this quote" class="quote-box__info"><div class="user-info">
<span class="user-info__name">Bonnie Porter</span><span class="user-info__additional-info">, <a href="https://buffer.com" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Buffer</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a></span>

<h3 id="summary-card">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#summary-card" tabindex="-1"></a>Twitter Card type: Summary Card</h3>
<p>Twitter requires the two properties below for a valid Twitter Card implementation.</p>
<ul>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:card</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:title</code></li>
</ul>
<p>But we highly recommend also including the three properties below to provide more context about the content:</p>
<ul>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:site</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:description</code></li>
<li><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:image</code></li>
</ul>

<h2 id="card-properties">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#card-properties" tabindex="-1"></a>Twitter Card properties in detail</h2>
<h3 id="card-type">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#card-type" tabindex="-1"></a>twitter:card</h3>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:card</code> property sets which of the four Twitter Card types you are using:</p>
<ol>
<li>Summary Card: text with a square image.</li>
<li>Summary Card with Large Image: text with a large, rectangular image.</li>
<li>App Card: for promoting apps.</li>
<li>Player Card: for showing videos. But to be able to use it, you need to be whitelisted.</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>Example code snippet</strong></p>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta name="twitter:card" content="summary_large_image" /&gt;</code></p>
<p><strong>Requirements</strong></p>
<p>You need to choose one of the four Twitter Card types.</p>
<p><strong>What happens if you don't define it</strong></p>
<p>If you don't define <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:card</code>, Twitter won't know what Twitter Card type you want your content to be shown as, and so they will treat your URL as if it doesn't have any Twitter Card markup.</p>
<h3 id="site">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#site" tabindex="-1"></a>twitter:site</h3>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:site</code> property describes the site's Twitter account or website.</p>
<p><strong>Example code snippet</strong></p>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta name="twitter:site" content="@contentking" /&gt;</code></p>
<p><strong>Requirements</strong></p>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:site</code> property should reference a working Twitter account of a website. Either define the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:site</code> property, or define the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:site:id</code> property, which lets you define your site's Twitter ID. A Twitter ID is a unique numeric value that identifies your Twitter account. You can look up your Twitter ID here: <a href="http://mytwitterid.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">http://mytwitterid.com/</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>. So in our case we'd use this example snippet:</p>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta name="twitter:site:id" content="15467325" /&gt;</code></p>
<p><strong>What happens if you don't define it</strong></p>
<p>The connection between your content and the associated Twitter account won't be clear. While this doesn't have a direct impact, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:site</code> is still a recommended property to define.</p>
<h3 id="creator">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#creator" tabindex="-1"></a>twitter:creator</h3>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:creator</code> property describes the author's Twitter account.</p>
<p><strong>Example code snippet</strong></p>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta name="twitter:creator" content="@stevenvvessum" /&gt;</code></p>
<p><strong>Requirements</strong></p>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:creator</code> should reference a working Twitter account. Either define <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:creator</code>, or define <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:creator:id</code>.</p>
<p>You can look up your Twitter ID here: <a href="http://mytwitterid.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">http://mytwitterid.com/</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a>. In Steven's case, he would use this example snippet:</p>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta name="twitter:siteid" content="8728882" /&gt;</code></p>
<p><strong>What happens if you don't define it</strong></p>
<p>You don't make the connection between your content and the author's Twitter account clear. This doesn't have a direct impact.</p>
<h3 id="title">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#title" tabindex="-1"></a>twitter:title</h3>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:title</code> property defines the Twitter Card title, similar to a page's title attribute.</p>
<p><strong>Example code snippet</strong></p>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta name="twitter:title" content="Hreflang: ranking the right pages for multi-language sites" /&gt;</code></p>
<p><strong>Requirements</strong></p>
<p>When optimizing your snippet for Twitter, keep in mind that the title will not fit on one line if it's longer than 55 characters. Note that Twitter recommends a maximum of 70 characters—but in our tests, they never show titles that long.</p>
<p><strong>What happens if you don't define it</strong></p>
<p>Twitter will use your <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">og:title</code> first, and then the page title, as a fallback.</p>
<h3 id="description">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#description" tabindex="-1"></a>twitter:description</h3>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:description</code> property defines the Twitter Card description. It's a short description of what the page is about.</p>
<p><strong>Example code snippet</strong></p>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta name="twitter:description" content="Learn how to implement hreflang to get search engines to rank the right pages for your multi-language site. Check it out now!" /&gt;</code></p>
<p><strong>Requirements</strong></p>
<p>When optimizing your snippet for Twitter, keep in mind that the description will not fit on two lines if it's longer than 125 characters. Note that Twitter recommends a maximum of 200 characters—but in our tests, they never show descriptions that long.</p>
<p><strong>What happens if you don't define it</strong></p>
<p>Twitter will use your <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">og:description</code> first, and then your description, as a fallback.</p>

<h3 id="image">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#image" tabindex="-1"></a>twitter:image</h3>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:image</code> property defines the image to be shown in your snippet. Make sure to select these images carefully. Size matters, and so do quality and dimensions.</p>
<p><strong>Example code snippet:</strong></p>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta name="twitter:image" content="https://www.contentkingapp.com/media/social-cards/og-en-contentking-hreflang@2x.png" /&gt;</code></p>
<p><strong>Requirements</strong></p>
<p>When using <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:image</code> with the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Summary Card Twitter Card</code> type, keep the following requirements in mind:</p>
<ul>
<li>Aspect ratio: 1:1</li>
<li>Minimum size: 144 x 144 pixels</li>
<li>Maximum size: 4096 x 4096 pixels</li>
<li>Maximum file size: 5 MB</li>
<li>Supported formats: JPG, PNG, WEBP, and GIF (for animated GIFs, only the first frame will be used). Note that SVG <strong>is not supported</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<p>When using <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:image</code> with the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Summary Card with Large Image</code> Twitter Card type, keep the following requirements in mind:</p>
<ul>
<li>Aspect ratio: 2:1</li>
<li>Minimum size: 300 x 157 pixels</li>
<li>Maximum size: 4096 x 4096 pixels</li>
<li>Maximum file size: 5 MB</li>
<li>Supported formats: JPG, PNG, WEBP, and GIF (for animated GIFs, only the first frame will be used). Note that SVG <strong>is not supported</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>What happens if you don't define it</strong></p>
<p>Twitter will first check if you've defined an <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">og:image</code>, and otherwise it just grabs one of the images on your page.</p>
<h3 id="image-alt">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#image-alt" tabindex="-1"></a>twitter:image:alt</h3>
<p>The <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:image:alt</code> lets you describe the twitter:image in text for visually impaired users.</p>
<p><strong>Example code snippet:</strong></p>
<p><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">&lt;meta name="twitter:image:alt" content="Hreflang image that symbolizes different languages" /&gt;</code></p>
<p><strong>Requirements</strong></p>
<p>Stick to a maximum length of 420 characters.</p>
<p><strong>What happens if you don't define it</strong></p>
<p>Nothing, as this is an optional property. However, it's recommended that you define it to help the visually impaired understand your content.</p>

<h2 id="best-practices">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#best-practices" tabindex="-1"></a>Best practices for Twitter Cards</h2>
<p>Keep in mind the following best practices when dealing with Twitter Cards:</p>
<ol>
<li>Make sure that you define at least all the required Twitter Card properties—but ideally the recommended ones as well.</li>
<li>Keep your <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:title</code> under 55 characters.</li>
<li>Keep your <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:description</code> under 125 chars.</li>
<li>For <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:image</code>, use high-quality images with dimensions of at least 144 x 144 pixels for <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Summary Card</code> and a minimum of 300 x 157 pixels for <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Summary Card with Large Image</code>, and maximum dimension of 4096 x 4096 pixels, while keeping the file size under 5 MB. The supported formats are: JPG, PNG, WEBP, and GIF..</li>
<li>When you're preparing the Twitter Card markup for a page, <strong>always</strong> run it through <a href="https://cards-dev.twitter.com/validator" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="extlink">Twitter's Card validator</span><span class="show-for-sr"> (opens in a new tab)</span></a> to preview your snippet, and to make sure all data is filled in. Tweak and tune it until you get it right. An added benefit is that you'll pre-cache the snippet too, so your snippet will already be shown correctly the first time it's shared.</li>
<li>Be sure not to block Twitterbot in your <a href="/academy/robotstxt/">robots.txt file</a>, as they need to be able to visit your pages to retrieve the Twitter Card markup in order to show it. That includes the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:image</code> you referenced, so be careful with your disallows.</li>
<li>If you've made any mistakes, you've fixed them, and you want Twitter to generate a new snippet: go to the Card Validator and retrieve the Twitter Card data again. This refreshes the cache. However, it doesn't automatically refresh the image as well—for that you'll need to define a new <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:image</code> URL and retrieve the Twitter Card data again through the Card validator. We recommend appending a query string after the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">twitter:image</code> so that you can easily get your snippet's image refreshes by changing the query string value.</li>
</ol>

<h3 id="twitter-card-image-size">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#twitter-card-image-size" tabindex="-1"></a>1. What Twitter Card image size should I choose?</h3>
<p>As of 2018, the best image size for your Twitter Cards is:</p>
<ul>
<li>Summary Card: 1024 x 1024 (as an aspect ratio of 1:1 is needed)</li>
<li>Summary Card with Large Image: 1024 x 512 (as an aspect ratio of 2:1 is needed)</li>
</ul>
<p>This ensures your Twitter Card images won't be blurry.</p>
<p>Furthermore, make sure to stick to…</p>
<ul>
<li>The maximum file size: 5 MB</li>
<li>The supported formats: JPG, PNG, WEBP, and GIF (for an animated GIF, only the first frame will be used). Note that SVG is not supported.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="card-image-not-showing">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#card-image-not-showing" tabindex="-1"></a>2. My Twitter Card image is not showing. What's wrong?</h3>
<p>If your Twitter Card image isn't showing, there are a couple of things that may be going wrong:</p>
<ol>
<li>You didn't define the correct image URL (including the protocol, HTTP or HTTPS).</li>
<li>Your Twitter Card image doesn't have the right dimensions, and/or exceeds the maximum file size of 5 MB.</li>
<li>Your Twitter Card image isn't in a supported format. Examples of unsupported formats: BMP and SVG.</li>
<li>You've blocked <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Twitterbot</code> from accessing your pages. Check your robots.txt file to see if <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Twitterbot</code> is allowed access to your pages. Keep in mind that you're also targeting Twitterbot when you define a block of disallows for: <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">User-agent: *.</code>
</li>
</ol>
<h3 id="meta-tags-not-found">
<a aria-hidden="true" class="anchor-link" href="#meta-tags-not-found" tabindex="-1"></a>3. The validator says no meta tags were found. What's wrong?</h3>
<p>There's a couple of reasons why this can happen, namely:</p>
<ol>
<li>Your page isn't returning a <a href="/academy/http-status-codes/#200">HTTP status 200</a>. Make sure it does.</li>
<li>Your page isn't returning <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Content-type:text/html</code> in the header. Make sure it does.</li>
<li>Twitter Card markup is being added after loading using Javascript. <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Twitterbot</code> does not execute Javascript. The <a href="/academy/meta-tags/">meta tags</a> must be static. Implement the Twitter Card markup in the &lt;head&gt; section as static meta tags.</li>
</ol>
